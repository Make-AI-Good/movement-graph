# Anti-Eviction Mapping Project

The Anti-Eviction Mapping Project (AEMP) is a San Francisco Bay Area–based data-visualization, critical-cartography, and multimedia-storytelling collective [documenting dispossession and resistance upon gentrifying landscapes](https://antievictionmap.com/about-the-aemp). [Founded in 2013](https://arts.ca.gov/grantee/the-anti-eviction-mapping-project/) by Erin McElroy, Manon Vergerio, and Sam Raby at the height of San Francisco's tech-boom displacement wave, AEMP operates as [an all-volunteer collective with fiscal sponsorship through Independent Arts & Media](https://antievictionmap.com/about-the-aemp) and has since expanded to chapters in Los Angeles and New York City. Its primary contribution to the make-AI-good corpus is the Landlord Tech Watch — a sustained research programme tracking the deployment of property technology (proptech) against tenants: facial-recognition door systems, biometric building access, algorithmic rent-setting software, and AI-driven tenant-screening services.

## Founding and structure

AEMP emerged from San Francisco's second tech boom, a period in which [Ellis Act no-fault evictions, Airbnb-driven short-term-rental conversion, and speculative acquisition by institutional landlords](https://notesfrombelow.org/article/anti-eviction-mapping-project) rapidly displaced long-term low-income tenants, disproportionately Black and Latino households. [McElroy holds a Feminist Studies doctorate from UC Santa Cruz; Vergerio came from tenant organising in Brooklyn and San Francisco; Raby brought cartography and data-science expertise.](https://notesfrombelow.org/article/anti-eviction-mapping-project) The founding coalition [partnered with the San Francisco Tenants Union and the direct-action collective Eviction Free San Francisco](https://notesfrombelow.org/article/anti-eviction-mapping-project), grounding the data work inside active organising campaigns from the outset.

AEMP operates [as an all-volunteer, non-hierarchical collective through Independent Arts & Media fiscal sponsorship](https://antievictionmap.com/about-the-aemp) rather than as a free-standing nonprofit — structurally analogous to [Detroit Community Technology Project](lg-detroit-community-technology-project.md)'s Allied Media Projects arrangement — drawing on institutional back-office capacity while preserving collective decision-making. [A New York City chapter was established in August 2017](https://notesfrombelow.org/article/anti-eviction-mapping-project); a Los Angeles chapter followed.

## Mapping, oral history, and EvictorBook

AEMP's [four output modes](https://antievictionmap.com/about-the-aemp) — digital mapping and software tools, narrative multimedia and oral history, research reports, and murals and public events — each orient housing-displacement data toward on-the-ground organising. The Narratives of Displacement and Resistance oral-history initiative [documents displaced tenants' experiences in their own voices](https://notesfrombelow.org/article/anti-eviction-mapping-project), attaching life histories to eviction-address point data so the maps carry human texture alongside statistical pattern. The more recent *(Dis)location: Black Exodus* project, [supported by a 2022–23 California Arts Council grant](https://arts.ca.gov/grantee/the-anti-eviction-mapping-project/), extends this methodology to the documented displacement of Black San Franciscans, combining oral histories, creative cartography, archival materials, and art.

EvictorBook is [an interactive database linking landlord names and building addresses to eviction histories](https://logicmag.io/commons/evictor-structures-erin-mcelroy-and-azad-amir-ghassemi-on-fighting/), consolidating publicly available but deliberately fragmented assessor, property-ownership, and unlawful-detainer records. Its analytical move is the exposure of [corporate-landlord networks that obscure ownership through multiple limited-liability-company shell structures](https://logicmag.io/commons/evictor-structures-erin-mcelroy-and-azad-amir-ghassemi-on-fighting/): a single investor group operating dozens of properties becomes legible as a network at the database level. The NYC chapter's [Worst Evictors project](https://notesfrombelow.org/article/anti-eviction-mapping-project) builds on the same approach using Right to Counsel coalition and JustFix.nyc data.

## Landlord tech and AI-in-housing work

AEMP's most direct contribution to the make-AI-good movement is its sustained documentation of property technology — AI and algorithmic systems embedded in the landlord-tenant relationship. The collective defines landlord tech as technology that [increases the power of landlords while disempowering tenants and those seeking shelter](https://www.antievictionlab.org/landlord-tech-watch): facial-recognition entry systems tracking tenant movement and visitors; predictive algorithms generating automated eviction notices; algorithmic rent-setting software coordinating pricing across large institutional portfolios; and tenant-screening data services that bar applicants with past eviction records.

The [Landlord Tech Watch](https://www.antievictionlab.org/landlord-tech-watch), [directed by McElroy at the University of Washington through the Anti-Eviction Lab](https://www.antievictionlab.org/landlord-tech-watch) with AEMP as a partner organisation, combines a crowdsourced tenant-reporting map with academic research and community workshops. In 2022, AEMP and the AI Now Institute co-produced [*Landlord Technologies of Gentrification: Facial Recognition and Building Access Technologies in New York City Homes*](https://www.antievictionlab.org/ny-report) — documenting how biometric "digital doorman" facial-recognition systems were installed in majority-tenant-of-colour buildings under a safety framing while [disproportionately misidentifying tenants of colour](https://www.antievictionlab.org/ny-report) and functioning as a gentrification accelerant. The [2023 *San Francisco Landlord Tech Report*](https://antievictionmap.com/reports-new) extended the methodology to the Bay Area market.

## Place in the movement

AEMP is the corpus's first entity anchoring the landlord-tech accountability strand of the make-AI-good movement — the organised tenant-side response to AI deployment inside residential housing. Where [Oakland Privacy](lg-oakland-privacy.md) works the municipal-ordinance path and the [Stop LAPD Spying Coalition](lg-stop-lapd-spying-coalition.md) works the policing-and-surveillance path, AEMP operates in the housing-and-property lane, where much of the day-to-day AI deployment affecting low-income urban communities occurs but where dedicated civil-society infrastructure is comparatively thin. Its counter-cartography methodology — and the philosophy of tools produced *with* rather than *about* communities — constitutes a distinct approach among corpus entities doing AI-adjacent data and mapping work.
