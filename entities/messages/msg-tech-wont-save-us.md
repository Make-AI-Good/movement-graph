# "Tech won't save us"

**"Tech won't save us"** is the counter-solutionist framing of the make-AI-good and digital-rights movement: the claim that social, political, and structural problems cannot be resolved by better-designed technology, and that the ideology asserting otherwise — "solutionism" — is itself a mechanism by which those problems are perpetuated and the political agency that might actually address them is displaced. The framing targets not any specific technology or company but the epistemic framework through which Silicon Valley has presented its expansion as public good: the claim that innovation, applied well, is the primary lever for human welfare. In the AI context, "tech won't save us" is the counter-register to AI boosterism's promise that large language models, predictive systems, and AI-enabled productivity will reduce poverty, solve climate change, and democratise access — a claim the framing names as the latest expression of a pattern that has repeatedly reproduced inequity while appearing to remedy it.

## Origin: solutionism as named critique

The intellectual crystallisation of the framing runs through Evgeny Morozov's 2013 [*To Save Everything, Click Here: The Folly of Technological Solutionism*](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/lsereviewofbooks/2013/05/01/book-review-to-save-everything-click-here-the-folly-of-technological-solutionism/) — the book that gave the counter-argument its named concept. Morozov's "solutionism" described a doctrine, not a preference: the belief that any social problem — political disengagement, obesity, dishonesty in public life, criminal recidivism — can be properly addressed only once it is reconceived as an engineering problem amenable to technological redesign. The critique was not that technology is useless but that the solutionist frame performs an epistemic operation before any particular technology is deployed: it converts problems whose proper resolution requires deliberation, politics, and structural change into problems whose proper resolution is platform architecture and product development. Once that conversion is performed, technological redesign appears as a solution, and the political work it displaces appears as unnecessary.

The phrase "tech won't save us" as movement vocabulary emerged through Paris Marx's podcast of the same name, [launched in April 2020](https://radicalurbanist.substack.com/p/introducing-tech-wont-save-us) during the first COVID-19 lockdown. Marx described the project as a counter to the "nauseating tech utopia idealism that usually surrounds Silicon Valley," grounded in the Luddite tradition's argument that technological development under capitalist imperatives is structured to control workers and commodify life rather than serve collective wellbeing. The [podcast's thesis](https://www.techwontsave.us/about) — that separating technology from politics has consequences for everyone, especially the most vulnerable — gave Morozov's academic concept a popular political register. Marx's 2022 book *Road to Nowhere: What Silicon Valley Gets Wrong about the Future of Transportation* (Verso) applied the framing to a specific domain: showing that Silicon Valley's transportation proposals — ridesharing, autonomous vehicles, the Hyperloop — are "constrained by the elite perspectives of the people dreaming them up" and substitute technological fantasy for the public transit, bike infrastructure, and urban planning that would actually improve mobility for poor and marginalized people.

## Academic and organizing crystallisation: the "gospel" formulation

[Ruha Benjamin](../persons/person-ruha-benjamin.md)'s work connected the structural tech critique to abolitionist and anti-racist theory. [*Race After Technology: Abolitionist Tools for the New Jim Code*](../publications/pub-race-after-technology.md) (2019) named the central mechanism: that tech companies deploy the language of neutrality, objectivity, and innovation to market systems that reproduce — and often automate and accelerate — the structural inequities of previous eras. The book's argument was not only that algorithmic systems fail communities of colour but that the promise of technological solution is itself the problem: each new platform or AI system is presented as overcoming the failures of the last, while the structural conditions generating those failures remain unaddressed.

Benjamin and Greta Byrum made the counter-solutionism frame explicit as an organizing thesis in ["Disrupting the Gospel of Tech Solutionism to Build Tech Justice"](https://ssir.org/articles/entry/disrupting_the_gospel_of_tech_solutionism_to_build_tech_justice) (*Stanford Social Innovation Review*, June 2022), the inaugural essay of the Just Tech fellowship at the Social Science Research Council. The "gospel" formulation is deliberate: solutionism functions as an ideological commitment carried by faith, not evidence — the repeated failure of tech solutions to resolve social problems is absorbed by the ideology as a call for better tech solutions rather than for a different frame. The Just Tech project proposed building an "ecosystem of support for analysts, artists, and activists experimenting with both speculative and trusted methods" — explicitly centring communities affected by tech's failures, not the engineers proposing to fix it.

## AI-accountability register

Within AI accountability organising, the counter-solutionism framing operates most sharply against the assertion that more powerful AI will itself solve the accountability problems created by earlier AI — that better models will reduce bias, that AI safety is an engineering problem that better alignment techniques will resolve, that AI governance problems require AI governance tools. The [Distributed AI Research Institute (DAIR)](../organizations/org-dair-institute.md), founded by [Timnit Gebru](../persons/person-timnit-gebru.md) in December 2021 outside corporate and government funding structures, premises its existence on this critique: the ethics infrastructure inside AI labs is itself solutionist — it offers the *form* of accountability without the structural independence that makes accountability real. DAIR's institutional stance — that meaningful AI accountability requires being entirely outside the industry structures that drive AI deployment — is the counter-solutionism framing as organisational practice.

[Meredith Whittaker](../persons/person-meredith-whittaker.md), president of Signal Foundation and a co-founder of the AI Now Institute, has articulated the AI register sharply: the case against framing AI "as a solution to the problems in cases where it is a hunger for data, and the companies push for billions of dollars in investment." Her argument — that AI governance ultimately comes back to "relationships of power," whose problems technology has historically solved, who decides which technical approaches get developed and deployed — names the structural question that solutionism forecloses. [AlgorithmWatch](../organizations/org-algorithmwatch.md)'s civil society advocacy in European regulatory spaces has developed the same critique in the legislative register: the impulse to address AI risks through AI-specific technical standards and auditing requirements is itself a solutionist move that can displace binding rights protection. The [ethics washing](msg-ethics-washing.md) framing — the critique that AI ethics discourse functions as a PR instrument forestalling binding regulation — is the narrower sibling of the counter-solutionism argument: where ethics washing names one specific genre of solutionism (ethics-as-substitute-for-law), "tech won't save us" names the broader ideological commitment of which ethics washing is one expression.

## Climate justice register

The counter-solutionism framing's most active grassroots expression in the 2020s has been in climate justice organising, where it targets the proposition that electric vehicles, green data centres, carbon capture technology, and AI climate modelling can substitute for structural economic change. [TechPolicy.Press's](https://www.techpolicy.press/big-tech-will-not-save-us-from-the-climate-crisis/) analysis of Big Tech's voluntary climate commitments — describing them as "flimsiness" designed to "pacify demands for livable communities, clean air, and drinkable water" without structural change — applies the core counter-solutionism argument to the climate domain. The criticism is structural: the technologies are unproven at the scale their claims require; the commitments are voluntary, guided by corporate strategic interest; and they reproduce the solutionist substitution of technological promise for political change.

*Nonprofit Quarterly*'s ["They Say Climate Tech Will Save Us, but Who Will Save Us from Climate Tech?"](https://nonprofitquarterly.org/they-say-climate-tech-will-save-us-but-who-will-save-us-from-climate-tech/) articulated the environmental-justice dimension: climate tech solutionism "operates within racial capitalism and extractivism rather than confronting them, keeping old hierarchies intact, just in shinier packaging." Community-owned alternatives — worker cooperatives, democratic utility control, participatory governance of energy infrastructure — are named as the substantive politics that the solutionist framing displaces. The Amazon Employees for Climate Justice campaign, tech worker organizing at Alphabet and Microsoft, and the Stop Data Centers coalition (which demanded a federal moratorium on new AI data center construction) each connect this register to the AI-specific context: AI's energy consumption and fossil-fuel supply chain partnerships are a concrete instance where the "tech will solve climate" claim is being tested against material conditions in real communities.

## Relationship to adjacent framings

The counter-solutionism framing intersects with several adjacent framings in the corpus without being reducible to any of them. [Surveillance capitalism](msg-surveillance-capitalism.md) names a specific economic logic — surveillance and behavioural prediction as a business model — while "tech won't save us" names the broader ideological shell that shelters that logic from political challenge. [Data colonialism](msg-data-colonialism.md) names a structural extraction relationship between the Global North data economy and the Global South; "tech won't save us" provides the ideological critique explaining why that extraction is presented as development benefit. [Ethics washing](msg-ethics-washing.md) is the narrower sibling noted above. The framing's breadth is its political utility and its analytical limitation: it connects disparate critiques under a common name but does not specify which structural alternatives should replace the solutionism it diagnoses, which is what each of those adjacent framings begins to do in its own domain.

## Why it has carried

The counter-solutionism framing's reach across critical technology studies, AI accountability organising, climate justice, and labour politics reflects its structural versatility: it is a meta-critique applicable wherever a technological promise displaces a political necessity. Any AI safety proposal that relies on better technical alignment without addressing the governance structures deciding what systems get built; any climate pledge that commits to technological decarbonisation without addressing the extractivist economic relations driving emissions; any labour platform promising algorithmic management improvements without addressing the collective bargaining that would give workers power over those systems — each is legible through the counter-solutionism frame.

The framing's limit is its asymmetry. Counter-solutionism is analytically corrosive — it dissolves the claims of technological fixes — but does not itself specify what the structural alternatives look like with enough tactical concreteness to anchor campaigns, legislation, or institutions. "Tech won't save us" names a failure mode more effectively than it organises toward a replacement. [MediaJustice](../organizations/org-mediajustice.md)'s community-centred work, DAIR's independence model, and the cooperative alternatives named by environmental justice organisers each represent attempts to fill that gap — to convert the framing's analytical force into an affirmative political practice. The framing has proved durable because the structural pattern it names (technological promise displacing political necessity) recurs in every new domain AI and Silicon Valley enter; whether the accumulated counter-solutionism critique generates a coherent positive programme, or remains a necessary but incomplete analytical vocabulary, is the movement's open question.
