# Data justice

**Data justice** is the affirmative register of the data rights and AI accountability movement — the framing that asks not just what is wrong with current systems of data collection and use but what fair, democratically accountable governance of data would look like. Where critical adjacent framings — [data colonialism](msg-data-colonialism.md), surveillance capitalism, algorithmic bias — diagnose a harm or name a pattern of extraction, data justice names the constructive prior question: under what conditions is datafication just? The answer, across the framing's several traditions, converges on community self-determination, structural accountability for data systems, and rights-based frameworks that ground data governance in collective power rather than individual consumer choice.

## Origin

The framing's academic crystallisation runs through [Linnet Taylor's 2017 paper "What is data justice? The case for connecting digital rights and freedoms globally"](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2053951717736335) in *Big Data & Society*. Taylor proposed three foundational rights that a global data justice framework would need to secure. **Invisibility** — the right to be able to opt out of data collection, to exist outside database infrastructures, to refuse the datafied subject position that digital systems increasingly require. **Representation** — the right to have data collected about you be accurate and representative rather than distorted by the structural biases that statistical models inherit from unequal historical data collection. **Engagement** — the right to participate meaningfully in the systems that use your data, both in the design of those systems and in the governance decisions about what they can do.

Taylor's paper was explicitly proposed as a bridge: between the Global North digital-rights tradition (European data protection, US civil liberties) and the Global South human-rights tradition (UN frameworks, development-justice organising), which had previously operated largely in parallel. Data governance was named as a site of justice work — not a technical problem to be solved by privacy engineering or a regulatory problem to be solved by data-protection law, but a political question about who has power over the systems through which social life is increasingly organised and whose interests those systems serve.

The [Data Justice Lab](https://datajusticelab.org/) at Cardiff University — founded by Lina Dencik, Arne Hintz, Joanna Redden, and Emiliano Treré — built the institutional infrastructure that has carried the framing into the research and civil-society world. The Lab's work has traced the framing across sectors (welfare systems, policing, labour, healthcare) and geographies, and its 2019 Data Justice Conference convened researchers and civil-society practitioners globally for the first time under this framing.

## Grassroots application: Our Data Bodies

The framing's most consequential grassroots-organizing application is the [Our Data Bodies](../organizations/org-our-data-bodies.md) (ODB) project — a US multi-city participatory action research initiative based in Charlotte, Detroit, and Los Angeles, 2015–2019. ODB translated the data justice register from an academic rights framework into a community organizing tool: the [*Digital Defense Playbook: Community Power Tools for Reclaiming Data*](https://www.odbproject.org/tools/) (2019), produced through approximately 135 in-depth community interviews with residents most affected by algorithmic surveillance systems.

ODB's working data justice definition placed community self-determination at the centre. The question was not only whether individual data subjects had privacy rights but whether communities had collective power over the data infrastructure that governed their access to housing, benefits, employment, and safety. The Playbook's organizing premise — that data threats facing marginalized communities are political problems requiring collective response, not hygiene lapses requiring individual behavior change — is the framing's grassroots-activism translation. The "Power Not Paranoia" framework, drawn from the Stop LAPD Spying Coalition's counter-surveillance practice and embedded in the Playbook, is its tactical spine.

[Virginia Eubanks](../persons/person-virginia-eubanks.md), an ODB co-founder, developed the parallel argument in [*Automating Inequality*](../publications/pub-automating-inequality.md) (2018) that algorithmic systems perpetuate and automate the structural inequities that welfare and criminal-legal systems had previously administered through human discretion — the same underlying claim in a book-length research register. [MediaJustice](../organizations/org-mediajustice.md) (Oakland, formerly Center for Media Justice) was a structural partner in ODB's formation, connecting the data justice framing to the longer traditions of media justice and communications-rights organising through which ODB's partner networks had been built.

## Global South applications

The framing has spread into the Global South digital-rights field, where it names a positive-justice demand alongside — and often in tension with — the critical framings that dominate international AI-governance conversation. [IT for Change](../organizations/org-it-for-change.md) (Bengaluru, founded 2000), led by [Anita Gurumurthy](../persons/person-anita-gurumurthy.md), carries the data justice register into its ["Centering Equity and Justice in Global Data Governance"](https://itforchange.net/research) research programme, which addresses how global data governance architectures — cross-border data flows, data localization debates, AI supply chains — can be redesigned to serve southern and low-income populations rather than extracting value from them. IT for Change's contribution sits at the intersection of the development-justice and digital-rights traditions: the question is whether international data governance can be made compatible with the rights and interests of the communities least represented in current governance architecture.

The [Aapti Institute](../organizations/org-aapti-institute.md) (Bengaluru, founded 2019) carries a data-stewardship strand of the framing into its work on data cooperatives and participatory data governance — the constructive complement to critical extraction analysis, asking how communities can build collective ownership structures over the data they generate.

The framing intersects with [indigenous data sovereignty](msg-indigenous-data-sovereignty.md) — the parallel framing developed by the global indigenous data sovereignty movement — at the shared question of collective governance and self-determination. The two framings share the structural insight that data governance cannot be reduced to individual privacy rights: communities and peoples, not only individual data subjects, are the relevant governance unit. They diverge in scope: indigenous data sovereignty is rooted in the specific rights and governance traditions of indigenous peoples and nations, while data justice operates as the broader umbrella covering any community seeking democratic accountability over the data systems that shape their lives. The overlap is a resource for both traditions; the distinction is a maintained boundary of each.

## Why it has carried

The data justice framing has proved durable because it fills a structural gap in the vocabulary of the data-rights and AI accountability movement.

The movement's dominant registers are overwhelmingly critical: they name harms (bias, discrimination, surveillance), diagnose structural causes (colonialism, capitalism, structural racism), and argue for removal or restriction (bans, moratoriums, accountability requirements). These are necessary framings, but they leave the question of what to build largely unaddressed. Data justice is the framing that names the goal — equitable, democratic, rights-based data governance — rather than only the problem, giving organizers and advocates something to fight *for* alongside the framings that name what they fight against. This positive register is what distinguishes it from adjacent critical framings like [data colonialism](msg-data-colonialism.md) while sharing their structural critique of the political economy of data.

The framing's second durability feature is its coalition-holding function. Data justice operates as an umbrella register under which technically distinct advocacy traditions — privacy rights, algorithmic accountability, digital divides, data sovereignty, community organising — can coordinate without resolving their differences. The term's relative generality is a feature rather than a limitation: it allows coalitions to name a shared positive goal while pursuing it through different tactics and analytical traditions. The [nothing about us](msg-nothing-about-us.md) tradition of participatory governance finds a natural home in the data justice register without being absorbed into it; feminist AI advocacy and community organising on surveillance both claim the framing without exhausting it.

The framing's limit, visible across the same record, is the double edge of the positive register: "data justice" names a goal but does not specify the path with the concrete tactical sharpness that a slogan like [#BanTheScan](msg-banthescan.md) or a specific demand like [meaningful human control](msg-meaningful-human-control.md) carries. The academic-advocacy register in which the framing operates most naturally — the Cardiff Data Justice Conference, the IT for Change research programme, the ODB Playbook's community-education work — gives it institutional staying power as a research and policy vocabulary but does not generate a protest-line register of its own. Where adjacent framings have generated hashtags, campaign identities, and street mobilization, data justice anchors a more dispersed research-and-organizing tradition whose power accumulates across institutional relationships rather than through concentrated public moments.
