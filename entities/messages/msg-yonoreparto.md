# "#YoNoReparto"

"#YoNoReparto" — Spanish for "I won't deliver" — is the hashtag and counter-narrative through which gig-economy delivery workers across Latin America and Spain organized a cycle of international strikes from April 2020 onward against algorithmic pay-setting, labour misclassification, and the absence of workplace protections on app-delivery platforms. The movement's central slogan, ["No somos superhéroes, somos superexplotados"](https://izquierdasocialista.org.ar/2020/index.php/blog/elsocialista/item/17464-paro-de-repartidores-no-somos-superheroes-somos-superexplotados) ("We are not superheroes, we are super-exploited"), rejected the pandemic-era cultural framing that valorised delivery workers as essential heroes while platforms offered no safety equipment, accident insurance, or employment rights. Coined and first mobilised by Argentine delivery worker groups in April 2020, #YoNoReparto became the Spanish-language organising call for what grew into a six-country international action on 22 April 2020, expanded to Mexico and Chile by 29 May, connected with the Brazilian [#BrequeDosApps](msg-breque-dos-apps.md) movement on 1 July, and extended to four continents by October 2020.

## Origin

Argentine delivery workers began [organising informal protest actions in Buenos Aires, Neuquén, and Mar del Plata](https://botpopuli.net/covid-pandemic-precarity-platform-delivery-workers-strike-latin-america/) in the years before the pandemic — partial strikes against Glovo, Rappi, and Uber Eats that established the first networks across which the hashtag would later travel. Guatemala held its own delivery-worker action on 5 April 2020, two weeks before the international date. The hashtag `#YoNoReparto` was assembled by the Agrupación de Trabajadores de Reparto (ATR) and Glovers Unidos Argentina to call the first coordinated international action for 22 April 2020. The backing groups — Glovers Unidos Spain, Glovers Ecuador, Glovers Costa Rica, Glovers Elite de Guatemala, and Repartidores de Peru — confirmed the Latin American and Iberian dimension from the start.

The timing was structural. The COVID-19 pandemic had simultaneously made delivery workers [indispensable and disposable](https://botpopuli.net/covid-pandemic-precarity-platform-delivery-workers-strike-latin-america/): food-delivery orders surged as lockdowns closed restaurants, but platforms classified workers as independent contractors, which meant no PPE obligations, no accident insurance, no sick pay, no quarantine compensation, and no minimum earnings floor. At the same time, newly unemployed workers entering the gig economy as a last resort depressed per-delivery rates. The ATR's framing — "we don't want to be heroes, we want safety equipment, time-off and quarantine compensation" — named the exact mechanism: the "hero" label was being deployed by platforms to obtain public goodwill while avoiding any legal obligation to the workers it celebrated.

## The counter-narrative

The platform economy built its labour model on a legal architecture of misclassification: workers were "partners," "independent micro-entrepreneurs," "clients" of the platform's service — never employees. This classification meant the algorithm that set their routes, their rates, their rankings, and their eligibility for high-value deliveries was a market mechanism, not a management tool, and workers had no employment rights against it. `#YoNoReparto`'s central innovation was to name this mechanism from the workers' perspective.

["No somos superhéroes, somos superexplotados"](https://izquierdasocialista.org.ar/2020/index.php/blog/elsocialista/item/17464-paro-de-repartidores-no-somos-superheroes-somos-superexplotados) expressed both a cultural and a legal claim simultaneously: the cultural claim was that the "hero" label was flattery used to extract work without rights; the legal claim was that what platforms called autonomous partnership was in practice directed labour — "superexplotación" (super-exploitation) requiring recognition and protection. The hashtag's refusal construction — "I *won't* deliver" — placed the locus of the breach on the platform, not the worker: the worker who earned below minimum wage, had no accident coverage, and received no transparent explanation for algorithmic pay reductions was the wronged party exercising a legitimate refusal, not an employee breaching a contract.

## Strike cycle 2020

The April 22, 2020 action drew roughly [18,000 registered Rappi and Glovo workers](https://izquierdasocialista.org.ar/2020/index.php/blog/elsocialista/item/17464-paro-de-repartidores-no-somos-superheroes-somos-superexplotados) in Argentina and counterparts in Ecuador, Guatemala, Costa Rica, Peru, and Spain. Core demands were a 100% increase in payment per delivery, provision of safety and hygiene equipment by the platforms, accident insurance, and an end to unjustified account deactivations.

[A second wave on 29 May 2020](https://contxto.com/en/mexico/delivery-workers-strike/) expanded the coordination to Mexico, Chile, and additional countries. By this point the demands had grown to include recognition as employees rather than independent contractors, paid quarantine leave, reinstatement of workers whose accounts had been terminated for organising activity, and justice for workers who had died or been injured on the job. Participating platforms included Glovo, Rappi, PedidosYa, UberEats, and SinDelantal.

The [third wave on 1 July 2020](https://www.americasquarterly.org/article/bumpy-ride-why-brazils-delivery-workers-are-fed-up/) coincided with and connected to the first Brazilian Breque dos Apps — the moment at which workers' own organisers described the international coordination as their first significant demonstration with international dimensions. #YoNoReparto and #BrequeDosApps ran as [parallel hashtags for the same international action](https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/2020/07/04/braz-j04.html), each anchored in a distinct national movement and language community but united by common demands: higher pay, insurance, an end to punitive scoring systems, access to basic facilities including phone charging points, bathrooms, water, and eating areas.

A fourth wave on 8 October 2020 extended the movement beyond Latin America to four continents, with confirmed participation from Nigeria, Japan, Germany, France, and Spain alongside the established Latin American base.

## Mexico: national dimension

Mexico's delivery worker movement had an independent origin predating the international #YoNoReparto cycle. [Ni Un Repartidor Menos](https://elcomercio.pe/mundo/mexico/ni-un-repartidor-menos-mexico-accidentes-secuestros-asesinatos-los-repartidores-luchan-por-su-seguridad-en-la-cdmx-delivery-historias-ec-noticia/) — "Not One Delivery Worker Less," an intentional echo of the feminist `#NiUnaMenos` movement — was founded in Mexico City on 27 November 2018, after José Manuel Matías Flores was killed by a garbage truck on his first UberEats delivery shift. The collective, led by Saúl Gómez Piña, focused initially on road safety: [documenting fifty-six worker deaths in the sector from March 2020 alone](https://elcomercio.pe/mundo/mexico/ni-un-repartidor-menos-mexico-accidentes-secuestros-asesinatos-los-repartidores-luchan-por-su-seguridad-en-la-cdmx-delivery-historias-ec-noticia/), organising sit-ins at the National Palace, and building memorials for workers killed in traffic accidents, robberies, and customer violence.

The Mexican movement diversified through the pandemic. [Unión de Repas](https://restofworld.org/2022/mexico-gig-workers-full-time-employees/) — with nearly 900 members primarily from Mexico City and the State of Mexico — focused on work flexibility and gender-specific protections for women, transgender, and disabled workers; its spokespeople Gabriela Gómez and Alfredo García Campos became prominent voices in the regulatory debate. UNTA (National Union of App Workers) registered with Mexico's labour department and negotiated formal classification proposals. By 2022, a manifesto signed by over fifty worker organisations had framed the sector's demands around autonomy rather than formal employment — workers opposing reclassification proposals that they feared would subordinate their schedule flexibility to company control.

The country-specific hashtag variant #YoNoRepartoMX circulated alongside the international #YoNoReparto tag during the 2020 strike cycle and in subsequent Mexican street actions, including a November 2021 cycling demonstration demanding labour rights in Mexico City.

## Regulatory legacy

The #YoNoReparto cycle seeded a regional legislative debate that ran from 2020 through the mid-2020s. [Mexico's Federal Labour Law reform for platform workers](https://www.nmas.com.mx/nacional/politica/ley-repartidores-2025-reforma-laboral-uber-didi-mexico-que-es-y-cuando-entra-en-vigor/), published in the Official Journal on 24 December 2024, is the most direct legislative outcome: it extends social security coverage (IMSS), pension access, Infonavit housing credits, and accident insurance to platform workers earning at least minimum wage, and — in direct address to the demands that had animated the strike cycle — mandates algorithmic transparency ("las reglas del algoritmo deben ser claras y comprensibles": algorithm rules must be clear and understandable) and requires that complaints be reviewed by humans rather than automated systems. A pilot phase runs from July to December 2025; full implementation begins 1 January 2026. The reform affects an estimated 658,000 workers in the sector. Worker organisations including Ni Un Repartidor Menos, Unión de Repas, and UNTA participated in the negotiation process, though divisions persisted: several independent groups opposed the reform as prioritising government tax collection over worker-defined autonomy.

## Why it has carried

Three features distinguish #YoNoReparto from the wider field of platform-labour messaging.

First, the refusal construction is precise. The hashtag does not demand better treatment from the algorithm — it names the worker as the party who refuses to deliver *to* a system that has failed to deliver rights *to* them. This reversal of the gig platform's own contractual logic was legible both as a strike call (a work stoppage) and as a political claim (a demand that misclassification be named for what it is). The same double meaning made it portable across very different national regulatory contexts.

Second, the counter-narrative targeted a specific cultural mechanism: the "hero" framing. "No somos superhéroes, somos superexplotados" did not argue abstractly about algorithmic management — it named the specific discursive move platforms and media had made, and rejected it on both cultural and legal grounds simultaneously. Its structural parallel to the Brazilian Breque dos Apps slogans ("Não somos heróis, somos trabalhadores"; "Não somos empreendedores") was not coincidence: both movements were contesting the same cultural and legal architecture in different national contexts, and the slogans converged independently on the same form of refusal.

Third, the movement's international coordination under a single hashtag demonstrated that algorithmic pay-setting was a transnational condition requiring transnational solidarity — a claim that the July 2020 connection with #BrequeDosApps made visible at scale. The four-continent reach of the October 2020 wave, extending to Europe, Asia, and North America, showed that the gig-labour counter-narrative had crossed the Spanish-language and Latin American boundaries of its origin, even as #YoNoReparto remained the Spanish-language anchor.
