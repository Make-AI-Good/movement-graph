# Amelia Andersdotter

Amelia Andersdotter is the Swedish digital-rights activist and technical-standards specialist who bridged Pirate Party parliamentary organising through civil-society infrastructure-building into a distinctive position as a technical-standards consultant carrying a human-rights mandate inside international standardisation bodies (see [Person entry](../persons/person-amelia-andersdotter.md)). She served as a Member of the European Parliament [from December 2011 to May 2014](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amelia_Andersdotter), sat on the Committee on Industry, Research and Energy, and was that committee's rapporteur on the Anti-Counterfeiting Trade Agreement (ACTA) — the full European Parliament voted to reject ACTA in July 2012. She is tracked here as a Voice because her public-output register — the [2021 Internet Policy Review article](https://policyreview.info/articles/analysis/policy-strategies-value-based-technology-standards) on standards as political instruments; the named-byline [EDRi](../organizations/org-edri.md) blog work on 5G standards and browser privacy; the webbkoll.dataskydd.net tool she co-created as chairperson of Dataskydd.net Sverige; and the sustained speaker footprint moving between engineering venues (FOSDEM, IEEE, IETF, HotPETS) and rights-advocacy venues (EDRi, Netnod, BornHack) — is itself the load-bearing object the corpus needs to track, and is not captured by the affiliation-and-recognition structure of the linked [Person entry](../persons/person-amelia-andersdotter.md).

## Voice anchor

Andersdotter's Voice closes two overlapping gaps in the corpus.

- **First Nordic/Scandinavian voice.** The corpus's voices slice had previously run UK, US, Continental Europe (Poland, Germany, Belgium-Brussels), Africa, Latin America, and South and West Asia, with no Swedish, Norwegian, Danish, Finnish, or Nordic voice. Scandinavian digital-rights organising is a structurally significant strand of European internet governance — the Swedish Pirate Party's 2006 founding, its European Parliament presence from 2009, Dataskydd.net's long-running GDPR-practice work, and Nordic civil-society influence inside [EDRi](../organizations/org-edri.md) — but no Voice entry had anchored it. Andersdotter anchors the Swedish register from inside three overlapping institutional tracks: the parliamentary track (MEP 2011–2014), the civil-society organising track (Pirate Party, Dataskydd.net, [EDRi](../organizations/org-edri.md)), and the technical-standards track ([ARTICLE 19](../organizations/org-article-19.md) / IEEE / IETF / 3GPP). She is structurally distinct from the Continental European voices already in the corpus: unlike [Katarzyna Szymielewicz](../persons/person-katarzyna-szymielewicz.md) (Polish national-member-organisation principal track inside EDRi) or [Ella Jakubowska](../persons/person-ella-jakubowska.md) (EDRi Brussels-secretariat policy leadership), Andersdotter's primary register runs through the engineering-standards track rather than the political-advocacy or legal-research track, and is anchored in Swedish civil society rather than Brussels institutional infrastructure.

- **First technical-standards-as-rights bridge.** The corpus's existing voice anchors approach digital rights primarily through legal advocacy, academic scholarship, journalism, and grassroots organising. Andersdotter occupies a structurally distinct position: active named participation in technical standards bodies — IEEE 802.11 privacy working group, IETF human-rights protocol considerations group, 3GPP privacy recommendations — with an [ARTICLE 19](../organizations/org-article-19.md) human-rights-advocacy mandate. Her public output translates this bridge into form accessible to three different audiences: the [Internet Policy Review article](https://policyreview.info/articles/analysis/policy-strategies-value-based-technology-standards) explains to policy audiences why technical standards are political; the EDRi blog posts explain to civil-society audiences why 5G and DNS-over-HTTPS choices matter for privacy and competition; the engineering-venue talks (FOSDEM, HotPETS, BornHack) explain to developer and infrastructure-operator audiences why human rights must enter standardisation rooms.

## Signature framings

Three arguments anchor Andersdotter's public-output register.

- **Technical standards are political instruments, not neutral technical choices.** The core thesis advanced across her [ARTICLE 19](../organizations/org-article-19.md) work, the [Internet Policy Review article](https://policyreview.info/articles/analysis/policy-strategies-value-based-technology-standards) co-authored with Łukasz Olejnik (September 2021), and her speaker footprint: standards bodies "already influence societal values through technical choices," and the appropriate response is not to treat them as outside politics but to embed deliberate value-commitments — privacy, interoperability, competition — into standardisation processes. The practical case she makes is EU regulatory engagement: Europe's 1985 New Approach standardisation framework was designed for physical-product harmonisation and is structurally ill-suited to ICT, where innovation and standardisation happen simultaneously and where standards determine what privacy-respecting or surveillance-enabling technical choices are available to deployers. The lesson drawn from the failed W3C "Do Not Track" standard — that technical standards need active policy backing to succeed in embedding rights values — recurs as the case for deliberate state and civil-society engagement in bodies like IEEE, IETF, and 3GPP rather than leaving them to industry.

- **"Digital colonies" — European strategic infrastructure dependence as a digital-rights problem.** Andersdotter's framing in her October 2019 Netnod Tech Meet-Up talk ("Digital colonies — EU strategies for independence?"): that EU digital infrastructure dependence on US-headquartered cloud, platform, and telecommunications infrastructure is not merely an economic or sovereignty question but a structural vulnerability for European digital rights and democratic autonomy. The framing bridges the Nordic civil-society tradition of approaching internet governance through state-capacity and democratic-infrastructure lenses with the Brussels digital-sovereignty discourse that grew from the Snowden revelations and the Schrems I and II invalidations of Privacy Shield. Her 2020 whitepaper on the Schrems II ruling for Safespring AB — a Swedish cloud-infrastructure provider — brings the "digital colonies" framing into concrete data-sovereignty form, analysing the operational consequences of the Court of Justice ruling for European organisations processing data through US-headquartered services.

- **Practical transparency tools over policy asks.** Andersdotter and Anders Jensen-Urstad's co-creation of [webbkoll.dataskydd.net](https://webbkoll.dataskydd.net/en/about) under Dataskydd.net Sverige (2015–2024, now maintained by the 5th July Foundation) embodies a distinctive tactic: rather than asking policymakers or platforms to improve privacy practices, build a public tool that makes existing practices transparent and legible to any user, regulator, or civil-society organisation. The tool — which checks what tracking technologies a website deploys from the user's perspective rather than from the server's configuration — has been adopted as a reference by the French government, German digital-rights networks, and ANEC (the European consumer-standards body). Its design reflects her technical-standards background: the question answered is the one that matters from the rights perspective, not the one easiest to measure from inside the system.

## Public output and venues

Andersdotter's public-facing work runs through three overlapping channels.

- **Academic-policy writing.** The flagship is the [September 2021 Internet Policy Review article](https://policyreview.info/articles/analysis/policy-strategies-value-based-technology-standards) "Policy strategies for value-based technology standards" (co-authored with Łukasz Olejnik), arguing that Europe needs a structured strategy for embedding European values into global technical standards bodies and proposing three concrete policy directions: simplified re-standardisation procedures, a modern EU standards-engagement strategy, and structured participation for value-focused civil-society stakeholders. Two shorter pieces on the Internet Policy Review platform — "New Charter unlikely to solve ailments of fundamental rights in digital environments" and "Internet governance needs to develop ambitions" — extend the register into governance-design commentary. Her CENTR Tech Trends Watch series (from April 2020) brings the standards-and-governance lens to the top-level-domain operator community. The IFIP Summer School 2016 proceedings paper "Evaluating Websites and Their Adherence to Data Protection Principles" (co-authored with Jensen-Urstad) is the underlying research that webbkoll operationalises.

- **Named-byline civil-society writing.** Her [EDRi](../organizations/org-edri.md) blog byline includes two 2018 pieces co-authored with Mallory Knodel: the October 2018 analysis ["New standards for networking challenge regulators & digital rights"](https://edri.org/new-standards-for-networking-challenges-regulators-and-digital-rights/) — arguing that 5G standards embed vertical integration that undermines EU telecommunications-law competition goals, and that regulators must engage with IEEE and 3GPP if they want rights-respecting outcomes — and the 2018 piece on DNS-over-HTTPS and browser privacy, which established the connection between technical protocol choices and user-side privacy protection. A 2020 whitepaper on the Schrems II ruling for Safespring AB supplies the data-sovereignty-practitioner register alongside the civil-society writing.

- **Speaker footprint bridging engineering and civil-society venues.** Andersdotter is a recurring named speaker at both technical-standards and civil-society conferences. Her FOSDEM 2025 talk ["Digital Identities in disarray"](https://archive.fosdem.org/2025/schedule/speaker/amelia_andersdotter/) (Open Source in the European Legislative Landscape track, February 2025) is the most recent entry in a long footprint. The 2019 cluster illustrates the range: [HotPETS 2019](https://amelia.andersdotter.cc/talks.html) ("Good privacy news from technical standards development bodies — the future is bright!") in the privacy-engineering research symposium; BornHack 2019 twice ("Adventures in hardware standardisation" and "We're in it for the long-haul — reflections on European internet policies in 2019"); the Netnod Tech Meet-Up ("Digital colonies — EU strategies for independence?") before a Nordic internet-infrastructure operator audience; and the DPForum IAPP Xmas event ("This year in data protection"). A September 2024 ULB SICT Doctoral School invited talk ("Greening network equipment and standardisation") anchors the most recent academic-venue entry.

## Why this is a Voice entry

A Voice entry is created here because Andersdotter's public output — the [Internet Policy Review](https://policyreview.info/articles/analysis/policy-strategies-value-based-technology-standards) academic-policy writing register, the [EDRi](../organizations/org-edri.md) named-byline civil-society writing on 5G and browser privacy, the webbkoll.dataskydd.net practical transparency tool, and the sustained speaker footprint moving between engineering (FOSDEM, IEEE, IETF, HotPETS) and civil-society (EDRi, Netnod, BornHack) venues — is the load-bearing object the corpus needs to track. The [Person entry](../persons/person-amelia-andersdotter.md) records her institutional affiliations ([ARTICLE 19](../organizations/org-article-19.md), [Ada Lovelace Institute](../organizations/org-ada-lovelace-institute.md), [EDRi](../organizations/org-edri.md), Dataskydd.net, Universal Privacy Alliance); this Voice entry tracks the public-output practice through which she has made the argument that technical standards are not neutral — and that the appropriate response to that non-neutrality is both institutional engagement in standardisation bodies and practical tool-building that makes the effects of technical choices legible to non-engineers. The corpus's voices slice had no Swedish, Nordic, or Scandinavian entry and no voice anchoring the technical-standards-as-rights-infrastructure register before this draft; this entry gives both their first anchor.
