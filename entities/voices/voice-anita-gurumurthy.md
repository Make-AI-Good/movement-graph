# Anita Gurumurthy

Anita Gurumurthy is the founding Executive Director and Senior Fellow, Research & Policy Engagement at [IT for Change](../organizations/org-it-for-change.md), the Bengaluru-based research-advocacy NGO she co-founded in 2000 (see [Person entry](../persons/person-anita-gurumurthy.md)). She is tracked here as a Voice because her public-facing output has produced a body of scholarly writing, policy submissions, and conference advocacy that has installed into international AI and data governance discourse a distinctive set of framings — AI constitutionalism, data extractivism, feminist data sovereignty, nested sovereignty, knowledge commons — that define the structural-critique-and-multilateral-engagement position of Global South feminist civil society on AI governance.

The Voice anchors three movement-area registers that the corpus's voices slice had previously left empty.

- **The first Indian and South Asian feminist AI governance Voice.** The corpus's South Asian voices had previously run only through [Nighat Dad](voice-nighat-dad.md) (Pakistan / feminist-tech / lawyer-organiser-and-multilateral-body) and [Apar Gupta](voice-apar-gupta.md) (India / civil-liberties / lawyer-founder-and-columnist). Gurumurthy anchors a distinct Indian sub-register — the scholar-practitioner at the intersection of feminist data governance, platform political economy, and UN multilateral engagement — that has no overlap with the civil-liberties litigation register of Gupta's voice or the online-violence helpline register of Dad's. The Person side has Gurumurthy and the IT for Change institutional register in corpus through [person-anita-gurumurthy](../persons/person-anita-gurumurthy.md) and [org-it-for-change](../organizations/org-it-for-change.md); the Voice now anchors the public-output footprint — twenty-plus years of essays, reports, journal articles, and multilateral interventions — through which that institutional register is carried into international discourse.

- **The AI constitutionalism framing anchor.** Gurumurthy is the corpus's most persistent and rigorous proponent of "AI constitutionalism" — the argument that AI governance requires structural legal-institutional transformation (rights-based frameworks, multilateral treaties, democratic data governance) rather than corporate ethics principles or sectoral technical standards. No other Voice in the corpus has developed this framing at the same scholarly and institutional depth; the [Development journal article on global digital constitutionalism](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8090914/) (2021) and the sustained UN process engagement are the empirical anchor for this register.

- **The feminist data sovereignty / data extractivism scholarly voice.** The corpus's feminist-data register had previously run through applied research organisations ([Pollicy](../organizations/org-pollicy.md), [Neema Iyer](voice-neema-iyer.md)), strategic litigation ([Cori Crider](voice-cori-crider.md)), and algorithmic-accountability frameworks ([Joy Buolamwini](../persons/person-joy-buolamwini.md)). Gurumurthy occupies the scholar-practitioner position: producing peer-reviewed articles in Development, Economic and Political Weekly, and Internet Policy Review; IT for Change working papers cited in UN CSTD and UNCTAD processes; and direct submissions to the UN Secretary-General's machinery that carry the feminist data sovereignty vocabulary into the machinery's outputs. The ["Beyond Data Bodies" working paper](https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.4037321) (December 2021, co-authored with Nandini Chami) — which moves feminist data sovereignty beyond individual bodily rights toward a social knowledge commons framing — is the corpus's clearest single anchor on the theoretical frontier of the field.

## Public output and venues

Gurumurthy's public-facing work runs through four overlapping channels.

- **The openDemocracy and popular-policy essay register.** Gurumurthy's principal popular-audience anchor is the [openDemocracy essay "How to make AI work for people and planet"](https://www.opendemocracy.net/en/how-make-ai-work-people-and-planet/) (March 2020, updated April 2026), which proposes four criteria for an AI governance architecture worth the name: sustainable datafication (addressing AI's ecological cost), economic democracy (redistributing AI-generated value), multiple value imaginaries (de-centering market metrics), and nested sovereignty (establishing layered rights for individuals, communities, and nations). The essay is the most widely circulated single condensation of the structural AI governance argument Gurumurthy develops across her more technical writing and is the register through which her frameworks have reached the broadest international civil-society audience.

- **The peer-reviewed journal and academic publication register.** Gurumurthy's scholarly register spans Development (Springer Nature), Economic and Political Weekly, and Internet Policy Review. The [Development journal article "Towards a Global Digital Constitutionalism: A Radical New Agenda for UN75"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8090914/) (Vol. 64, No. 1, 2021) is the corpus's clearest single academic source for the AI constitutionalism framing — proposing a global multilateral rights framework for internet and data governance in place of technical-standards-based governance. The [IT for Change / Data Governance Network working paper "Beyond Data Bodies: New Directions for a Feminist Theory of Data Sovereignty"](https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.4037321) (December 2021, co-authored with Nandini Chami) develops the feminist-data-sovereignty argument beyond individual bodily-rights approaches toward data-as-social-knowledge-commons, using menstrual-tracking-app surveillance as the case study.

- **The UN multilateral process engagement register.** Gurumurthy's multilateral engagement is among the deepest of any civil society actor in the corpus. She [serves on the UN Secretary-General's 10-Member Group supporting the Technology Facilitation Mechanism](https://sdgs.un.org/panelists/ms-anita-gurumurthy-33200) and participated in the High Level Committee of NetMundial+10, the Paris Peace Forum's algorithmic governance working group, and the UN CSTD's Working Group on Data Governance (2024–25), and co-led the T20 digital transformation track during both South Africa's and Brazil's G20 presidencies. Her engagement spans the UN Human Rights Council, UN-CEDAW, UN-CSW, UNCTAD, UNESCO, and WTO Public Forum — a breadth that marks her as a scholar-practitioner who combines research-generation with direct participation in the multilateral processes shaping AI and data governance outcomes.

- **The IT for Change institutional research and UN submission channel.** [IT for Change](../organizations/org-it-for-change.md)'s research output — the ReGenAI programme, the Reform the Gig platform-labour series, the FemFirst Research Observatory, the Centering Equity and Justice in Global Data Governance project — carries Gurumurthy's frameworks into institutional policy processes. Recent submissions include the 2024 IT for Change paper to the UN CSTD Inter-sessional Panel on integrated data and AI governance, and "Encoding Digital Technologies for a Feminist Social Contract" (2024, co-authored with Anuradha Ganapathy and Nandini Chami), submitted to UN DESA.

## Signature framings

Four formulations recur across Gurumurthy's public output and have done the most to install her register into the international AI governance and feminist-tech field.

- **"AI constitutionalism" — structural rights-based governance beyond ethics.** Gurumurthy's principal analytical contribution to the AI governance debate is the argument, most fully made in the [Development journal article](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8090914/) (2021) and restated across her UN submissions, that AI governance cannot be secured through corporate ethics principles or sectoral technical standards — it requires a structural constitutional framework at the multilateral level, rooted in the full spectrum of human rights (civil, political, and socio-economic). The "AI constitutionalism" framing names the absence of a rights-based architectural layer in existing AI governance and proposes a global digital constitutionalism as its replacement. It is the framing through which [IT for Change](../organizations/org-it-for-change.md)'s institutional posture is carried into UN and G20 processes.

- **"Data extractivism" — platform capitalism as extraction.** Across the platform-economy research strand and the structural AI governance essays, Gurumurthy deploys "data extractivism" to name the pattern by which the contemporary digital economy concentrates data's generative value in platform corporations while externalizing ecological costs and labor precarity onto workers, communities, and Global South countries. The framing runs through the ReGenAI programme and the Reform the Gig labour research, and positions [IT for Change](../organizations/org-it-for-change.md) within the international critical digital-studies field alongside [Neema Iyer](voice-neema-iyer.md)'s "digital extractivism in Africa mirrors colonial practices" framing.

- **"Feminist data sovereignty" as knowledge commons, not bodily rights.** The ["Beyond Data Bodies" working paper](https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.4037321) (December 2021, with Nandini Chami) makes the most theoretically precise version of this framing: the dominant feminist response to data surveillance has been organised around bodily autonomy (my body, my data), but bodily autonomy is an individual-rights framework that cannot address the structural problem — data is social, generated in and through collective relations, and governance must match that reality. The alternative Gurumurthy proposes is data as a social knowledge commons, governed by feminist relational ontology and collective consent structures rather than individual property rights. The case study is menstrual-tracking-app surveillance, where the failure of the individual-rights response is structurally visible.

- **"Nested sovereignty" — layered rights for individuals, communities, and nations.** The ["How to make AI work for people and planet"](https://www.opendemocracy.net/en/how-make-ai-work-people-and-planet/) essay (2020) introduces "nested sovereignty" as one of four criteria for a just AI governance architecture: rights to data and AI benefits must be established at multiple overlapping scales — individual, community, national — rather than reduced to either individual consumer rights or state sovereignty alone. The framing addresses the Global South concern that individual-rights frameworks in data governance reproduce Global North epistemic dominance by treating national data-governance interests as protectionist rather than legitimate.

## Organisational vehicle

Gurumurthy's public output runs entirely through [IT for Change](../organizations/org-it-for-change.md), the Bengaluru-based research-advocacy NGO she co-founded in 2000 and continues to lead as Executive Director. The organisation's portfolio — the Intelligent Infrastructures programme on democratic data governance; the Reform the Gig platform-labour research; the ReGenAI programme challenging AI development's concentration and ecological harm; the FemFirst Research Observatory building a sub-regional scholarly community on feminist AI futures in South and Southeast Asia; the Future of Rights and Citizenship in the Digital Age algorithmic governance research; and the Global Digital Compact multilateral engagement — is the substantive vehicle through which her frameworks are developed, tested, and carried into international policy processes. Outside IT for Change, her [ETC Group board membership](https://www.etcgroup.org/users/anita-gurumurthy) routes her into the international civil-society network tracking corporate power and emerging technology concentration, and her [Transnational Institute](https://www.tni.org/en/profile/anita-gurumurthy/) affiliation connects her work to the global progressive research-advocacy network on platform regulation and data capitalism.

## Why this is a Voice entry

A Voice entry is created here, rather than additional structure on the [Person entry](../persons/person-anita-gurumurthy.md), because Gurumurthy's public-facing output is itself the load-bearing object the corpus needs to track: the "AI constitutionalism" framing that has installed into international AI governance discourse the argument for structural rights-based multilateral frameworks over ethics principles, most fully articulated in the [Development journal article](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8090914/) (2021); the "feminist data sovereignty as knowledge commons" argument in the ["Beyond Data Bodies" working paper](https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.4037321) (2021, with Nandini Chami), which has moved the frontier of the feminist data governance debate beyond individual bodily-rights approaches; the "data extractivism" critique of platform capitalism, which positions Global South civil society as a structural analyst of the AI economy; the "nested sovereignty" framing that gives multilateral AI governance a rights vocabulary beyond individual consumer rights and state sovereignty; and the sustained UN process participation — the 10-Member Group, NetMundial+10, UN CSTD, T20, Paris Peace Forum, UN Human Rights Council, CEDAW, CSW, UNCTAD, UNESCO, WTO Public Forum — through which these frameworks have entered the actual outputs of the UN digital governance machinery. The corpus's voices slice carried no Indian anchor on feminist data governance, no AI constitutionalism anchor, and no scholar-practitioner-in-multilateral-process sub-type before this entry; this entry gives all three their first first-person voice.
