# Bonnie Docherty

Bonnie Docherty is [Senior Arms Advisor](https://www.hrw.org/about/people/bonnie-docherty) in Human Rights Watch's Crisis, Conflict and Arms Division and [Lecturer on Law at Harvard Law School](https://humanrightsclinic.law.harvard.edu/team/bonnie-docherty/), where she also directs the International Human Rights Clinic's Armed Conflict and Civilian Protection Initiative. She is tracked here as a Voice because her public scholarly output — the HRW/IHRC report trilogy that built the comprehensive legal case against autonomous weapons, the conceptual vocabulary (accountability gap, meaningful human control, digital dehumanization) that became the campaign's legal architecture, and a sustained record of UN submissions and legal-venue advocacy — has done the intellectual work that gives the [Stop Killer Robots](../organizations/org-stop-killer-robots.md) coalition its legal standing. She is the scholarly-legal counterpart to [Mary Wareham](../persons/person-mary-wareham.md)'s campaign-coordination voice: Wareham installed the campaign's public framings into multilateral and press discourse; Docherty built the legal-academic structure those framings rest on. See the linked [Person entry](../persons/person-bonnie-docherty.md) for affiliations.

Her dual [HRW](../organizations/org-human-rights-watch.md) / Harvard position is the structural source of her distinctive register. HRW supplies operational reach — real-time intelligence from its crisis and conflict reporting, institutional access to UN processes, and the credibility to brief states and UN delegations. The IHRC supplies legal-academic authority — research protocols, peer-engagement register, and a teaching program that has trained the next generation of IHL practitioners. The joint HRW / IHRC reports she has led are the primary vehicle through which these two registers are fused into a single legal argument.

## The three-report arc

Docherty's signature contribution to the autonomous-weapons debate is a three-report series that built the legal case incrementally across thirteen years — starting from the IHL argument (2012), adding the human-rights-law argument (2014), then extending both into peacetime contexts (2025).

**[*Losing Humanity: The Case Against Killer Robots*](../publications/pub-losing-humanity.md) (November 2012).** The [50-page founding text](https://www.hrw.org/report/2012/11/19/losing-humanity/case-against-killer-robots) of the Stop Killer Robots coalition, jointly published by HRW and the IHRC five months before the coalition's April 2013 London launch. The report argues that fully autonomous weapons cannot satisfy international humanitarian law's requirements of distinction (discriminating combatants from civilians), proportionality (weighing military advantage against civilian harm), and military necessity. The more lasting contribution was its identification of the **accountability gap**: when a machine makes a lethal decision autonomously, no individual — not the commander who deployed it, not the programmer who wrote the targeting algorithm, not the manufacturer — can be held criminally responsible under existing legal frameworks. The gap is not a problem to be solved through better legal interpretation; it is a structural feature of systems that remove human decision-making from the lethal act. The campaign's name derived from the report's framing; the ban-advocacy posture followed from the accountability gap argument.

**"Shaking the Foundations: The Human Rights Implications of Killer Robots" (HRW/IHRC, 2014).** A follow-up report that extended the accountability-gap argument from the IHL register (applicable during armed conflict) into the human-rights-law register (applicable at all times, including peacetime). The report addressed the rights to life and to peaceful assembly — arguing that autonomous weapons could not satisfy human-rights standards for lawful use of force, including the requirements of necessity, proportionality, and precaution, even in peacetime policing contexts. The IHL + human rights law pairing established by these two reports [became the two-track legal argument](https://humanrightsclinic.law.harvard.edu/killer-robots-threaten-human-rights-during-war-peace/) the campaign and UN submissions have used ever since.

**[*A Hazard to Human Rights: Autonomous Weapons Systems and Digital Decision-Making*](../publications/pub-a-hazard-to-human-rights.md) (April 2025).** The [61-page follow-up](https://www.hrw.org/report/2025/04/28/a-hazard-to-human-rights/autonomous-weapons-systems-and-digital-decision-making), released two weeks before the 12–13 May 2025 UN informal consultations on autonomous weapons. The report extended the human-rights argument across three additional rights — the right to a remedy, the right to non-discrimination, and the right to human dignity — and introduced the concept of **digital dehumanization**: the process by which autonomous systems [reduce human beings to data proxies](https://www.hrw.org/news/2025/04/28/killer-robots-threaten-human-rights-during-war-peace) (weight, heat signature, sound patterns, movement pattern) and apply force on the basis of algorithmic classification rather than human judgment about a person's humanity. The digital-dehumanization concept maps the case against autonomous weapons into policing, protest, border enforcement, and surveillance contexts beyond the battlefield. The report functioned simultaneously as a legal artefact and a UN-process input, formally presented at the May 2025 informal consultations.

## Signature framings

Four concepts originating in Docherty's legal-academic output have passed into the working vocabulary of states, UN processes, and the autonomous-weapons literature.

**The accountability gap.** The central concept of [*Losing Humanity*](../publications/pub-losing-humanity.md) and the legal mechanism that makes a ban necessary rather than merely desirable. Docherty's formulation: when a fully autonomous weapon makes a lethal decision, criminal responsibility cannot be assigned to the weapon (which cannot be tried), to the commander (who exercised no control over the specific decision), or to the programmer (who set parameters, not specific target choices). The gap is structural — it follows from removing human decision-making from the lethal act — and the remedy is therefore systemic (prohibiting such systems) rather than interpretive (adjusting existing IHL doctrine).

**Meaningful human control.** The operative treaty prescription that flows from the accountability gap. Docherty's legal analysis defined "meaningful human control" as the threshold of human involvement over targeting and attack decisions that existing international law effectively requires — the threshold below which the accountability gap opens. "Meaningful" is load-bearing: it excludes human involvement that is nominal (a human exists in the loop but exercises no real judgment) and captures human involvement that is substantive (a human understands the situation, makes the lethal decision, and can be held accountable). The phrase has become the baseline language in treaty negotiations, UN resolutions, and state statements across more than a decade of CCW and UNGA-track debate.

**Digital dehumanization.** Docherty's 2025 conceptual contribution, introduced in [*A Hazard to Human Rights*](../publications/pub-a-hazard-to-human-rights.md). The concept captures a harm distinct from the accuracy or proportionality failures the IHL framework addresses: the reduction of a human being to a data signature and the application of lethal or restraining force on the basis of that proxy rather than a human assessment of the person's situation, intent, or dignity. Its most cited formulation is that "every person should be able to look their killer in the eye" — a framing that extends the case against autonomous weapons beyond the battlefield into any context where force is applied algorithmically.

**The IHL + human rights law dual track.** Docherty's structural contribution to the legal argument: developing the two-report foundation that showed autonomous weapons violate both the *lex specialis* of international humanitarian law (the 2012 report) and the general human rights law applicable in peacetime (the 2014 report, extended in 2025). This dual-track argument addressed the common objection that IHL, as the law of armed conflict, says nothing about peacetime applications — the 2014 and 2025 reports closed that gap and placed the campaign's legal case on complementary foundations rather than a single doctrinal track.

## UN advocacy and the Belén Communiqué pivot

Docherty's legal output has been most consequential in UN-process contexts — both as background argument in CCW informal-consultations submissions and in the 2023 strategic moment captured in the [March 2023 Just Security article](https://www.justsecurity.org/86063/latin-america-and-caribbean-nations-rally-against-autonomous-weapons-systems/) she co-authored with [Mary Wareham](../persons/person-mary-wareham.md).

The article analysed the [February 2023 Belén Communiqué](../events/event-belen-communique-laws-2023-02-24.md), in which 33 Latin American and Caribbean states issued a joint declaration calling for a new international instrument with a binding prohibition on autonomous weapons not subject to meaningful human control. The Communiqué was the first significant regional-bloc commitment on autonomous weapons — a move that shifted the negotiating dynamic from the consensus-blocked Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons track, where any major-power state could block progress, toward the UN General Assembly First Committee track. Docherty and Wareham framed the Communiqué as exactly that strategic pivot, closing: "After a decade of debate, it is urgent to start drafting new legally binding rules to prevent the automation of killing." The November 2023 UNGA First Committee resolution (164 states in support) was the direct consequence — the first UN General Assembly vote calling for a new international instrument on autonomous weapons.

For the [May 2025 UN informal consultations](https://www.hrw.org/news/2025/05/21/un-start-talks-treaty-ban-killer-robots), Docherty's [*A Hazard to Human Rights*](../publications/pub-a-hazard-to-human-rights.md) functioned simultaneously as legal artefact, campaign tool, and UN-process input — the working mode of the HRW/IHRC joint report series throughout its history.

## Why this is a Voice entry

A Voice entry is created here, rather than additional structure on the [Person entry](../persons/person-bonnie-docherty.md), because Docherty's public scholarly output is itself a load-bearing object the corpus needs to track independently of her affiliations. Three things justify the promotion.

First, the conceptual vocabulary she has produced — accountability gap, meaningful human control, digital dehumanization — is not merely cited in the campaign's materials; it *is* the campaign's legal architecture. States arguing for a treaty ban cite these concepts as their legal basis; the concepts' origins are traceable to her reports. A corpus that tracks the Stop Killer Robots cluster but not the Voice whose scholarly work supplied the cluster's legal vocabulary would be systematically incomplete.

Second, the scholarly-legal register she occupies is genuinely distinct from [Mary Wareham](../persons/person-mary-wareham.md)'s campaign-coordination register, and both registers are load-bearing on the cluster. Wareham installed public framings into multilateral and press discourse and ran the first decade of coalition advocacy. Docherty built the legal-academic structure those framings rest on. The corpus needs both registers anchored by a Voice entry — having one without the other leaves the cluster's intellectual foundation without a named voice.

Third, the three-report arc across thirteen years (2012 → 2014 → 2025) is an unusually coherent piece of scholarly-legal advocacy — systematically extending an initial IHL argument into human rights law and from battlefield to peacetime contexts as the technology and deployment contexts evolved. This arc is itself a documented strategy the corpus's analytical layer can trace. A Voice entry creates the anchor point.
