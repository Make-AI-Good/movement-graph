# Jan Vobořil

Mgr. et Mgr. Jan Vobořil, Ph.D. is [Executive Director and principal lawyer](https://edri.org/our-work/for-the-right-to-analog-iure-strengthens-the-digital-freedoms-program/) at [Iuridicum Remedium (IuRe)](../organizations/org-iure.md), Prague — the Czech Republic's sole digital-rights organisation. He has led IuRe's Digital Freedoms programme since 2009 and holds a seat on the [Government Council for Human Rights' Committee for Human Rights and Modern Technology](https://www.ochranademokracie.cz/lide/jan-voboril), advising on analogue alternatives in state digitalisation. He is tracked here as a Voice because his public output — two landmark enforcement victories in 2025, a sustained public-education campaign against biometric mass surveillance, annual Big Brother Awards, and the "Right to Analogue" framing — constitutes a coherent advocacy contribution that the corpus needs to trace independently of IuRe's institutional record. See the linked [Person entry](../persons/person-jan-voboril.md) for affiliations and biography.

The Voice anchors three registers the corpus's CEE and advocacy coverage had previously left empty.

- **The first Czech and corpus's second Central European Voice.** The corpus's Central and Eastern European presence before this entry was anchored by [Panoptykon Foundation](../organizations/org-panoptykon-foundation.md) in Poland at the organizational level; no Czech individual voice had been added. Vobořil anchors it as the principal named lawyer in the two most consequential Czech AI Act enforcement actions on the public record and as the only person at the senior operational level of the Czech Republic's sole digital-rights organisation. The structural position is analogous to [TASZ](../organizations/org-tasz.md) across the border: a small-team civil-liberties NGO combining legal aid with digital-rights strategic litigation, now running one of the first Article 5 AI Act enforcement tests in Central Europe.

- **The multi-decade litigation arc as a movement model.** No other Voice in the corpus has a completed litigation arc spanning 20 years with a final court ruling. The data-retention campaign — beginning with IuRe's Constitutional Court win in 2011, running through the 2019 setback, the 2021 civil lawsuit crowdfunding, and concluding with the April 2025 Municipal Court ruling — is the clearest corpus illustration of patient movement lawyering across three court levels and two constitutional challenges over two decades. Vobořil was lead counsel in the decisive final phase. The pattern is distinct from the faster complaint-to-fine cycles of the adtech enforcement model and from the one-case litigation model; it names sustained institutional litigation as a viable movement strategy at much longer timescales.

- **"Right to Analogue" as a novel legal framing.** No other Voice in the corpus has named "the right to analogue" as a distinct legal and political category — the affirmative claim that citizens cannot be compelled to engage with state services through digital-only interfaces and that access to cash, paper forms, and non-digital service delivery is a rights claim. This framing routes digital exclusion through rights rather than service-design, making it legible to civil-liberties law rather than accessibility policy. It is the first entry in the corpus to name this as a distinct movement resource.

## Two enforcement milestones

Vobořil's work in 2024–2025 produced two documented enforcement outcomes that stand among the first CEE cases in the AI Act enforcement literature on the public record.

**Prague airport facial recognition shutdown (August 2025).** Czech police had [deployed a facial recognition system at Václav Havel Airport from 2018](https://edri.org/our-work/czech-police-forced-to-turn-off-facial-recognition-cameras-at-the-prague-airport-thanks-to-the-ai-act/), converting facial contours into numerical bio-indexes for comparison against databases of wanted or missing individuals — with no explicit legal basis of the kind Czech law required. IuRe filed a formal complaint with the Czech Data Protection Authority in 2021; the DPA [inspection ran for nearly four years](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202510/czechia-turns-off-prague-airport-facial-recognition-system-amid-legal-violations) before confirming violations of personal data protection legislation. When the EU AI Act's biometric surveillance provisions entered force in February 2025 — requiring a specific judicial authorisation for each individual use, which Czech police had never obtained — the compounding legal pressure produced the system's shutdown in August 2025. IuRe subsequently called on Interior Minister Vít Rakušan to initiate legislative review of the Czech biometric surveillance framework. The case is one of two documented AI Act Article 5 enforcement actions achieving a police biometric system shutdown in 2025, alongside the equivalent [TASZ](../organizations/org-tasz.md) case in Hungary.

**Data retention: "The state has failed" (April 2025).** The [April 2025 Municipal Court ruling](https://edri.org/our-work/the-blanket-collection-of-metadata-on-communications-in-the-czech-republic-is-illegal-iuridicum-remedium-wins-data-retention-dispute/) that Czech data retention legislation directly contradicts European law ended a 20-year litigation arc. Vobořil had been lead counsel since the civil lawsuit phase, crowdfunding it in 2021 with journalist Jan Cibulka as plaintiff. The judge's published summary — "The state has failed" — captures the operative finding: blanket retention of communications metadata by Czech telecoms operators without a case-specific legal basis violates European legal requirements. The ruling additionally found that processing data under the retention regime itself constitutes individual harm, opening the path for financial compensation claims by other Czech citizens whose communications were retained. Vobořil's framing of the stakes in the 2021 crowdfunding named the documented misuse history: ["Data has been misused in the past, either to obtain information about contacts of influential people, to identify participants of anti-government demonstrations, or to identify journalistic sources."](https://edri.org/our-work/lure-data-retention-czech-republic/)

## Public-facing campaigns and output

**"Czech Republic is not China."** Following the Czech government's September 2024 approval of a [Ministry of Interior proposal to extend airport facial recognition authority](https://edri.org/our-work/biometric-surveillance-in-the-czech-republic-the-ministry-of-the-interior-is-trying-to-circumvent-the-artificial-intelligence-act/) — read by IuRe as an attempt to circumvent the AI Act's individual-authorisation requirement by authorising surveillance of "predefined categories of persons" broad enough to encompass any traveller — Vobořil's team launched an interactive public-education website making the biometric-surveillance argument legible to non-specialist Czech audiences. The campaign name directly counters the techno-nationalist frame that presents facial recognition as an aspirational governance upgrade; it positions the Czech Republic's democratic heritage as the operative argument against mass biometric surveillance adoption. IuRe's civil-society pressure during the legislative drafting process is credited with limiting the current text's scope to airport perimeters only, with the explanatory memorandum indicating a long-run interest in expansion that the campaign made politically costly.

**Big Brother Awards Czech Republic.** Since 2005 IuRe has [organised the annual Czech Big Brother Awards](https://edri.org/our-work/avoiding-regulation-of-biometric-surveillance-and-loyalty-applications-the-20th-big-brother-awards-took-place-in-the-czech-republic/), naming the most egregious surveillance and data-abuse actors of the preceding year. The ceremony is Vobořil's principal recurring public-mobilisation output — it translates specialist legal arguments into Czech public discourse at the register of a named, shamed institution rather than an abstract legal violation. The 20th edition in March 2025 selected awardees for biometric surveillance infrastructure and corporate loyalty-application data practices. Across its 20-year run, the Awards have named state ministries, police units, and corporate data programmes, creating a consistent annual accountability moment in Czech civil society.

**Right to Analogue and government advisory.** IuRe's [strategic litigation against mandatory cashless payment](https://edri.org/our-work/for-the-right-to-analog-iure-strengthens-the-digital-freedoms-program/) makes the right to analogue legally actionable — arguing that requiring digital-only service interfaces is a rights violation rather than a service-design choice. Vobořil's advisory role on the [Government Council for Human Rights' Committee for Human Rights and Modern Technology](https://www.ochranademokracie.cz/lide/jan-voboril) places this framing at the governmental advisory table, promoting analogue alternatives in public administration digitalisation. The advisory and litigation channels are complementary: the litigation makes the rights claim in court; the advisory role makes it in the governmental process that shapes the digitalisation policy the litigation then tests.

## Signature framings

Four formulations recur across Vobořil's public output and identify his specific register within the digital-rights movement.

**"The state has failed."** The judge's April 2025 formulation — the outcome Vobořil's litigation was designed to achieve — names the relationship between democratic state and citizen that the data-retention and surveillance files are organised around: a state that collects data wholesale on its own population without case-specific legal basis has defaulted on its fundamental obligation. That a court articulated this formulation, rather than an advocacy organisation, is the enforcement-outcome marker that distinguishes Vobořil's litigation model from advocacy framing alone.

**"Czech Republic is not China."** The campaign title names the democratic self-understanding the biometric-surveillance argument appeals to in Central European political discourse. The formulation presupposes that the Czech audience recognises "China" as naming a biometric mass-surveillance system incompatible with democratic self-government, and that identifying a Ministry of Interior proposal as moving toward that system is a politically salient argument in the Czech context. It is a framing choice — invoking comparative legitimacy rather than legal technicality — and illustrates Vobořil's movement-communication register alongside the legal register of the court filings.

**"Right to Analogue."** The naming of a *right* — rather than an access provision or accommodation requirement — is the conceptually significant move. Positioning the claim that digital-only state services impose an unjust burden on analogue users as a human-rights argument rather than a service-design argument carries standing in rights-bearing legal frameworks, travels to courts rather than only to policy discussions, and makes the harm legible in the vocabulary the digital-rights movement already operates in. It is the only framing in the corpus that directly addresses the digital exclusion problem from the affirmative-rights side.

**"Data has been misused in the past."** Vobořil's justification for the data-retention campaign consistently grounds the legal argument in documented historical misuse rather than abstract future risk: specific past instances of surveillance data being used against journalists' sources, against demonstration participants, against contacts of influential people. This historical-grounding move connects Czech digital-rights advocacy to the country's living memory of authoritarian data collection and makes the threat legible as a continuity rather than a novel technology concern — a framing specific to the Central European political context and largely absent from the Western European digital-rights voices in the corpus.

## Why this is a Voice entry

A Voice entry is created here, rather than additional structure on the [Person entry](../persons/person-jan-voboril.md), because Vobořil's public-facing contributions — two 2025 enforcement victories, the "Czech Republic is not China" campaign, the Big Brother Awards, the Right to Analogue framing, the government advisory channel — constitute a pattern of public output the corpus needs to track independently of IuRe's institutional record. The Voice entry is where the corpus stores what his output contributes to the movement: the demonstrated 20-year litigation model showing that sustained movement lawyering against state surveillance produces court wins at generational timescales; the AI Act enforcement precedent showing that a small civil-liberties NGO can force a national police biometric surveillance deployment offline through DPA complaint and legal pressure; and the public-framing moves — "Czech Republic is not China," "Right to Analogue," "The state has failed" — that translate the digital-rights argument into Central European political discourse at registers that move beyond the specialist legal community. This Voice entry provides the corpus's first Czech anchor, its second CEE individual Voice, and its first entry for the right-to-analogue framing.
