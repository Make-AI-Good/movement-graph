# Steven Renderos

[Steven Renderos](../persons/person-steven-renderos.md) is the Salvadoran-American Executive Director of [MediaJustice](../organizations/org-mediajustice.md) and the principal public voice on the corpus's US racial-justice-meets-tech-organizing register — a register that frames AI, platform, and data infrastructure not as regulatory compliance problems but as racial-justice and environmental-racism questions. He is tracked here as a Voice because his public-output practice — named-byline writing across national and progressive outlets, a co-hosted podcast applying organizer discipline to cultural commentary, campaign-based public commentary and legislative testimony, and executive media production — is the load-bearing object the corpus needs to track beyond the organizational-affiliation record in his [Person entry](../persons/person-steven-renderos.md).

## Voice anchor

Renderos's Voice closes the intersectional racial-justice tech-organizer slot in the corpus's US voices.

- **First intersectional racial-justice tech-organizer voice.** The corpus's US voices prior to this entry anchored the algorithmic-accountability research register, the labor-organizing register (creative-industry and gig-worker organizers), and the surveillance-litigation register. None occupied the register Renderos holds: a grassroots community organizer leading a national racial-justice organization whose central work is contesting how corporations and governments use media and technology against communities of colour, and whose public-output practice spans campaign-based commentary, progressive media, and cultural production. This register situates AI and data infrastructure inside a longer history of racial capitalism and media consolidation rather than treating them as novel technical governance problems.

- **Distinct from police-surveillance focus.** Several corpus entries approach US AI surveillance through law-enforcement facial recognition and predictive policing. Renderos's public register covers that overlapping terrain from a different angle: media justice and communicative infrastructure as the platform on which racial-justice organizing either runs or is suppressed. His public writing [connects net neutrality, prison phone rates, corporate social-media platforms, and hyperscale data centres](https://www.cockatoo.com/content/how-billionaires-captured-the-media-5cast-19-ft-steven-renderos) as a single system — "the data centers and the newsrooms are not separate projects" — a framing that ties AI infrastructure to narrative control and to organizers' access to communications infrastructure. This integrative lens is absent from the corpus's existing surveillance-focused voice anchors.

## Signature framings

Three arguments anchor Renderos's public-output register.

- **AI and data infrastructure as racial-justice and environmental-racism questions.** Renderos's central public argument is that the hyperscale data-centre build-out powering AI is not primarily a regulatory or technical governance problem but a continuation of a longer history of ["environmental racism, disinvestment, and union busting"](https://mediajustice.org/news/out-now-first-of-its-kind-comprehensive-regional-analysis-of-data-centers-in-the-south/) in communities of colour. His ["crisis of a lifetime" framing](https://mediajustice.org/news/out-now-first-of-its-kind-comprehensive-regional-analysis-of-data-centers-in-the-south/) names data centres as facilities consuming the electricity of entire cities, draining billions of gallons of drinking water, and siphoning public resources through subsidies while delivering few permanent local jobs — a racial-justice framing of AI infrastructure that positions communities most affected by the build-out as the appropriate agents of contestation, not regulators or researchers. The [Fight Data Centers campaign](../campaigns/camp-kairos-mediajustice-fight-data-centers-2024-ongoing.md) that [MediaJustice](../organizations/org-mediajustice.md) co-leads with [Kairos Fellowship](../organizations/org-kairos.md) is the organizational vehicle for this framing; Renderos is its public face.

- **Platform and media infrastructure as unified systems of corporate control.** In his Truthout essay ["Twitter's Collapse Is Bad News for Organizers – But Another Commons Is Possible"](https://truthout.org/articles/twitters-collapse-is-bad-news-for-organizers-but-another-commons-is-possible/) and his 5CAST appearance ["How Billionaires Captured the Media"](https://www.cockatoo.com/content/how-billionaires-captured-the-media-5cast-19-ft-steven-renderos), Renderos argues that tech billionaires' simultaneous consolidation of data infrastructure and media properties makes the data-centre fight and the newsroom fight the same fight. AI training data biases [operate at the infrastructure level](https://www.cockatoo.com/content/how-billionaires-captured-the-media-5cast-19-ft-steven-renderos), affecting millions; algorithmic amplification suppresses organizing voices in communities of colour; and organizers who build on corporate platforms build on extractive infrastructure that can be weaponized against them. The practical implication — building community-owned, decentralized communications infrastructure — connects [MediaJustice](../organizations/org-mediajustice.md)'s work on net neutrality and community media to its contemporary data-centre and AI organizing.

- **Article 19 freedom-to-communicate as the organizing frame, not US First Amendment doctrine.** Renderos's [framing of MediaJustice's mission](https://mediajustice.org/news/celebrating-mediajustices-14th-birthday-25-questions-with-executive-director-steven-renderos/) anchors on the United Nations Article 19 tradition — "real 'free' speech is about being able to receive information as much as it is about being able to express something" — rather than US constitutional free-speech doctrine. This frame locates the media-justice fight in an international civil-liberties context and addresses access to communications infrastructure as the structural question, not speech protection as such. It connects communities of colour's unequal access to broadband, unequal treatment on corporate social-media platforms, and unequal exposure to data-centre build-out as expressions of the same communicative-infrastructure inequality.

## Public output and venues

Renderos's public-output register runs through four overlapping channels.

- **Named-byline writing in national and progressive outlets.** His byline appears in the Washington Post, Los Angeles Times, The Guardian, [ColorLines](https://colorlines.com/authors/steven-renderos), Truthout, and TechCrunch. His [ColorLines writing](https://colorlines.com/authors/steven-renderos) covers government surveillance and its criminalization of communities of colour; his [Truthout essay on Twitter's collapse](https://truthout.org/articles/twitters-collapse-is-bad-news-for-organizers-but-another-commons-is-possible/) argues for community-owned communications infrastructure; his national-outlet commentary tracks [MediaJustice](../organizations/org-mediajustice.md)'s campaign-by-campaign public-facing work from prison phone rates and net neutrality through the Amazon Rekognition campaign and the data-centre organizing wave.

- **Bring Receipts podcast (2021–).** The [Bring Receipts podcast](https://www.bringreceiptspodcast.com/), co-hosted with Brandi Collins-Dexter, is a long-form debate format requiring hosts to argue unpopular opinions about pop culture with substantiated evidence — a format that applies organizer discipline (the receipts standard) to cultural and political commentary. Available on Spotify, Apple Podcasts, and iHeart, it connects Renderos's organizing formation to the broader Black cultural-production and progressive media landscape.

- **Campaign-based commentary and legislative testimony.** As the public face of [MediaJustice](../organizations/org-mediajustice.md)'s campaigns, Renderos's named commentary runs through panel appearances at the Facing Race National Conference on surveillance of communities of colour, interviews with the Benton Institute on broadband and media justice, and legislative testimony — including testimony on California net-neutrality bill SB 822 before the Privacy and Consumer Protection Committee in Sacramento. This is the register through which [MediaJustice](../organizations/org-mediajustice.md)'s campaign wins — [prison phone rates](https://mediajustice.org/staff/steven-renderos/), net neutrality, Amazon Rekognition restrictions for law enforcement, the Trump Twitter deplatforming — enter the public record as named-organizer-attribution work product.

- **Executive producer of Revolutionary Spirits.** His executive production of the Revolutionary Spirits podcast, a narrative history of Francisco Madero, connects his public-output practice to the community-radio and historical storytelling formation that runs through his 2010 co-founding of Radio Pocho on KFAI Minneapolis.

## Why this is a Voice entry

A Voice entry is created here because Renderos's public output — named-byline writing across national and progressive outlets, a co-hosted podcast, campaign-based media work, and executive media production — is the load-bearing object the corpus needs to track beyond his organizational affiliations. The [Person entry](../persons/person-steven-renderos.md) records his [MediaJustice](../organizations/org-mediajustice.md) executive directorship, board roles at Americans for Financial Reform and Upturn, and campaign associations; this Voice entry tracks the public-output practice through which he has made the case that AI and data infrastructure are racial-justice and environmental-racism questions, and that communities of colour organizing against the hyperscale build-out is the appropriate democratic response. The corpus's US voices slice had no entry occupying the intersectional racial-justice-tech-organizer register before this draft; this entry gives it its first anchor.
