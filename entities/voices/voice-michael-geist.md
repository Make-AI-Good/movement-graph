# Michael Geist

Michael Geist is [Canada Research Chair in Internet and E-Commerce Law at the University of Ottawa](https://www.michaelgeist.ca/about/) (faculty since 1998; Chair since 2003), founder of the [Canadian Internet Policy and Public Interest Clinic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Internet_Policy_and_Public_Interest_Clinic) (CIPPIC, 2003), and the most widely-read public commentator on Canadian digital rights, internet law, copyright, privacy, surveillance, and AI policy (see [Person entry](../persons/person-michael-geist.md)). His public output runs through four overlapping channels: daily commentary at michaelgeist.ca, the Law Bytes podcast, syndicated columns in the Globe and Mail and Toronto Star, and direct parliamentary testimony — making him the dominant public voice shaping Canadian government and public understanding of digital-policy choices across copyright reform, privacy law, lawful access, and AI regulation. He holds the [Electronic Frontier Foundation](../organizations/org-electronic-frontier-foundation.md) Advisory Board seat and has been recognised with the EFF Pioneer Award (2008), the Canadian Journalists for Freedom of Expression Vox Libera Award (2018), and an appointment to the Order of Ontario (2018).

## Voice anchor

Geist's Voice closes the Canada-anchored digital-rights-law commentator slot. The corpus had no Canada-rooted Voice entry before this draft — a gap given that Canada is one of the few common-law jurisdictions running its own parliamentary digital-policy track (the 2012 Copyright Modernization Act, the Online Streaming Act / Bill C-11, the Online News Act / Bill C-18, PIPEDA reform / Bill C-27 and AIDA, and now the Lawful Access Act / Bill C-22) that has attracted sustained international attention as a test-bed for digital regulation. The "Geist position" — academically grounded legal analysis, publicly accessible commentary, consistent civil-liberties orientation, and willingness to name government overreach — has been the primary reference point through which Canadian journalists, legislators, and civil society organisations understand what is at stake in each of those legislative cycles.

Three distinctions from adjacent Voices already in the corpus:

- **Distinct from transnational advocacy voices (e.g. [Nighat Dad](../persons/person-nighat-dad.md) for Pakistan-anchored internet freedom).** Dad and similar transnational voices operate through international NGOs (Access Now, Article 19, EFF itself), making advocacy across multiple jurisdictions. Geist operates from within a single national legal system — as its most prominent legal commentator and as CIPPIC's founder — and his authority derives from deep expertise in the specific statutes, regulatory structures, and parliamentary processes of Canada's digital-policy track. The two registers are complementary; neither duplicates the other.

- **Distinct from US digital-rights voices in the corpus (e.g. [Virginia Eubanks](../persons/person-virginia-eubanks.md), [Safiya Noble](../persons/person-safiya-noble.md)).** Eubanks and Noble anchor the US organizer-academic and information-science-academic registers respectively, with US legislation, US courts, and US public policy as their primary terrain. Geist's terrain is explicitly Canadian — his standing before Canadian parliamentary committees, the influence of his research on Canadian Supreme Court copyright decisions, and his framing of Canadian public debate make him non-substitutable by any US voice, however prominent.

- **Distinct from [Apar Gupta](../persons/person-apar-gupta.md)'s Indian digital-rights-law Voice.** Gupta anchors the India-specific internet-freedom-law register through Internet Freedom Foundation and Indian legislative and constitutional proceedings. The structural parallel is exact — both are national-jurisdiction-specific legal voices — but their national legal contexts, legislative tracks, and political environments have no overlap. Both are needed; each is the other's complement in a global-coverage sense.

## Signature framings

Three framings anchor Geist's public-output register and have carried the most weight in shaping Canadian digital-policy vocabulary.

- **"Balanced copyright."** The framing Geist installed through the 2005 edited collection *In the Public Interest* and the 2010 collection [*From "Radical Extremism" to "Balanced Copyright"*](https://www.michaelgeist.ca/ccda/) (Irwin Law, 2010): that Canadian copyright law should be structured around a genuine balance between creator rights, public interest, education, innovation, and access — not tilted by lobbying toward the maximalist rights-expansion the US DMCA represented, nor toward zero-rights libertarian positions at the other extreme. The framing was legally consequential: it entered the vocabulary of the parliamentary debates on the Copyright Modernization Act (Bill C-11, 2012), where the balanced-copyright position was cited in committee hearings, and its influence is traceable in the fair-dealing provisions and user-rights sections of the resulting statute. Multiple Supreme Court of Canada copyright decisions invoked the user-rights frame that Geist's "balanced copyright" discourse helped make legible in Canadian legal culture.

- **"Total policy disaster" / evidence-based critique of digital legislation.** Geist has applied a consistent analytical template to each of the Trudeau government's major digital-regulation bills: identify the gap between stated legislative objectives and actual structural effects; document where the regulatory design produces outcomes opposite to what was promised; name who benefits from the design versus who loses. The most cited instance is his characterisation of the Online News Act (Bill C-18) as ["a total policy disaster"](https://thehub.ca/2023-09-22-michael-geist-why-the-online-news-act-has-been-a-total-policy-disaster/) — he argued the bill's link-tax design gave both Google and Meta structural incentives to exit the Canadian news ecosystem entirely rather than pay, and that the government had made no contingency plan for that outcome. Google's deal (which he argued gutted the bill's intent) and Meta's exit confirmed the structural analysis. The same template applied to Bill C-11 (the Online Streaming Act), where he documented how the government publicly denied that user-generated content was regulated by the bill while internal legal advice confirmed it was — an episode he called "gaslighting" — and to AIDA (the AI provisions of Bill C-27), where he called for the government to ["hit the regenerate button"](https://www.michaelgeist.ca/2023/04/why-the-government-should-hit-the-regenerate-button-on-its-ai-bill/) and start the AI regulatory process over with adequate public consultation.

- **"Lawful access two-headed surveillance monster."** Geist's most recent major framing, applied to Bill C-22 (2026): that the bill simultaneously creates a metadata-retention regime requiring telecommunications providers to retain records on virtually all Canadians' digital communications, and creates conditions for police to demand access to encrypted communications — two distinct privacy threats that compound each other rather than being alternatives. His analysis documented that the RCMP had publicly confirmed they wanted the bill to provide access to encrypted communications, and that the bill's metadata-retention requirements were [expansive enough to constitute "a comprehensive surveillance map of virtually every Canadian."](https://www.michaelgeist.ca/2026/05/the-lawful-access-two-headed-surveillance-monster-how-bill-c-22-went-off-the-rails/) The framing attracted international notice: he documented that U.S. Congressional leaders had formally warned the Canadian government that the bill threatened US national security and economic interests, and that VPN services (Signal, Windscribe, NordVPN) had threatened to exit the Canadian market if it passed.

## Public output and venues

Geist's public-output practice runs through four channels.

- **michaelgeist.ca.** His primary publication surface — daily or near-daily commentary on Canadian digital policy since approximately 2003, now also available via Substack syndication at mgeist.substack.com. The site carries both short commentary posts and longer analytical pieces; together with the Law Bytes podcast it constitutes the most sustained single-author public-commentary operation on Canadian digital policy. Coverage spans copyright, privacy, surveillance, net neutrality, AI regulation, online content regulation, and the intersection of Canadian digital policy with US and international developments.

- **Law Bytes podcast.** [271+ episodes as of June 2026](https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/law-bytes/id1455262794), positioned among Canada's top technology podcasts, covering digital policy through conversations with legal experts, policymakers, and technologists. Episodes track Canadian legislative developments in real time — the podcast covered all major phases of Bills C-11, C-18, C-27, and C-22 as they moved through parliament. Available on Apple Podcasts, Spotify, and through his website and Substack.

- **Newspaper columns.** Regular syndicated columns in the Globe and Mail and Toronto Star — Canada's two national print institutions with the broadest reach among federal policymakers and the educated public — providing the primary channel through which Geist's analysis reaches audiences who do not follow the technical policy world. The columns have run for over twenty years, making his framing of digital-rights questions a persistent presence in the national media conversation.

- **Parliamentary testimony.** Geist is among the most frequent expert witnesses before Canadian parliamentary standing committees on digital policy — appearing on Heritage, Industry/Science/Technology, Public Safety, and Access to Information/Privacy/Ethics committees across multiple parliaments on copyright reform, net neutrality, AI regulation, lawful access, and privacy law. This gives his public-output register a direct line into the legislative deliberation where the outcomes of these debates are formally determined.

## Why this is a Voice entry

A Voice entry is created here because Geist's named public output — the sustained daily commentary practice at michaelgeist.ca, the Law Bytes podcast, the newspaper columns, and the signature framings that have shaped Canadian parliamentary and public vocabulary on digital rights — is the load-bearing object the corpus needs to track, and it is not fully captured by the affiliation-and-recognition structure of the linked [Person entry](../persons/person-michael-geist.md). The Person entry records his institutional positions, affiliations, and awards; this Voice entry tracks the specific public-output practice that makes his voice consequential in the make-AI-good movement's Canadian strand. Geist's work anchors what the corpus does not otherwise have: a systematic Canada-anchored voice applying legal-academic analysis and public-commentary practice to the full range of digital-rights questions — copyright, privacy, surveillance, AI regulation — that the movement is working on, from within the national legislative system where those questions are resolved.
