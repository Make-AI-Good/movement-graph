# Panoptykon Foundation

The Panoptykon Foundation is the [Warsaw-headquartered Polish digital-rights non-profit](https://en.panoptykon.org/about-us/what-is-panoptykon) founded in 2009 and now the leading Eastern European civil-society anchor on surveillance, consumer-protection, algorithmic accountability, and AI policy. Its [self-description](https://en.panoptykon.org/) — that it is "keeping an eye on the state and tech corporations" and "fights for laws to protect people's freedom and privacy" — captures the dual front the foundation has worked since its founding: the state-surveillance side (mass-surveillance legislation, data retention, biometric identification, Polish-court constitutional litigation) and the platform-accountability side (the European recommender-systems and surveillance-advertising files, the EU Digital Services Act, and the EU AI Act). The organisation's name is drawn from Jeremy Bentham's *panopticon* and Michel Foucault's reading of it as the architectural emblem of pervasive surveillance — an intellectual inheritance the foundation has translated into a sustained, evidence-based critique of mass-surveillance and platform infrastructures in Poland and at the EU level.

## Founding and structure

Panoptykon was [established on 17 April 2009](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panoptykon_Foundation) by four co-founders — Katarzyna Szymielewicz, Małgorzata Szumańska, Piotr Drobek, and Krystian Legierski — in response to the rapid build-out of state and commercial tracking infrastructure in post-accession Poland and the absence of a Polish civil-society organisation focused on the resulting human-rights questions. The foundation's [first two years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panoptykon_Foundation) were anchored in a monthly public-seminar series on surveillance (2009–2011), early campaigns against compulsory personalisation of the Warsaw city transit card, opposition to a proposed Polish "List Of Forbidden Websites and Services", the 2010 mobilisation against the EU's planned passenger-name records arrangements, and the 2010–2012 European mobilisation against the Anti-Counterfeiting Trade Agreement (ACTA) that culminated in protests in more than 200 European cities and the European Parliament's July 2012 rejection of the agreement.

Panoptykon is governed by a small [three-person board](https://en.panoptykon.org/about-us/management) — Katarzyna Szymielewicz as President, Wojciech Klicki as Vice President, and Anna Obem — and a [four-person Council](https://en.panoptykon.org/about-us/management) chaired by Piotr Drobek and including Monika Bełdowska, Witold Klaus, and Krystian Legierski. Szymielewicz, a co-founder, has anchored the foundation's public-facing leadership since 2009 and is the most-quoted Panoptykon spokesperson in European digital-rights coverage of biometric surveillance, the AI Act, and platform accountability.

## Programme structure

Panoptykon's work is organised across three connected fronts that the foundation has built up over its sixteen-year operating history. The first is the state-surveillance and constitutional-rights front: long-running [Polish-court constitutional litigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panoptykon_Foundation) against mass-surveillance legislation, opposition to data-retention obligations, contestation of biometric identification deployments, and policy work on national-security and police-access-to-data files. The second is the platform-accountability and algorithmic-systems front: research and advocacy on recommender systems, surveillance advertising, content-moderation algorithms, and consumer-protection in algorithmic decision-making, anchored on the *[Algorithms of Trauma](https://en.panoptykon.org/algorithms-of-trauma)* report track and the recurring [Vlop!](https://en.panoptykon.org/about-us/what-is-panoptykon), [SIN vs Facebook](https://en.panoptykon.org/about-us/what-is-panoptykon), [Dealing with Disinformation](https://en.panoptykon.org/about-us/what-is-panoptykon), and [Who (really) targets you?](https://en.panoptykon.org/about-us/what-is-panoptykon) projects. The third is the European-policy front: sustained co-drafting of joint civil-society statements at every stage of the EU AI Act and Digital Services Act processes, signatory co-leadership in the Reclaim Your Face European Citizens' Initiative, and membership in the [European Digital Rights (EDRi)](../organizations/org-edri.md) network through which Panoptykon brings its national work into the European institutional venues.

## Algorithms of Trauma

Panoptykon's signature research-publication line on platform algorithms is the *[Algorithms of Trauma](https://en.panoptykon.org/algorithms-of-trauma)* report, [published on 28 September 2021](https://en.panoptykon.org/algorithms-of-trauma) by authors Dorota Głowacka and Karolina Iwańska — Iwańska is now [Digital Rights Advisor at the European Center for Not-for-Profit Law and an EDRi board member](https://edri.org/about-us/board/), a measure of the report's standing in the European field. The methodology was a sustained empirical experiment on a single Facebook user's newsfeed: more than 2,000 ads were tracked across two months, with the finding that approximately one in five concerned health topics and that Facebook had assigned the test subject 21 health-related interest categories — including oncology and genetic disorders — that the platform's systems regenerated rapidly when the user disabled specific ad preferences. The report's substantive argument was that users do not have meaningful control over algorithmic content delivery and that the appropriate remedy is regulatory — specifically, that the then-pending [Digital Services Act](https://en.panoptykon.org/algorithms-of-trauma) should restrict platforms' use of algorithmic predictions for advertising and content recommendations. The report became one of the most-cited European civil-society research artefacts on surveillance advertising during the 2021–2022 DSA negotiations and established the participatory-experiment register Panoptykon has continued to use across subsequent platform-accountability work.

## EDRi membership and EU AI Act civil-society coordination

Panoptykon is a long-standing [member organisation](https://edri.org/take-action/our-network/) of [European Digital Rights (EDRi)](../organizations/org-edri.md), the Brussels-headquartered pan-European network of civil-society organisations that is the principal European convening table on AI, biometric surveillance, content moderation, data protection, and platform regulation. Through that membership Panoptykon has been one of a small stable nucleus of European digital-rights organisations co-drafting EDRi-coordinated joint civil-society statements through the entire 2021–2024 [EU AI Act legislative cycle](../campaigns/camp-edri-eu-ai-act-fundamental-rights-coalition-2021-2024.md).

The foundational document is the [30 November 2021 joint civil-society statement *An EU Artificial Intelligence Act for Fundamental Rights*](https://algorithmwatch.org/en/eu-artificial-intelligence-act-for-fundamental-rights/), signed at publication by 115 organisations and co-drafted by [EDRi](../organizations/org-edri.md), [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md), the Panoptykon Foundation, epicenter.works, [AlgorithmWatch](../organizations/org-algorithmwatch.md), the European Disability Forum, Bits of Freedom, Fair Trials, PICUM, and ANEC — see the in-corpus [foundational coalition manifesto entry](../publications/pub-civil-society-statement-eu-ai-act.md). The statement established the seven-prohibition position list (social scoring; remote biometric identification in publicly accessible spaces; emotion recognition; discriminatory biometric categorisation; AI physiognomy; predictive-policing systems; profiling and risk-assessment in migration contexts) and the architectural-reforms structure (impact-based risk methodology; mandatory user-side fundamental-rights impact assessments; public registers of high-risk deployments; rights and redress including a right to explanation; accessibility throughout the AI lifecycle; environmental-impact disclosure; future-proof standards with civil-society participation; comprehensive rights-centred enforcement) that the coalition then carried into the trilogue endgame.

Panoptykon remained inside the EDRi-coordinated coalition through the [12 July 2023 trilogue-stage joint statement *Civil society calls on the EU to protect people's rights in the AI Act 'trilogue' negotiations'*](https://edri.org/our-work/civil-society-statement-eu-protect-peoples-rights-in-the-ai-act-trilogue-negotiations/), signed by 150 organisations and co-drafted with EDRi, [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md), [AlgorithmWatch](../organizations/org-algorithmwatch.md), Amnesty International, Bits of Freedom, the European Federation of National Trade Unions, the European Center for Not-for-Profit Law, the European Disability Forum, Fair Trials, Homo Digitalis, the Irish Council for Civil Liberties, and PICUM. The trilogue statement organised the coalition's position around three pillars — accountability frameworks built on fundamental-rights impact assessments and public registers; restrictions on harmful surveillance by law-enforcement and migration authorities including bans on remote biometric identification and predictive policing; and the elimination of regulatory loopholes that would have allowed providers to self-classify high-risk systems as lower-risk — and remained the operating civil-society reference for the AI Act endgame and the implementation phase that followed adoption in 2024.

## Reclaim Your Face

Panoptykon was one of the [twelve founding civil-society organisations](https://reclaimyourface.eu/) in the EDRi-coordinated [Reclaim Your Face](https://reclaimyourface.eu/) coalition launched in October 2020 against biometric mass surveillance in European public space, alongside EDRi as coordinator, [AlgorithmWatch](../organizations/org-algorithmwatch.md) and AlgorithmWatch Switzerland, [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md), ARTICLE 19, Bits of Freedom, Privacy International, Homo Digitalis, the Hermes Center, IT-Pol Denmark, Liberties, and [La Quadrature du Net](../organizations/org-la-quadrature-du-net.md). The coalition's central organising vehicle was a [European Citizens' Initiative](https://reclaimyourface.eu/goodbye-eci-hello-ai-act-negotiations/) — the EU's only formal mechanism for citizens to compel the European Commission to consider a legislative proposal — registered on 7 January 2021, with signature collection running from 17 February 2021 until 1 August 2022 (extended from the original 16 February 2022 deadline due to COVID-19). The ECI ultimately gathered nearly 80,000 verified signatures, short of the one-million threshold for compulsory Commission response, but the coalition's framing is that the words "ban" and "remote biometric identification" entered the European Commission's April 2021 AI Act proposal text as a direct consequence of the mobilisation. The [in-corpus message entry](../messages/msg-ban-biometric-mass-surveillance.md) on the "Reclaim our public space. Ban biometric mass surveillance!" framing names Panoptykon among the founding twelve.

## Funding and independence

Panoptykon's [funding-transparency page](https://en.panoptykon.org/about-us/funding) names the foundation's current institutional funders as the [Open Society Foundations](../funders/fund-open-society-foundations.md), the [Sigrid Rausing Trust](../funders/fund-sigrid-rausing-trust.md), and the [European AI & Society Fund](../funders/fund-european-ai-society-fund.md), alongside the Norwegian Funds (Active Citizens National Fund), the European Commission's CERV programme, the Digital Freedom Fund, [EDRi](../organizations/org-edri.md) sub-grants, and Civitates. Corporate donors include Samsung, Google, and the Avast Foundation; individual donations are received via the Polish 1.5%-tax-deduction mechanism and direct contributions. The foundation's published funding-ethics policy — "we only accept funding if it does not limit our independence" — pairs with an annual financial-reporting standard the foundation has maintained since 2012, publishing detailed grant-source breakdowns, profit-and-loss statements, balance sheets, and itemised corporate-donation disclosures.

## Posture in the movement

Panoptykon's distinctive contribution to the make-AI-good movement is the work of carrying Polish-court and Polish-public-debate organising into the European institutional venues. As the leading Polish civil-society organisation on surveillance and algorithmic accountability, and one of the longest-operating Eastern European member organisations of [EDRi](../organizations/org-edri.md), the foundation is the principal Eastern European node in the European civil-society coalitions that have shaped the EU AI Act, the Digital Services Act, and the European biometric-mass-surveillance debate. Its empirical-research register — the *Algorithms of Trauma* methodology of single-user newsfeed tracking, the *Who (really) targets you?* investigative work on online advertising, the SIN vs Facebook strategic-litigation track on content moderation — supplies a model of how a national digital-rights NGO can translate platform-accountability evidence into European-level legislative argument; and the four-co-founder, three-person-board organisational form is itself an example of how a small Eastern European civil-society institution sustains a sixteen-year operating record on the surveillance and platform-regulation fronts that the broader European field has come to depend on. In the corpus's terms it is the load-bearing Eastern European anchor on the EU AI Act civil-society coalition track, the Polish/Eastern European counterpart to [AlgorithmWatch](../organizations/org-algorithmwatch.md) on the Continental European algorithmic-accountability axis, and one of the small group of organisations whose names recur at every stage of the European civil-society record on biometric mass surveillance from Reclaim Your Face to the AI Act's adoption.
