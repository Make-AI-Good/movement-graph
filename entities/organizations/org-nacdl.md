# National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers

The National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers (NACDL) is the principal professional association for criminal defense attorneys in the United States. Founded in 1958 in Chicago and headquartered in Washington, D.C., NACDL represents approximately 9,000 direct members — private criminal defense lawyers, public defenders, military defense counsel, law professors, and defense counsel in international criminal tribunals — and a further roughly 31,000 members through 90 state, local, and international affiliate organizations. The organization publishes *The Champion*, a flagship magazine covering criminal law, and aims to serve as a leader in identifying and reforming systemic flaws in the criminal legal system.

Since 2018, NACDL has built and maintained the primary national infrastructure for challenging the deployment of algorithmic surveillance tools in criminal proceedings. Its Fourth Amendment Center provides free litigation support to defense attorneys confronting cases involving facial recognition, predictive policing software, risk assessment instruments, and a widening range of AI-generated evidentiary products. An AI Task Force, established in 2023, extends that work to the broader questions generative AI poses for criminal practice.

## Fourth Amendment Center

NACDL [launched the Fourth Amendment Center in April 2018](https://www.nacdl.org/Landing/FourthAmendmentCenter) with the stated goal of building a robust legal infrastructure to challenge outdated constitutional doctrines in the digital age. The Center is directed by Jumana Musa and operates under the NACDL Foundation for Criminal Justice.

The Center offers direct case assistance to defense attorneys at any stage of proceedings: motion drafting, suppression hearing preparation, appellate strategy, brief writing, and oral argument support. Its network of expert consultants — drawn from computer science, civil liberties law, and forensic science — is available via a single contact point (4AC@nacdl.org). The Center also runs nationwide training programs for defense lawyers who need to understand the technical and constitutional dimensions of surveillance evidence.

Areas of focus include device and account searches, reverse search warrants, body camera evidence, digital location tracking, government hacking, and the full range of AI-driven identification and prediction systems the Center characterizes as "technologies that upend traditional rules governing law enforcement investigations and are often introduced without transparency or notice." Standing coalition partners for Fourth Amendment Center initiatives include the [American Civil Liberties Union](../organizations/org-aclu.md) and the [Electronic Frontier Foundation](../organizations/org-electronic-frontier-foundation.md).

## AI Task Force

In 2023 NACDL established a Task Force on Artificial Intelligence, [funded through the NACDL Foundation for Criminal Justice](https://www.nacdl.org/Landing/ai), to examine how emerging AI technologies affect criminal defense practice and the rights of the accused. The Task Force addresses the full stack of AI deployment in criminal proceedings: law enforcement use of large language models to draft police reports; AI-driven bail and risk assessment tools; facial recognition for suspect identification; and, increasingly, the use of AI by prosecutors to model conviction likelihood from historical caseload data.

The Task Force has produced training resources and *The Champion* articles addressing the reliability risks of generative AI (hallucination, prompt-engineering vulnerabilities) and the ethical obligations of defense counsel when AI-generated outputs appear as evidence. In June 2024 then-President Michael P. Heiskell [framed the dual-use challenge squarely](https://www.nacdl.org/Article/June2024-FromthePresidentGenerativeAIforCriminalDe): "Lawyers must keep their defense hats on at all times to help identify the biases this technology may support and to implement creative safeguards to ensure the presumption of innocence is maintained in our courts."

## Algorithmic risk assessment tools

NACDL has maintained a sustained advocacy and litigation position against the use of proprietary algorithmic risk assessment instruments at bail determinations and sentencing. These tools — of which over 200 are deployed across U.S. and international jurisdictions — assign numerical risk scores drawn from demographic and historical data, and their methodologies are typically protected as trade secrets, [preventing defense scrutiny of how scores are calculated or factors weighted](https://www.nacdl.org/Landing/ai).

The organization ran a major webinar in April 2021 — ["Systemic Racism and Inherent Biases in AI, Machine Learning, and Beyond"](https://www.nacdl.org/Content/Challenging-the-Use-of-Secret-Algorithms-and-T-(2)) — convening Rashida Richardson of Rutgers Law School, mathematician and algorithmic auditor Cathy O'Neil, and Harvard doctoral researcher Cierra Robson to examine how algorithmic decision-making systems "reproduce social hierarchies, amplify discriminatory outcomes," and are concentrated on over-policed marginalized communities. The session gave defense attorneys specific litigation strategies for challenging government use of automated decision systems on due-process and equal-protection grounds.

## Facial recognition

NACDL characterizes facial recognition technology as "unregulated, untested, and flawed" and [has built a dedicated litigation toolkit](https://www.nacdl.org/Content/Facial-Recognition) for defense attorneys confronting facial-recognition identification evidence. Resources include a Facial Recognition Primer, a Motion to Suppress Identification Testimony, a Discovery "Wish List" for requesting system accuracy data and demographic error-rate breakdowns, and a model affidavit outlining due process and Brady disclosure concerns.

NACDL's Fourth Amendment Center filed an amicus brief in *Willie Allen Lynch v. State of Florida* (2019) raising systemic concerns about the accuracy and discriminatory impact of facial recognition in criminal identification. In March 2024, Clare Garvie of the Fourth Amendment Center [delivered testimony before the U.S. Civil Rights Commission](https://www.nacdl.org/getattachment/0725bfd1-6567-4d81-b980-f1bf150f349c/garvie_testimony_civil-rights-commission_face-recognition.pdf) on the civil rights implications of federal use of facial recognition technology — marking the Center's entry into formal federal oversight proceedings on the technology.

A favorable New Jersey appellate decision in *State v. Arteaga* has established that defense counsel are entitled to access information about the facial recognition systems used against their clients, confirming the legal viability of the Center's discovery strategy.

## Predictive policing

NACDL frames [predictive policing as a form of surveillance](https://www.nacdl.org/Content/Predictive-Policing) deploying technologies to target "at-risk" individuals and communities using methods that it argues entrench historical racial bias. The Fourth Amendment Center has promoted scholarship that grounds this critique empirically: "Dirty Data, Bad Predictions" (2019) demonstrates how civil rights violations in police databases produce structurally compromised algorithmic outputs, while "Garbage In, Gospel Out" (2021) — authored by Lee, Musa, and Pinard — documents how data-driven systems reproduce and legitimate historical racism. The Center provides litigation assistance in cases challenging Chicago's gang database, LAPD surveillance programs, and the New Orleans predictive policing investigation.

## Coalition work and training

In October 2024 the Fourth Amendment Center co-hosted the [Artificial Justice CLE program](https://www.nacdl.org/Content/ArtificialJusticeCLE) with Georgetown Law's Center on Privacy & Technology. The event addressed AI-generated police reports, DHS DNA database expansion, youth surveillance in schools, jail-based data mining systems, probabilistic genotyping software, and automatic license plate readers — reflecting the breadth of AI evidence now requiring constitutional challenge. Additional academic partners included Columbia University's Center for Justice and the University of Washington's Allen School of Computer Science & Engineering.

Domestically, NACDL works with the [Algorithmic Justice League](../organizations/org-algorithmic-justice-league.md) and Berkeley Center for Law and Technology on shared research into algorithmic bias in criminal identification. The organization has also filed amicus briefs opposing legislation — including the Cooper Davis Act and EARN IT Act — that it argues would create new government data-collection channels with implications for criminal defense.
