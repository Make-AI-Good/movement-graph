# Our Data Bodies

Our Data Bodies (ODB) is a US multi-city participatory action research and community organizing project [founded in 2015](https://www.odbproject.org/our-data-bodies-project/) to examine how data collection and algorithmic surveillance systems affect the lives of marginalized communities — specifically communities of color, low-income households, and communities touched by the criminal legal system. ODB conducted approximately [135 in-depth community interviews](https://www.odbproject.org/our-data-bodies-project/) across three sites — Charlotte (North Carolina), Detroit (Michigan), and Los Angeles (California) — each led by a local grassroots organization operating from within the affected communities.

## Structure and partner organizations

ODB is [organized through Allied Media Projects](https://alliedmedia.org/projects/our-data-bodies-odb) (Detroit). Three core partner organizations anchor the city sites:

- **Detroit Community Technology Project (DCTP)** — a sponsored project of Allied Media Projects based in Detroit's majority-Black neighborhoods, led by [Tawana Petty](../persons/person-tawana-petty.md) as Data Justice Program Director. DCTP had built community-driven approaches to technology and data governance in Detroit prior to ODB; the project extended that work into the data-justice research frame.
- **Center for Community Transitions** (Charlotte, NC) — an organization serving people returning from incarceration, led by Tamika Lewis. ODB's Charlotte site focused on how data surveillance systems specifically affect re-entry populations.
- **[Stop LAPD Spying Coalition](../local-groups/lg-stop-lapd-spying-coalition.md)** (Los Angeles, CA) — a sponsored project of Los Angeles Community Action Network, led by Mariella Saba. The Coalition had developed a community-based counter-surveillance practice in LA before ODB's formation; its "Power Not Paranoia" organizing framework is embedded in the Digital Defense Playbook.

[Virginia Eubanks](../persons/person-virginia-eubanks.md) co-founded ODB and served as a research collaborator across all three sites; her concurrent work on *Automating Inequality* (2018) shares community and analytical foundations with ODB's inquiry. [Dr. Seeta Peña Gangadharan](https://www.lse.ac.uk/media-and-communications/research/research-projects/our-data-bodies) (Department of Media and Communications, London School of Economics) served as principal academic researcher and co-author on ODB's publications, providing the project's institutional research anchor in the UK.

## Research approach

ODB's methodology is explicitly participatory: community members were not passive research subjects but co-researchers who shaped the questions and received outputs in formats they could use in their organizing. The project documented how residents navigate data collection across intersecting domains: [public benefit and welfare systems, housing, policing, and criminal-legal re-entry](https://www.odbproject.org/our-data-bodies-project/). The research traced how algorithmic and database systems encode historical inequities into the daily encounters of low-income and over-policed communities — restricting people's ability to meet basic needs by encoding suspicion and precarity into automated eligibility determinations, background-check systems, and predictive-policing tools.

The primary funding source was the Digital Trust Foundation, a charitable fund established from the settlement of a class-action lawsuit over Facebook's "Beacon" program, with assets directed toward digital privacy and public-interest work. ODB received Digital Trust Foundation grants from [approximately 2015 through 2019](https://www.odbproject.org/our-data-bodies-project/).

## Digital Defense Playbook

The project's flagship public output is the *[Digital Defense Playbook: Community Power Tools for Reclaiming Data](https://www.odbproject.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/03/ODB_DDP_HighRes_Single.pdf)* (2019), authored by Gangadharan, Petty, Lewis, and Saba and [published through Allied Media Projects](https://researchonline.lse.ac.uk/100314/). The Playbook is a popular-education workbook — approximately 200 pages of facilitation guides, community stories, and tip sheets on specific data-threat scenarios — designed for use by community organizers, not as a privacy-tips document for individuals. Its organizing premise: data threats facing marginalized communities are political problems requiring collective response, not hygiene lapses requiring individual behavior change. The "Power Not Paranoia" section, drawn from Stop LAPD Spying Coalition's counter-surveillance practice, is the Playbook's theoretical and tactical spine. Spanish-language and stand-alone tip-sheet versions were distributed through allied organizing networks.

## Significance

ODB arrived at a moment — mid-2010s — when dominant framings for algorithmic harm were either regulatory (GDPR, US consumer-privacy bills) or academic (fairness audits, bias benchmarks). ODB produced a different research tradition: closer to the popular-education and organizing lineages of welfare-rights, disability-rights, and tenant movements, and explicitly shaped by communities most affected by the systems under study. Its three-city comparative design and focus on the interlocking of data systems with criminal-legal exposure, housing, and poverty shaped subsequent data-justice research and organizing in the US and contributed to the broader framing in which [*Automating Inequality*](../publications/pub-automating-inequality.md) and [*Race After Technology*](../publications/pub-race-after-technology.md) found their audience.
