# Association for Progressive Communications (APC)

The **Association for Progressive Communications** is the [73-member international civil-society network](https://www.apc.org/en/about/who-we-are) of digital-rights, internet-governance, and feminist-tech organisations that has, since its [May 1990 founding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_for_Progressive_Communications) by seven national networks from five continents, functioned as the principal Global-South-rooted civil-society interlocutor in the international internet- and digital-technology-governance system. APC's [self-description as "a human rights and feminist network"](https://www.apc.org/en/about/who-we-are) working "to strengthen collective organising towards building a transformative movement to ensure that the internet and digital technologies enable social, gender and environmental justice for all people" places it in this corpus as the senior pan-regional network that anchors the make-AI-good movement's Global-South-led civil-society wing: the convening table at which national digital-rights organisations in Asia, Africa, Latin America, and Eastern Europe coordinate on AI, biometric surveillance, internet shutdowns, online gender-based violence, and platform accountability, and the principal civil-society conduit through which Global-South positions are taken into the UN Human Rights Council, the International Telecommunication Union, UNESCO, the Internet Governance Forum, and the multilateral AI-governance processes that have proliferated since 2023.

## Founding and structure

APC's [organisational pre-history](https://www.apc.org/en/history-0) runs from 1982 onward, when independent national non-profit computer networks built to serve activists, peace and environmental NGOs began appearing in North America and Europe. The [Institute for Global Communications](https://www.apc.org/en/history-0) (IGC, USA — itself the umbrella for PeaceNet and EcoNet) and [GreenNet](https://www.apc.org/en/history-0) (UK) began exchanging electronic conferences across the Atlantic in 1987, and by late 1989 NordNet (Sweden), Web Networks (Canada), IBASE (Brazil), Nicarao (Nicaragua), and Pegasus (Australia) had joined the trans-national exchange. The [formal founding meeting](https://www.apc.org/en/founding-apc-coincidences-and-logical-steps-global-civil-society-networking) of APC was convened in IGC's office in San Francisco in May 1990 by these seven networks, as a coordinating body for what was at that point one of the only operating international civil-society electronic-communications networks in the world. APC was [granted UN ECOSOC consultative status](https://www.apc.org/en/history-0) in June 1995, anchoring the multilateral-engagement track that the organisation has carried since.

APC is [registered as a California non-profit public benefit corporation](https://www.apc.org/en/about/who-we-are), incorporated on 5 May 1998 and tax-exempt under section 501(c)(3) of the US Internal Revenue Code, with its principal office in South Africa — a deliberate Global-South placement of the network's secretariat that distinguishes APC structurally from the Brussels- or Washington-anchored international civil-society networks active on the same files. The strategic priorities are set every four years by the membership through an assembly process, and the network operates a [four-programme structure](https://www.apc.org/en/about/who-we-are) — the Local Access Programme on community networks and connectivity, the Social and Environmental Justice Programme that houses the AI work, the Women's Rights Programme, and the Safety for Voices initiative on digital-security support for human-rights defenders.

## Membership and governance

The APC network currently comprises [73 organisational members](https://www.apc.org/en/about/who-we-are) across five continents, with the membership weighted toward Asia, Africa, and Latin America in line with APC's deliberate Global-South orientation. In the corpus's terms, four current APC members carry their own entity entries: [EngageMedia](../organizations/org-engagemedia.md), which joined the APC network in January 2010 and co-convenes the Coconet / Digital Rights Asia-Pacific Assembly camp lineage with APC across the Asia-Pacific; [7amleh — the Arab Center for Social Media Advancement](../organizations/org-7amleh.md), the Palestinian and Arab civil-society digital-rights organisation that APC [describes on its member roster](https://www.apc.org/en/member/7amleh-arab-center-social-media-advancement) as "a non-profit organisation aimed at enabling Palestinian and Arab civil society to effectively utilise the tools of digital advocacy"; [Derechos Digitales](../organizations/org-derechos-digitales.md), the Santiago-headquartered pan-Latin-American digital-rights organisation; and the [Internet Freedom Foundation](../organizations/org-internet-freedom-foundation.md), the Indian digital-rights organisation that [joined the APC network on 3 April 2024](https://www.apc.org/en/news/internet-freedom-foundation-joins-apc-network-digital-rights-are-indispensable-and-not) with IFF Communications Lead Purvai Dwivedi framing the accession in terms of an alignment with APC's "core values of solidarity and community". The network is governed by a member-elected Board of Directors that sets organisational policy between the four-yearly strategic-priorities assemblies; long-serving Executive Director [Anriette Esterhuysen](../persons/person-anriette-esterhuysen.md), who held the post for seventeen years (June 2000–April 2017) and was [recognised with the Electronic Frontier Foundation's 2015 Pioneer Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_for_Progressive_Communications) for her digital-rights leadership, remains the most internationally-recognised figure associated with the APC secretariat.

## AI and human rights

APC's published AI work is anchored on three named instruments. The first is the network's [policy explainer on Artificial Intelligence](https://www.apc.org/en/pubs/apc-policy-explainer-artificial-intelligence), originally published on 20 October 2021 and most recently updated on 15 November 2025, which sets out APC's seven-category framing of the AI harm landscape: privacy intrusion through expanded data extraction; discrimination reinforcement through algorithms trained on biased historical data; labour disruption through what the explainer calls "heteromation" — the reorganisation of human-machine labour division, with particular impact on the Global South; censorship through automated content moderation; kinetic harm through military AI making lethal decisions; liability gaps when systems cause harm with unclear responsibility; and the absence of transparency, accountability, and community participation in AI deployment. On the regulatory side the explainer endorses the OECD AI Principles as a baseline but argues these "must be operationalised through binding measures" — moratoria on high-risk AI including biometric recognition, mandatory human-rights due diligence for AI development, transparency policies with independent auditing, expanded explainability requirements, and the active participation of vulnerable communities in AI decision-making — and names the network's multilateral-engagement footprint as the UN Human Rights Council and Special Procedures, the International Telecommunication Union, the Council of Europe, the OECD, UNESCO, the Internet Governance Forum and its regional initiatives, and the Freedom Online Coalition.

The second is the [2019 Global Information Society Watch edition on Artificial Intelligence](https://www.apc.org/en/news/what-have-we-learned-revisiting-giswatch-edition-artificial-intelligence), co-published by APC and [ARTICLE 19](https://www.apc.org/en/news/what-have-we-learned-revisiting-giswatch-edition-artificial-intelligence) under the organising framing "Artificial intelligence: Human rights, social justice and development". The edition assembled 40 country reports, 3 regional reports, and 8 thematic reports written predominantly by Global-South civil-society researchers in APC's network and partner organisations, and remains the corpus's most comprehensive single multi-country civil-society survey of AI deployment and rights harms in the Global South — country-report examples include the Digital Empowerment Foundation in India on caste, gender, and religious bias in AI deployments in education; Cooperativa Sulá Batsú in Costa Rica on citizen-organisation participation in healthcare AI decision-making; Bytes for All Bangladesh on automation's impact on garment-worker labour; and Nodo TAU in Argentina on union responses to AI in working conditions. APC's own retrospective frames the edition's central lesson as the observation that "conversations on AI have been driven largely by Western and Global North predictions" while "the very real effects of AI are also more diverse" across jurisdictions, and that the AI-and-human-rights field requires the kind of incorporation of ground-level Global-South perspectives that the GISWatch edition assembled.

The third is the [5 February 2025 joint civil-society statement to the AI Action Summit](https://www.apc.org/en/pubs/joint-statement-civil-society-ai-action-summit), coordinated by APC and signed by approximately 150 civil-society and rights organisations including [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md), Amnesty International, Greenpeace, and a range of regional digital-rights organisations. The statement is organised around five demands that connect AI governance to climate and environmental justice — phase-out of fossil-fuel reliance in AI data centres with a switch to 100% locally-produced renewable energy and a halt to AI contracts with oil-and-gas companies; computing within planetary limits via energy caps on data centres, prioritisation of critical sectors like schools and hospitals, and grid-aware computing; responsible supply chains achieving 100% renewable energy by 2030 with elimination of carbon offsets and protection of mining communities from environmental and human-rights harms; equitable participation of impacted communities in decision-making through participatory impact assessments and protection of climate activists and journalists from criminalisation; and transparency through disclosure of energy and water usage across AI lifecycles, reporting of data-centre locations and environmental impacts, and tracking of enabled emissions from AI applications. The statement's central argument that "AI can never be a 'climate solution' if it runs on fossil fuels" anchors APC as one of the principal civil-society coordinating organisations on the rapidly-developing AI-and-environmental-justice track in the post-2024 AI-governance landscape.

## Take Back the Tech! and the feminist-internet line

APC's feminist-tech work is the longest single line of feminist-internet-rights advocacy in the global digital-rights field. The network's signature campaign is [Take Back the Tech!](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_for_Progressive_Communications), an annual 16 Days of Activism Against Gender-Based Violence campaign (25 November to 10 December) launched in 2006 and now run as a coordinated multi-country mobilisation through APC's Women's Rights Programme and member organisations. The campaign frames gender-based violence as inseparable from the technologies through which it is increasingly mediated, and supplies the field's most-cited civil-society register for what the campaign calls technology-facilitated gender-based violence. The Women's Rights Programme also produces the Feminist Internet principles, the Gender Evaluation Methodology (GEM) toolkit for integrating gender analysis into ICT initiatives, and the network's [policy explainer on cybercrime and gender](https://www.apc.org/en/pubs/joint-statement-civil-society-ai-action-summit) tracking the worldwide trend toward cybercrime-law misuse against women and LGBTQIA+ organising. The feminist-internet line is the principal organising frame through which APC connects to the corpus's feminist-AI work — the [Derechos Digitales](../organizations/org-derechos-digitales.md) and [Coding Rights](../organizations/org-coding-rights.md) anchored 2024 GISWatch "Latin America in a Glimpse" edition on community-based feminist AI sits inside this lineage — and is the source of much of APC's distinctiveness in international digital-rights forums dominated by Brussels- and Washington-anchored peer organisations.

## Funding and independence

APC's [funders page](https://www.apc.org/en/about/people/funders) names the network's current support as drawn from a mix of foundations, bilateral aid agencies, and inter-governmental bodies: the Dutch Ministry of Foreign Affairs (DGIS), the European Union's European Instrument for Democracy and Human Rights (EIDHR), the [Ford Foundation](../funders/fund-ford-foundation.md), the Global Fund for Women, the International Development Research Centre (IDRC), the Internet Society (ISOC), [Luminate](../funders/fund-luminate.md), the Oak Foundation, the Swedish International Development Cooperation Agency (Sida), the UK Foreign Commonwealth and Development Office, and the UK Government's Digital Access Programme (formerly DFID). The Sida partnership underwrites APC's sub-granting programme, which since 2016 has disbursed over USD 1.2 million to dozens of network and partner organisations and which functions as one of the principal Global-South-anchored sub-granting vehicles in the international digital-rights field. APC's funding history — captured on the same page across "Early support 1993-2002", "Support 2003-2020", and current periods — also names the Open Society Foundations and the MacArthur Foundation among historic supporters, reflecting the long-running alignment between APC's Global-South-network model and the philanthropic field's funding interest in supporting non-Northern-headquartered civil-society infrastructure.

## Posture in the movement

APC's distinctive contribution to the make-AI-good movement is the Global-South-rooted, feminist, and multilateral-engagement-oriented coordination function. Where [European Digital Rights (EDRi)](../organizations/org-edri.md) is the convening secretariat for the European civil-society wing of digital-rights advocacy and works the EU institutions, APC is the convening secretariat for the global-network-of-networks model of civil-society coordination and works the UN bodies — the Human Rights Council and Special Procedures, UNESCO, the ITU, the IGF, and the AI-governance instruments that have proliferated through the UN Advisory Body on AI and the AI Action Summit cycle. The network's 73-member roster, weighted toward Asia, Africa, and Latin America, gives APC a distinctive capacity to assemble Global-South civil-society positions on AI, biometric surveillance, online gender-based violence, content moderation, and internet governance into single statements and reports at a scale that no Northern-headquartered network can match — the 2019 GISWatch AI edition and the 2025 AI Action Summit joint statement are the corpus's two clearest expressions of this. In the corpus's terms APC is the senior pan-regional Global-South-rooted civil-society network in the make-AI-good movement, the principal multilateral-engagement vehicle through which national digital-rights organisations in the corpus's other entries — [EngageMedia](../organizations/org-engagemedia.md) in the Asia-Pacific, [7amleh](../organizations/org-7amleh.md) in Palestine and Israel, [Derechos Digitales](../organizations/org-derechos-digitales.md) across Latin America, and the [Internet Freedom Foundation](../organizations/org-internet-freedom-foundation.md) in India — convert their national and regional work into UN-level and multilateral-AI-governance positions, and the organisational anchor for the feminist-internet, environmental-justice-AI, and Global-South-AI-governance lines that distinguish this wing of the movement from the Brussels-and-Washington-centric AI-policy field.
