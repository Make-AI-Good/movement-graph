# Investor Alliance for Human Rights

The Investor Alliance for Human Rights is a collective action platform for responsible investment [grounded in respect for people's fundamental rights](https://investorsforhumanrights.org/about), launched in 2018 by the [Interfaith Center on Corporate Responsibility](../organizations/org-iccr.md). Its membership of more than 250 institutional investors — asset managers, trade union funds, public pension funds, foundations, endowments, faith-based organizations, and family funds across 21 countries, representing more than US$20 trillion in assets under management — constitutes the organized capital-leverage capacity of the investor community on the global business and human rights agenda, with a dedicated program targeting the digital rights and AI accountability of major technology companies.

## Founding and structure

The Alliance was convened by ICCR and launched on May 24, 2018, at an event [hosted by Bloomberg LP](https://investorsforhumanrights.org/news/investor-alliance-human-rights-launch) in New York. At launch, 101 institutional investors representing $2 trillion in assets had already joined. John Ruggie, the Harvard Kennedy School professor who developed the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, delivered the keynote address, situating the Alliance within the UNGPs framework as an institutional expression of the investor responsibility to respect human rights in their portfolio companies.

The Alliance operates as a membership initiative staffed through ICCR at 475 Riverside Drive, New York — the Interchurch Center — the same headquarters from which ICCR's own programmes run. This co-location reflects the founding relationship: the Alliance was created as a vehicle to extend ICCR's shareholder-engagement model beyond faith-based capital to the broader institutional-investor community. The current Director is Dr. Rebecca DeWinter-Schmitt; David Schilling, a long-serving ICCR programme director who served as interim director after a 2020 leadership transition, remains as Senior Advisor. The Alliance has grown from its 101 founding investors to more than 250, with assets represented growing from $2 trillion at launch to more than $20 trillion.

## Membership and governance

The Alliance's membership spans the full spectrum of institutional capital: asset management firms, public pension funds, trade union funds, university endowments, foundations, faith-based investment pools, and family funds. Members span 21 countries and include some of the world's largest institutional investors alongside specialist responsible-investment and faith-based funds. The breadth of the membership is the mechanism — it allows the Alliance to present a cross-investor consensus on corporate human rights expectations, making company engagement harder to dismiss as the position of a narrow ideological constituency.

The Alliance structures its work through issue-specific programmes and working groups. Members participate in collective corporate-engagement initiatives, [receive capacity-building resources](https://investorsforhumanrights.org/about) and practical tools for human rights integration at each step of the investment lifecycle, and coordinate policy advocacy with governments and standard-setting bodies.

## Technology and Human Rights programme

The Alliance's Digital Rights and AI Accountability working group has been active since 2019, coordinating a collective investor engagement initiative with the largest global technology and telecommunications companies. The engagement is [grounded in benchmarked data published by Ranking Digital Rights](https://investorsforhumanrights.org/digital-rights-and-ai-accountability-working-group) and focuses on digital rights performance — covering rights to privacy, freedom of expression, and non-discrimination online — as those rights are affected by AI development and deployment in products and services.

The working group meets bi-monthly and draws in investors, civil society stakeholders, and digital rights experts. Named civil society collaborators include [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md), [Amnesty International](../organizations/org-amnesty-international.md), and [European Digital Rights (EDRi)](../organizations/org-edri.md), whose research and findings inform the investor engagements. More than 170 institutional investors representing $9.2 trillion in assets have [collectively endorsed investor expectations of tech companies on digital rights](https://investorsforhumanrights.org/investor-actions-ict-engagement), and a sub-group of over 25 investors participate directly in focused corporate engagements with 20 major technology and telecommunications companies.

Corporate engagements press companies across several AI-specific accountability demands: meaningful human rights impact assessments for AI systems in development and deployment; transparency about efforts to prevent and remedy adverse impacts; visible remediation mechanisms for those harmed by discriminatory outputs; and identification of human rights risks linked to operations, including bias in training data. Companies are [encouraged to adopt and implement human rights due diligence policies](https://investorsforhumanrights.org/information-and-communication-technologies-ict-engagement) that include mechanisms enabling remedy for victims of abuses.

## Policy advocacy

The Alliance has pursued parallel policy advocacy at the regulatory level. Sixty-five investors managing more than $8.7 trillion joined the Alliance's support for the EU Digital Services Act, which prohibited targeted advertising based on sensitive personal data (including religious and political opinions, sexual preferences, and health data) and [banned advertising directed at children](https://investorsforhumanrights.org/investor-actions-ict-engagement). One hundred and forty-nine investors representing $1.66 trillion signed the Alliance's statement on the EU Artificial Intelligence Act calling for mandatory human rights due diligence, enforceable safeguards for high-risk AI applications, restrictions on algorithmic surveillance in advertising, and meaningful stakeholder and rightsholder participation in AI governance decisions.

The Alliance [coordinates these advocacy positions with civil society](https://investorsforhumanrights.org/about) to ensure engagements are informed by the lived experience of impacted communities, providing the investor-side leverage complement to civil-society litigation and advocacy campaigns directed at the same regulatory and corporate targets.
