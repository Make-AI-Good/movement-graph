# World Council of Churches

The World Council of Churches (WCC) is an international ecumenical fellowship of approximately 352 Christian denominations, representing over 580 million Christians across Protestant, Anglican, Orthodox, and other traditions from more than 110 countries. Founded in Amsterdam in 1948, the WCC operates from the Ecumenical Centre in Geneva. It is the broadest and most inclusive multilateral ecumenical body in Christendom and the primary institutional vehicle through which mainline Protestant, Anglican, and Eastern and Oriental Orthodox churches coordinate theological work, social witness, and public advocacy. The Roman Catholic Church is not a member but maintains formal dialogue through joint commissions. Since 2023, the WCC has produced a formal statement on AI regulation and has been the primary ecumenical convening infrastructure for faith-based advocacy on autonomous weapons and digital rights, mobilizing its member churches as a global advocacy constituency on both fronts.

## Founding and structure

The WCC was constituted at the First Assembly in Amsterdam on 23 August 1948, uniting 147 member churches from 44 countries after two decades of preparatory work by the ecumenical movement. Its mandate is threefold: to call member churches toward visible unity, to advance common witness and service, and to foster renewal of the church in unity, worship, mission, and service. The organization is governed by an Assembly meeting every eight years, with day-to-day authority delegated to a Central Committee (meeting annually) and an Executive Committee. The Central Committee is the body that issues formal WCC statements — including the 2023 AI statement — and acts as the ecumenical parliament between Assembly gatherings. Rev. Prof. Dr. Jerry Pillay, a South African Reformed theologian, [became the ninth General Secretary on 1 January 2023](https://www.oikoumene.org/news/rev-prof-dr-jerry-pillay-installed-as-ninth-wcc-general-secretary), replacing Chilean theologian Olav Fykse Tveit. Pillay's tenure has coincided with the WCC's most active engagement on AI and digital-rights issues.

## AI statement and regulatory advocacy

On 26 June 2023, the WCC Central Committee meeting in Geneva [adopted a formal Statement on the Unregulated Development of Artificial Intelligence](https://www.oikoumene.org/resources/documents/statement-on-the-unregulated-development-of-artificial-intelligence), making the WCC one of the earliest inter-denominational Christian bodies to produce a binding institutional position on generative AI. The statement expressed concern that AI development, without adequate oversight, risked centering profit over human wellbeing, enabling plagiarism in place of theological insight, threatening Indigenous knowledge systems, and substituting digital facsimiles for genuine human pastoral relationships.

The Central Committee appealed to governments to establish "strong and binding regulatory regimes based on the precautionary principle," encouraged legal frameworks holding AI developers "legally responsible for resulting harms," urged governments to negotiate "a common international normative standard" for AI regulation, and requested that the UN Secretary-General prioritize AI regulation in the New Agenda for Peace. Member churches were called upon to advocate with their governments for swift regulatory action and to engage theological institutions in reflection on AI's ethical implications.

## Autonomous weapons and Stop Killer Robots

The WCC's engagement on autonomous weapons predates its 2023 AI statement. In November 2019, the WCC Executive Committee issued a formal Minute on Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems declaring such systems "fundamentally objectionable and unconscionable" and calling for a pre-emptive ban on their further development and deployment. The WCC subsequently joined the [Stop Killer Robots](org-stop-killer-robots.md) coalition, bringing the ecumenical fellowship's weight behind the campaign for a new binding international treaty on autonomous weapons.

In May 2023, the WCC joined an interfaith coalition in [delivering a statement to the UN](https://www.oikoumene.org/news/wcc-joins-other-organizations-in-interfaith-statement-supporting-campaign-to-stop-killer-robots) calling on states to establish and protect "meaningful human control over the use of force" and ban autonomous systems that target humans without human oversight. The statement framed the issue theologically: "Digital dehumanisation is fundamentally repulsive to all people who share a belief in the inalienable dignity of the human person." To support member-church mobilization on the issue, the WCC published [*Killer Robots: A Campaign Guide for Churches*](https://www.oikoumene.org/sites/default/files/2021-12/KillerRobots_WebFin.pdf), a practical resource written by Jennifer Philpot-Nissen for congregations and church bodies seeking to engage the killer robots debate.

[PAX](org-pax.md), the Dutch peacebuilding NGO and founding member of Stop Killer Robots, traces its lineage to Pax Christi Netherlands and the Interkerkelijk Vredesberaad — faith-rooted predecessor organizations — giving the Stop Killer Robots coalition a shared faith-institutional substrate with the WCC's autonomous-weapons advocacy.

In September 2024, the WCC [co-sponsored a side event at the UN General Assembly](https://www.oikoumene.org/news/wcc-co-sponsored-side-event-at-un-general-assembly-tackles-faith-ethics-and-ai) addressing faith, ethics, and AI. Co-organizers included Religions for Peace, ACT Alliance, the Lutheran World Federation, Finn Church Aid, the Network for Religious & Traditional Peacemakers, and the Bahá'í International Community. The panel addressed autonomous weapons and the moral responsibility of states and developers to ensure human control over lethal force, positioning faith communities as a standing constituency within the multilateral disarmament conversation.

## Theological consultation and communique (2025)

In August–September 2025, the WCC convened a theological consultation in Pocheon-si, South Korea, drawing 45 church leaders, theologians, and academics from six continents. Participants included representatives from five major Christian communions: the World Communion of Reformed Churches, the Lutheran World Federation, the World Methodist Council, the Council for World Mission, and the United Society Partners in the Gospel. The consultation [produced a theological communique](https://www.oikoumene.org/news/faith-communities-issue-urgent-call-to-transform-ai-from-profit-to-life) anchoring its position in the doctrine of imago Dei: "Scripture proclaims that humanity is created in the image and likeness of God. This dignity is irreducible. It cannot be replaced by data, commodified by algorithms, or rendered disposable by automation." The communique called on governments to establish ethical AI governance with transparency and accountability, ensure data sovereignty, and protect displaced workers; and on the tech industry to develop AI for the common good, include marginalized voices in system design, and provide technologies to the Global South at reduced cost.

## Digital rights symposium (2026)

In April 2026, the WCC organized a two-day symposium in Berlin titled "Our Common Future: Advocating for Digital Rights and AI Accountability." The event [brought together participants](https://www.oikoumene.org/news/diverse-voices-speak-for-digital-rights-ai-accountability) from across the WCC's global network — including the Arab Reporters for Investigative Journalism, St Paul's University, and the World Association for Christian Communication — to address algorithmic racial bias, AI transparency requirements, equitable AI governance, cybersecurity for civil-society organizations, and digital access for populations in Asia still outside broadband reach. WCC Vice Moderator Rev. Merlyn Hyde Riley and WCC Programme Executive Dr. Masiiwa Gunda facilitated sessions alongside digital-rights specialists from the Middle East/North Africa and researchers in digital justice.
