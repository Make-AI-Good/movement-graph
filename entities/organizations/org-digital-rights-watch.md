# Digital Rights Watch

Digital Rights Watch is Australia's dedicated civil society organisation for digital rights — a nationally operating, [ACNC-registered charity](https://www.acnc.gov.au/charity/charities/a2e9d675-3aaf-e811-a963-000d3ad24077/profile) incorporated in Victoria (ABN 90 509 129 914) that [works to "ensure that Australians are equipped, empowered and enabled to uphold their digital rights."](https://digitalrightswatch.org.au/about/) Founded in 2016 in Melbourne in direct response to Australia's mandatory telecommunications data retention scheme, the organisation operates across four programmatic areas — Privacy & Security; Personal Information & Data Rights; Power, Access & Technology; and Journalism & Information Flows — running advocacy campaigns, government submissions, public education, and direct parliamentary engagement. It is the closest Australian analogue to the civil society digital-rights field's large European poles (Article 19, EDRi) and the organisation most engaged with Australia's federal AI-governance process among civil society bodies in this corpus.

## Founding and structure

Digital Rights Watch was [established in 2016](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Rights_Watch) at a meeting of representatives from Australian digital and human rights organisations, activists, political advisers, technology consultants, and academics who judged that Australia lacked a dedicated independent voice capable of countering the surveillance-expansive policy trends the data retention scheme represented. The immediate catalyst was the Data Retention Act — the mandatory telecommunications data retention scheme passed in 2015 requiring telecommunications providers to retain customer metadata for two years and make it accessible to law enforcement without a warrant, a regime digital rights advocates characterised as warrantless mass surveillance of the Australian population.

Tim Singleton Norton served as founding Chair until [mid-2020](https://digitalrightswatch.org.au/2020/07/01/a-farewell-from-the-founding-chair/), a tenure that saw the organisation launch [*The State of Digital Rights*](../publications/pub-digital-rights-watch-state-of-digital-rights.md) annual report, run the 2017 "Get a VPN Day" national day of action, and lead the national campaign against facial recognition. Lizzie O'Shea — human rights lawyer, co-founder, and recipient of [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md)'s 2019 Human Rights Hero Award for her work campaigning against Australia's encryption laws — has served as Chair since. Tom Sulston, co-founder and a software consultant with twenty-five years of industry experience, serves as both Deputy Chair and Head of Policy. Lucie Krahulcova, a digital rights advocate who previously worked at [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md) specialising in surveillance, encryption, and data retention, serves as a board member; she had previously held the Executive Director role. The remaining board members include Lilly Ryan (Treasurer, digital security consultant and speaker), Piotr Debowski (Secretary, privacy specialist with experience across Victorian, Australian, and EU privacy-law frameworks), Professor Mark Andrejevic (Monash University School of Media, Film, and Journalism, specialising in data mining and online monitoring), and Caitlin McGrane (Postdoctoral Research Fellow at RMIT, researching gender, identity, and technology). Current campaigning staff include Dale Clisby, whose stated focus is "harms brought about by the rapid adoption of AI systems," and Lucinda Thorpe, Digital Campaigner.

The organisation operates as a member-run body with an annually elected board, consistent with its stated [principle of accountability](https://digitalrightswatch.org.au/about/) to members and the Australian public.

## Core campaigns

**Data retention and surveillance (2016–2017).** The organisation's founding purpose crystallised immediately in advocacy against the operational implementation of the mandatory data retention scheme. In August 2016 DRW [raised privacy concerns about the Australian national census](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Rights_Watch) as the Australian Bureau of Statistics moved to link census data with administrative records. In April 2017 it coordinated a national day of action — promoted as "Get a VPN Day" — encouraging Australians to use virtual private networks to protect their metadata from the retention regime. In August 2017 it [ran an event at the Melbourne Writers Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Rights_Watch) demonstrating the metadata footprint of former Australian Human Rights Commission President Gillian Triggs, translating abstract surveillance claims into a concrete public demonstration. In September 2017 it [partnered with Privacy International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Rights_Watch) on intelligence-sharing transparency, situating the Australian data retention regime inside the broader Five Eyes intelligence-sharing architecture.

**Encryption and the Assistance and Access Bill (2018–2019).** DRW's most prominent advocacy campaign of the early period was the sustained opposition to the Assistance and Access Bill 2018 (AABill), which proposed to compel device manufacturers and service providers to build access capabilities for law enforcement into encrypted communications systems. DRW helped build and anchor a broad coalition — the Alliance for a Safe and Secure Internet — that [brought together major Australian telecoms (Telstra, Optus), US technology platforms (Amazon, Twitter, Facebook, Google), Internet Australia, Amnesty International, and the Human Rights Law Centre](https://advox.globalvoices.org/2019/06/24/australias-digital-rights-advocates-and-tech-sector-push-back-against-law-undermining-encryption/) against the legislation, arguing that mandated encryption backdoors would compromise the security of all users. The AABill was [passed in December 2018](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Rights_Watch). In 2019 DRW partnered with Electronic Frontiers Australia on the "Save Australian Technology" campaign, continuing the push for repeal or reform of the legislation.

**State of Digital Rights (2018–ongoing).** DRW's flagship annual publication [*The State of Digital Rights*](../publications/pub-digital-rights-watch-state-of-digital-rights.md), launched in May 2018 with endorsement from seven civil society organisations, is the organisation's primary vehicle for documenting how digital rights are experienced in Australia and making annual policy recommendations to government. Subsequent editions — covering surveillance expansion, COVID-19 contact-tracing overreach, platform power, algorithmic management, data-broker markets, and facial surveillance — have tracked the evolution of Australia's digital rights landscape through the terms of successive governments. The 2022 edition documented DRW's engagement footprint: 12+ roundtables, 10 government submissions, 8 community events, and over 100 media appearances in the reporting year.

**Facial recognition (2018–ongoing).** DRW has run a sustained national campaign opposing the deployment of facial recognition by Australian state and federal agencies, arguing that Australian law provides no adequate consent framework for the state's use of biometric data at scale.

## AI governance

Digital Rights Watch's engagement with AI governance follows the same structural-critique register as its earlier work on data retention and encryption: rather than advocating for specific technical standards or risk tiers, the organisation has consistently argued that the design of systems — the data-extraction and biometric-commodification architecture of commercial AI — is itself the upstream problem that surface-level regulation cannot resolve without structural intervention. Chair Lizzie O'Shea, in her [July 2024 testimony](../events/event-digital-rights-watch-australia-senate-ai-committee-2024-07.md) to the Senate Select Committee on Adopting Artificial Intelligence, put the position directly to the committee: "It is clear that our laws are decades out of date." DRW's submission and oral evidence called for enhanced biometric data rights (voice, likeness, biometric identifiers), tighter consent mechanisms for personal information in AI systems, and — most structurally — interventions that would "limit the use of personal information and seek to put limits on data-extracted business models." The Senate committee's November 2024 final report cited DRW alongside the Human Rights Law Centre, unions, and industrial lawyers in its call for Australia to shift from voluntary AI governance toward mandatory regulatory guardrails for high-risk AI uses.

Within DRW's current staff structure, Dale Clisby's campaigning focus is explicitly on "harms brought about by the rapid adoption of AI systems" — indicating that AI-specific advocacy has graduated from a policy-submissions function to an active campaigning line.

## Funding

Digital Rights Watch is philanthropically and community-funded; its [public donation transparency register](https://digitalrightswatch.org.au/about/donation-transparency/) documents named donors and grants by financial year. The major recent philanthropic lines include the [Minderoo Foundation](../funders/fund-minderoo-foundation.md) ($301,500 in 2024-25), Mannifera ($80,000 annually in 2023-24 and 2024-25), and the [Internet Society Foundation](../funders/fund-internet-society-foundation.md) ($120,000 in 2020-21 and $125,624 in 2021-22 for internet economy research). The [Oak Foundation](../funders/fund-oak-foundation.md) has provided recurring support ($21,595 in 2022-23; $30,000 in 2024-25). Corporate donors have included Apple, DuckDuckGo, Google Australia, and Amazon Australia in various years. Earlier grants came from the Australian Communities Foundation, Centre for Australian Progress, and [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md) ($3,300 in 2018-19). Government funding is minimal and episodic — a Department of Social Services grant of $2,000 appears in 2018-19; the organisation's independence from state revenue is consistent with its advocacy stance.

## Position in the corpus

Digital Rights Watch is the Australian civil society organisation in the corpus with the longest institutional history on digital rights and the deepest formal engagement with Australia's AI-governance legislative process. It sits in a distinct position from [Reset.Tech Australia](../organizations/org-reset-australia.md) — which is primarily a platform-regulation and Big-Tech accountability research organisation — in that DRW's founding register is human-rights and civil-liberties advocacy on surveillance and privacy infrastructure, with AI governance entering as a natural extension of the data-rights and biometric-protection arguments it has been making since 2016. The two organisations together constitute the principal Australian civil society poles on digital rights and AI governance in this corpus. DRW's presence here also anchors [*The State of Digital Rights*](../publications/pub-digital-rights-watch-state-of-digital-rights.md) annual report and the [July 2024 Senate AI committee testimony](../events/event-digital-rights-watch-australia-senate-ai-committee-2024-07.md) as connected outputs of a single organisational identity rather than standalone artefacts.
