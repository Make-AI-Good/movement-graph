# Royal College of Nursing

The Royal College of Nursing (RCN) is the United Kingdom's principal professional association and trade union for nursing staff, representing [over half a million](https://www.rcn.org.uk/About-us) registered nurses, midwives, health care practitioners, and nursing students. Described as the world's largest nursing union, it operates simultaneously as a professional standards body and an employment-relations union — a dual mandate that positions it distinctively within the make-AI-good movement: when the RCN takes a position on artificial intelligence in healthcare, it speaks for both the clinical authority of the nursing profession and the labour rights of the NHS's largest occupational group.

## Founding and structure

The RCN was [founded on 27 March 1916](https://www.rcn.org.uk/about-us/our-history) as the College of Nursing Ltd — a wartime initiative by Dame Sarah Swift (matron of Guy's Hospital), Arthur Stanley, Rachael Cox-Davies (matron of the Royal Free Hospital), and Alicia Lloyd-Still (matron of St. Thomas Hospital) — with 34 founding members. It grew rapidly: 2,553 members by year's end, 13,047 by 1919. The organisation received its [Royal Charter in June 1928](https://www.rcn.org.uk/about-us/our-history), adopted the "Royal" prefix in 1939 under George VI, and [registered as a trade union in 1976](https://www.rcn.org.uk/about-us/our-history). Health care assistants were admitted to full membership in 2011 following an 81% member vote.

The RCN's governance is [explicitly democratic](https://www.rcn.org.uk/About-us/our-structure): 17 elected Council members set strategy and ensure accountability to members; the Executive Team implements; country and regional boards across 12 countries and regions advise their Directors. Approximately 2,000 workplace representatives are embedded across NHS trusts and private healthcare settings. The Professional Nursing Committee and Trade Union Committee serve as the two principal decision-making bodies, accountable to Council. In July 2024, [Professor Nicola Ranger](https://www.rcn.org.uk/news-and-events/news/uk-rcn-appoints-permanent-general-secretary-and-chief-executive-100724) — previously chief nurse and executive director of midwifery at King's College Hospital NHS Foundation Trust — was appointed permanent General Secretary and Chief Executive, succeeding Professor Pat Cullen who stood down to contest a parliamentary seat.

## AI posture and professional demands

The RCN began articulating a sustained position on artificial intelligence in healthcare through Congress debates and official statements from 2024 onward. At [RCN Congress 2024 in Newport](https://www.rcn.org.uk/congress/congress-events/the-evolution-of-ai-in-health-care-2024), the Digital Nursing Forum convened a formal matter-for-discussion titled "The Evolution of AI in Health Care", examining machine learning applications, the Moravec Paradox, AI ethics, and — centrally — nursing's role in shaping AI policy and patient advocacy. The position that emerged was direct: nurses must be active participants in AI development and governance, not passive recipients of technology deployed by administrators and vendors.

On [21 October 2025](https://www.rcn.org.uk/news-and-events/Press-Releases/ai-has-great-potential-and-must-enhance-personal-nursing-care-but-not-replace-it), the RCN published a formal statement in response to NHS AI administrative trials. UK Head of Nursing Practice Stephen Jones articulated the core framing: "The goal of AI must be freeing up nursing time, allowing staff to deliver more care where it's needed, rather than spending time on outdated administrative tasks." The statement set out four demands: nursing professionals must be involved in AI development and implementation; adequate staff training time must be provided; clear lines of accountability and patient safety measures must be in place; and any AI adoption must be accompanied by new investment in growing the nursing workforce — not substituted for it. General Secretary Ranger reinforced the constraint: "ministers shouldn't be completely seduced by it alone." The formulation — AI as a force-multiplier for nursing capacity rather than a headcount-reduction mechanism — distinguishes the RCN's posture from both uncritical techno-enthusiasm and blanket opposition.

At [RCN Congress 2025 in Liverpool](https://www.rcn.org.uk/congress/congress-events/artificial-intelligence-in-nurse-education-2025), AI featured in two separate formal sessions: the Dorset Branch's discussion on AI in nurse education (covering generative AI curriculum integration, AI-powered simulation tools, academic integrity concerns, and digital literacy requirements) and the Education Forum's session on AI as a learning tool addressing benefits, challenges, and ethics. Digital and data themes ran throughout the Congress, with delegates raising concerns about the profession's capacity to evaluate AI tools critically in the absence of robust data transparency.

## Joint action on NHS workforce planning

The sharpest single expression of the RCN's AI stance came on [11 June 2026](https://www.rcn.org.uk/news-and-events/Press-Releases/nhs-workforce-plan-must-be-paused-over-concerns-it-fails-to-safely-grow-staff-numbers), when the RCN joined a coalition of five other health unions and professional bodies — the British Medical Association, Society for Acute Medicine, Royal College of Emergency Medicine, Queen's Institute of Community Nursing, and Unite — in a joint open letter to Health Secretary James Murray MP demanding that the government pause publication of its revised ten-year NHS workforce plan. Together, the signatories represented over one million NHS staff.

The letter's core objection was that the revised plan's assumptions about AI and digital technology "risk overstating near-term productivity gains without sufficient evidence or safeguards" — and that reliance on technology as a productivity lever would substitute for investment in the staffing numbers that patient safety requires. The revised plan was reported to reduce the nursing growth target from the 170,000–190,000 additional nurses proposed in 2023 to approximately 50,000. Ranger's statement — that the plan would "erroneously rely on AI and digital technologies to boost productivity and avoid the need for investment in the workforce" — placed the RCN's AI concern squarely in the frame of labour rights and safe staffing: the objection is not to AI itself but to AI being deployed as a political rationale for understaffing. The coalition demanded the plan be paused and rewritten with fully funded staffing modelling, robust AI safeguards, and meaningful professional-body engagement.

## Posture in the movement

The RCN's contribution to the make-AI-good movement is distinctive in that it operates through the professional-body and trade-union channel rather than the civil-society-NGO or litigation channel. Its half-million-strong membership means that its position statements and Congress debates mobilise the nursing profession itself — not a specialist AI-activist constituency — as the audience engaged in shaping AI deployment in the NHS. The 2026 joint open letter to the Health Secretary represents the clearest operationalisation of this: a multi-union coalition of clinical bodies pressing a government workforce plan that treats AI productivity gains as a staffing substitute. Within the corpus, the RCN is the United Kingdom's structural counterpart to the United States' [National Nurses United](../organizations/org-national-nurses-united.md), whose parallel AI advocacy — the "Nurses and Patients Bill of Rights" framing backed by a 2,300-respondent workforce survey — represents the same professional-union-as-AI-advocacy-vehicle structure adapted to the US labour context.
