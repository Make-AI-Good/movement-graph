# Community-rooted, globally-distributed AI research as counter to extractive Big Tech AI research (DAIR)

This Strategy analyses how the [Distributed AI Research Institute](../organizations/org-dair-institute.md) pursues independent AI research positioned against the incentive structures of Big Tech corporate research labs, and tracks the auditable effects of that approach.

## What the strategy is — grounded observations

*The claims in this section restate facts established in `org-dair-institute` and verified in `product/audits/org-dair-institute.md`.*

DAIR's strategy has five structurally-distinct components, each a deliberate organisational choice rather than a default.

**Independent institutional form.** DAIR was founded on 2 December 2021 — exactly one year after Gebru's contested 2 December 2020 exit from Google's Ethical AI team — as an explicit corrective to the incentive structures of Big Tech AI research (`org-dair-institute` Claims 1, 8). Rather than incorporating directly as a 501(c)(3), DAIR launched as a fiscally-sponsored project of Code for Science & Society, a deliberate choice that allowed the team to operate without building back-office infrastructure from scratch (Claim 2). The launch was framed as a corrective both to AI-inevitability discourse and to the institutional structures that produce that discourse: research labs whose budgets, publication processes, and personnel pipelines run inside the same companies whose products they are studying (Claims 6, 7).

**Foundation-only funder mix.** Founding funding was $3.7M from four named foundations: the Ford Foundation, MacArthur Foundation, Kapor Center, and Open Society Foundations (Claim 5). No corporate AI-company support is in the mix — independence from the entities whose products the institute studies is a structural prerequisite of the strategy, not an aesthetic preference.

**Global team distribution with explicit demographic shape.** DAIR's team is distributed across 18+ countries, 7 time zones, and 20+ languages (Claim 4), with a structurally pan-African-diaspora distribution spanning Ethiopia, Ghana, South Africa, Kenya, Europe, Australia, and the United States. Senior staff include [Timnit Gebru](../persons/person-timnit-gebru.md) as Founder and Executive Director, Alex Hanna as Director of Research, and Dylan Baker as Lead Research Engineer — Hanna and Baker having transitioned from Google's Ethical AI team in February 2023 (Claims 9, 11). Founding advisors Safiya Noble and Ciira wa Maina, named in the December 2021 CS&S announcement, are now seated as board members (Claim 17).

**Three working principles as method.** DAIR's stated method is three working principles published on its own homepage (Claim 20): cutting through AI hype by demonstrating what AI can realistically do and where its limits are; grounding research in community expertise and local needs; and cultivating alternative technological futures through what the team calls imaginative discipline. The principles are paraphrased in DAIR's MacArthur grantee record as data for marginalised communities, ethical frameworks for AI research, and inclusive technological futures (Claim 27).

**Community-led research programmes.** The strategy's most institutionally-load-bearing programme is the [Data Workers' Inquiry](https://data-workers.org/) — a global, radically participatory research initiative spanning nine countries across five continents where nineteen data workers themselves serve as community researchers (Claim 21). DAIR is the initiative's primary funder, with the Weizenbaum Institute and Technische Universität Berlin as named institutional partners (Claim 22). Programme coverage includes Sama, Cloud Factory, and Remotasks content-moderation work in Kenya, refugee data annotation work in Lebanon and Syria, platform-mediated data work in Brazil, and content-moderation and chatbot-impersonation work across France, Germany, Spain, and the UK (Claim 23). The [Data Labellers Association](../organizations/org-data-labellers-association.md) — Kenya's first worker-led association for data annotators, launched in Nairobi on 13 February 2025 — sits as one of the Data Workers' Inquiry's named projects, with DAIR identified on the DLA's own public page as one of the international supporters that anchored the launch alongside [AI Now](https://ainowinstitute.org/), Turkopticon, the German Essen content moderators, and [Foxglove](../organizations/org-foxglove.md) (Claims 24, 25).

## What it accomplishes — interpretive claims

*This section is judgment, marked as judgment. The claims rest on the observations above and on the labelled-attribution effects in frontmatter, not on facts outside `grounded_in`.*

**Judgment:** the strategy substitutes an organising-and-research-infrastructure investment for the policy-advocacy or litigation tactic the corpus's other algorithmic-accountability entries lead with. Where the [Algorithmic Justice League](../organizations/org-algorithmic-justice-league.md) pursues a research-and-public-advocacy register, where Foxglove pursues a strategic-litigation register, and where the Ada Lovelace Institute pursues a policy-research register, DAIR's primary tactic is to *build the research infrastructure itself* — the institute, the funded community-led programmes (DWI), and the supporter-of-record relationships with worker-led bodies in the AI supply chain (the DLA). This is a slower, more capital-intensive, and more institutionally-load-bearing approach than the alternatives — and a complementary rather than competing one within the make-AI-good movement.

**Judgment:** DAIR's most distinctive contribution is the geographic and demographic relocation of the *site of AI research itself*. The corpus's existing algorithmic-accountability research-and-advocacy entries — AJL (Boston / US), Ada Lovelace (London / UK), AlgorithmWatch (Berlin / Germany) — share a critical-AI-research posture with DAIR but operate inside Northern Hemisphere institutional geographies and Northern Hemisphere staff distributions. DAIR is the corpus's first independent AI research entity built around an African-diaspora team distribution and explicit movement-organising partnership with worker-led bodies in the AI supply chain. The judgment-level claim: DAIR's structural answer to "who gets to do AI research about whom" is not a credentialing or representation argument inside Big Tech's existing research labs, but the construction of a parallel research institution whose geographic and demographic shape matches the workforce whose labour underwrites the systems being studied. Whether that parallel institution succeeds at the field level — at moving where AI research is centred — is unproven and contested (see counter-read).

**Judgment:** the strategy treats *the supply chain of AI* as the appropriate object of research and movement-organising, not the deployed-system layer alone. The Data Workers' Inquiry, the DLA support relationship, the Sama / Cloud Factory / Remotasks-shape coverage, and the Foxglove-adjacent litigation backdrop in the same supply chain all sit downstream of an analytic move: the AI system's politics and labour are co-constitutive of the system's outputs, so a research institute studying AI's harms must study the labour that produces those systems. This is a non-trivial analytic commitment — the corpus's other algorithmic-accountability entries do not centre supply-chain labour to the same degree. (The labour-organising entries that the corpus separately tracks — the African Content Moderators Union, the DLA itself — sit downstream of the strategy's analytic move rather than upstream of it.)

**Judgment, low-confidence:** the strategy's coupling of independent research with explicit supply-chain movement-organising support may be a deliberate hedge against the institute-form failure mode named in the counter-read — the risk that an independent research institute produces critical commentary without building movement capacity that can act on it. By naming worker-led bodies as direct programme partners (DLA via DWI), the strategy embeds DAIR in a worker-organising context that runs on a different timescale and theory of change than the institute's own publication cycle. Whether this coupling actually offsets the institute-form's limits, or merely complicates the accountability question of whose movement DAIR is building research infrastructure for, is a judgment a reader could go either way on.
