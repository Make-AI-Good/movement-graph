# Tech-worker collective refusal inside AI labs and platforms

Engineers, researchers, and product staff inside an AI lab or platform organise a collective refusal to work on a specific contract or product line — a military contract, a state-surveillance integration, a model release deemed reckless, a fired colleague's reinstatement. The refusal is signed, leaked, or staged as a walkout, and is paired with an internal demand — cancel the contract, publish the safety review, restore the worker — designed to be visible to the company's board and to the wider technical labour market that the signatories can leave for.

An actor chooses this strategy because the privileged-tech-worker workforce is the one constituency a frontier lab cannot easily replace, and its members hold both the moral standing of being inside the work and a labour-market position that survives the act of dissent. A collective refusal exploits a temporary asymmetry — that a lab depends on the people whose consent it has not asked for — and uses it to attach a public cost to a specific decision while it can still be unwound. It works best where the contract or release is itself politically vulnerable and the company values its standing with future recruits.

It trades durability for one-off leverage and depends on a labour market loose enough that signatories can survive a retaliatory dismissal. The strategy weakens sharply in a hiring downturn — workers price the personal cost above the collective gain — and is brittle against retaliation that targets organisers individually rather than the group. Sustained worker power inside frontier labs would require formal union recognition the industry has so far refused, and most refusals therefore remain episodic rather than building durable institutional weight.

## Verdict — high-leverage at the contract scale, fragile at the institutional scale

The strategy earns its keep where the target is a **specific contract or product line the company can withdraw from**, and it weakens sharply when the target is **the institution's posture as a whole**. The April 2018 [Project Maven petition](../events/event-google-project-maven-petition-2018-04.md) is the cleanest case: a single named contract (Maven), a single named counterparty (DoD), a single named renewal date (March 2019), and a single corporate-policy decision (renew or not) — every load-bearing element of the campaign was anchored on a discrete object the company could and did decline. The non-renewal announcement on [1 June 2018](https://theintercept.com/2018/06/01/google-drone-ai-project-maven-contract-renew/) and the [AI Principles publication six days later](https://blog.google/technology/ai/ai-principles/) are the strongest documented effects this strategy has produced inside an AI lab on the public record. The framing — ["Google should not be in the business of war"](../messages/msg-google-not-in-the-business-of-war.md) — is the corpus's founding artefact of the contemporary tech-worker-conscience lineage, and its anonymous-internal-letter form, multi-team cross-functional organising shape, and "no [tech] for [state-violence system]" rhetorical posture supplied the documented template that subsequent campaigns inherited.

The strategy is weaker — but not absent — where the institutional scale is the target rather than a contract. The November 2018 global Google walkout (approximately 20,000 workers across 50 offices) ran on the cross-team employee networks the Maven campaign built and is the most legible evidence that the organising form had compounded — at least briefly — into durable cross-team infrastructure inside one employer. The [12 October 2021 No Tech For Apartheid letter](https://laborforpalestine.net/2021/10/12/we-are-google-and-amazon-workers-we-condemn-project-nimbus-the-guardian/) signed by 390+ Google and Amazon workers on Project Nimbus reproduced the form three years later inside the same employer and explicitly cited the Maven lineage in its text. Neither of these institutional-scale campaigns produced a Maven-class corporate-policy outcome; both produced earned media and political legibility without changing the underlying contract architecture. The verdict that holds across these cases is the gradient one: the strategy is sharply effective at the contract scale and structurally weaker — but still productive of public-record evidence — at the institutional-posture scale.

Two further conditions sharpen this verdict. First, **the strategy depends on a loose labour market and a managerial register that treats employee dissent as a legitimacy problem rather than a discipline problem**. Both conditions held in 2018 and have weakened since. The April 2024 Google firings of approximately fifty workers for sit-in protests over Project Nimbus mark a sharp departure from the management-side ["ethics is complicated, ethics is hard"](https://collectiveaction.tech/2020/tech-workers-versus-the-pentagon/) register the Maven campaign encountered, and the 2022–2024 tech-industry layoff cycle visibly tipped the personal-cost calculation against signing. Whether the strategy is viable at its pre-2022 cadence under the post-2024 retaliation regime is the open question. Second, **the strategy's wins do not yet compound into institutional power**. The [Tech Workers Coalition](https://www.coworker.org/petitions/tech-should-not-be-in-the-business-of-war) that propagated the Maven framing in 2018 has gone substantially dormant; the Alphabet Workers Union–CWA Local 1010 founded January 2021 is a minority union with no collective-bargaining authority. Each campaign disperses afterwards and the next begins on bare ground, which is the structural ceiling the counter-read in the frontmatter names as load-bearing.

Even with these conditions, the strategy's case is robust at the contract scale. It has produced the documented inflection event (Maven non-renewal), the written corporate-policy commitment (the AI Principles), the framing template ("no tech for X") that two downstream campaigns ([#NoTechForICE](../campaigns/camp-mijente-no-tech-for-ice-2018-ongoing.md), No Tech For Apartheid) have inherited, and the cross-employer industry-wide worker coalition (the [Coworker.org petition](https://www.coworker.org/petitions/tech-should-not-be-in-the-business-of-war)) that demonstrated the framing's portability outside the originating employer. Good strategy at the scale it was designed for; weaker — but still producing public-record value — at the scale newer campaigns have tried to extend it to.

## Ecology

The strategy is reinforced by [open-letter collective-signatory action](strat-open-letter-collective-signatory-action.md) — every Maven-lineage campaign has run through the open-letter form, and the medium is what gives the strategy its portable rhetorical template and its scale-of-signal across employers. It is closely adjacent to [insider whistleblowing from frontier labs](strat-insider-whistleblowing-from-frontier-labs.md) but distinct on the boundary that matters: collective refusal is a plural in-situ act demanding a specific contract or product change, whereas whistleblowing is typically a post-departure or anonymous disclosure of safety-culture or structural concerns. The June 2024 [Right to Warn letter](../events/event-openai-employees-safety-letter-2024-06-04.md) sits on the whistleblowing side of that boundary — it demanded structural protections rather than refusing a named contract — and is the cleanest test case for keeping the two strategies analytically distinct.

The strategy feeds [map the AI supply chain of state harm](strat-map-the-ai-supply-chain-of-state-harm.md) — the "no tech for X" grammar was born of the supply-chain frame, and the [Mijente #NoTechForICE](../campaigns/camp-mijente-no-tech-for-ice-2018-ongoing.md) and No Tech For Apartheid campaigns both translate worker-side refusal into an external coalition's supply-chain pressure architecture. It shares lineage with [empirical audit and expose](strat-empirical-audit-and-expose.md) at the inside-lab research-dissent end: the [forced departure of Timnit Gebru](../events/event-timnit-gebru-firing-google-2020-12-02.md) over the Stochastic Parrots paper and the founding of [DAIR](../organizations/org-dair-institute.md) sit at the boundary where collective refusal, individual research conscience, and institutional alternative-building meet. It crosses the [humanitarian-disarmament treaty](strat-humanitarian-disarmament-treaty.md) track at military AI: [Stop Killer Robots](../campaigns/camp-stop-killer-robots-autonomous-weapons-treaty.md) records Google's June 2018 withdrawal from Maven as a milestone in its own chronology, and the two strategies pull on the same lever (the contract-supply relationship) from different sides of the corporate wall. And it sits in productive tension with [organise the AI supply-chain workers](strat-organize-ai-supply-chain-workers.md): the two strategies address different constituencies — engineers and researchers inside frontier labs vs. content moderators, data labellers, and gig workers across the supply chain — but they share the worker-power frame and are beginning to coordinate via cross-sector vehicles like the [Africa Tech Workers Movement](../organizations/org-africa-tech-workers-movement.md) that link the moderator base in Nairobi to the engineering base in Mountain View through the same multinationals' contracts.

Adopter-thin in the corpus by structural design, not omission. The strategy's primary in-lab adopters — the Project Maven worker group, the No Tech For Apartheid Google/Amazon worker group, the Microsoft HoloLens IVAS protest group, the OpenAI Right-to-Warn signatories — are autonomous internal collectives at private-sector employers outside the corpus's grassroots-organising remit, and the cross-employer industry-wide vehicle that historically organised them (the Tech Workers Coalition) has gone dormant. The cleanest in-corpus adopter is [Mijente's #NoTechForICE](../campaigns/camp-mijente-no-tech-for-ice-2018-ongoing.md), which carries the strategy as one of its load-bearing supply-chain pressure surfaces — externally-coordinated rather than spontaneously internal, but actively cultivating internal worker dissent at the named contractor companies as a documented organising track. The strategy's distinctive contribution to the corpus is the framing template the rest of the no-tech-for-X lineage runs on and the inflection-event evidence (Maven non-renewal, AI Principles) that internal collective refusal can produce a Maven-class corporate-policy outcome at the contract scale when the employer is willing to read employee dissent as a legitimacy problem worth engaging on its own terms.
