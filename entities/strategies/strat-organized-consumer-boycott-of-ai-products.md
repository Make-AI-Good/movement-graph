# Organised consumer boycott of AI products

Coordinate individual consumers to cancel — or publicly pledge to cancel — paid subscriptions to a named AI product (ChatGPT Plus, Copilot, Midjourney, Perplexity Pro, a specific enterprise-consumer bundle), attached to a *specific* organising demand the vendor is expected to meet. The pledge machinery — a hosted pledge form, a running counter of confirmed cancellations, a coalition endorsement page, a press-release cadence — is what converts a diffuse consumer sentiment into a legible market signal the vendor's investor-relations, sales, and product teams have to price into their forecasts.

An actor chooses this strategy because it is the movement's *lowest-barrier direct-action instrument* — the participation cost is a few minutes on a settings page — and because it produces a form of pressure the vendor's other counter-parties cannot supply on the same timeline. Legislative pressure runs on a multi-year cycle; a shareholder resolution runs on the annual proxy cycle; a boycott produces a downward revision to the vendor's ARR forecast in the current quarter. The strategy also converts the movement's abstract governance demand — model refusal on autonomous weapons, transparency on training-data provenance, retraction of a specific deployment — into a demand the vendor's own subscription-metrics dashboard makes legible in real time.

It trades scale for substitution risk. The pledge mechanism produces a public commitment more powerful than a private cancellation, but a cancellation that migrates the consumer to a *competitor AI product* is not a market signal against AI — it is a churn event the vendor's competitor absorbs at no net movement gain. The strategy therefore lives or dies on whether the demand's framing routes the movement toward *non-AI alternatives* or toward *governance-compliant AI alternatives*, and the sectoral coalition composition (democracy, climate, labour) is what determines which of the two the pledge machinery communicates. The instrument is also captured by the vendor's price-and-feature response: a targeted vendor that responds with a compliance concession removes the boycott's target, but a targeted vendor that responds with a subscription-price cut fragments the coalition by giving reluctant participants an off-ramp back into use.

The strategy pairs at the *consumer-pay-side* register with creator-class collective bargaining (supplier-side refusal of AI training on rights-holder work), with institutional procurement refusal (institutional-buyer-side), and with cross-professional refusal (professional-body-side). All four are refusal strategies operating on different counter-parties in the vendor's revenue stack; the consumer arm is the register the other three cannot reach, and the aggregate leverage of a coordinated four-arm refusal push is what defines the strategy's ceiling.
