# International human-rights body engagement on AI harms

Route AI harms through the existing international human-rights machinery: submissions to the UN Special Rapporteurs (privacy, freedom of expression, racism, contemporary forms of slavery, extreme poverty, right of peaceful assembly, freedom of association, migrants, Palestine, occupied territories, extrajudicial executions); shadow reports to the treaty bodies (Human Rights Committee, CESCR, CERD, CEDAW, CRC, CAT) at each state party's periodic review; submissions to the Universal Periodic Review of the state's AI-related conduct; interventions at regional bodies (Council of Europe, Inter-American Commission on Human Rights, African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights); and briefings to the Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights and the UN General Assembly's Third Committee. The output is a *documented international-record placement*: a rapporteur report, a treaty-body concluding observation, a UPR recommendation the state either accepts, notes, or rejects on the international record.

An actor chooses this strategy because it converts a domestic AI harm the target state's own political process may be unwilling to address into a state obligation the international system periodically re-examines the state against. The rapporteur, treaty body, or regional court is not enforcement — the state can and often does ignore recommendations — but the *record* the machinery produces is durable, cross-referable, and travels: it becomes citable evidence for a domestic constitutional challenge, a European or Inter-American court application, a coalition lobbying campaign, and the next cycle's UPR. The strategy is also one of the movement's few instruments for state-to-state pressure: a UPR recommendation from one state to another routes through the receiving state's diplomatic apparatus at a level below the domestic political blockage.

It trades enforcement speed for legitimacy. The machinery moves in multi-year cycles (UPR is 4.5 years; treaty-body reviews are 4–8 years; a Special Rapporteur report is 6–18 months from thematic call to General Assembly presentation), the recommendations are non-binding, and the state's compliance is voluntary in the first order. The strategy is also brittle to political capture at the receiving end: a state that has strategic partners on the Council can trade recommendation-acceptance for reciprocal silence on the partner's own record. The instrument is strongest when the domestic movement uses the international record as *fuel* for a domestic advocacy cycle, not when the international placement is treated as the terminal outcome.

The strategy sits *upstream* of the humanitarian-disarmament-treaty arm — a treaty coalition needs the multi-year record of Special Rapporteur and treaty-body concerns to demonstrate that the existing law is insufficient — and *complementary* to coalition lobbying of binding regional regulation, where the European or regional legislator is influenced by the same international-record substrate the strategy is producing. Empirical audit and expose feeds the substantive submissions; survivor-led testimony supplies the direct-affected voice the machinery formally receives; map-the-supply-chain work names the specific vendors and contracting states the shadow report or rapporteur report identifies as accountable.
