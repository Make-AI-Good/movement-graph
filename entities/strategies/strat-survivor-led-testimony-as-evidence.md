  Four structural limits on the strategy that the body's logic understates. (1) Procedural attrition is the strategy's principal vulnerability against a corporate respondent. The Motaung petition was filed 10 May 2022; four court years later, on 12 February 2026, Justice Nduma Nderi postponed the substantive ruling that had been expected on the consolidated petitions, with the claimants signalling they would petition the Chief Justice to expedite. Meta has spent the entire period litigating jurisdiction — gag-order applications, strike-out motions, Court of Appeal stays, Supreme Court certification attempts. The strategy can survive this only with NGO infrastructure (Foxglove's communications, Nzili & Sumbi's litigation capacity) holding the witness through years of pre-trial limbo; without that infrastructure, no merits ruling is reachable. (2) The witness's thesis can be wrong. The GMCDP/DWP case closed with Foxglove writing that "to the best of our knowledge, the DWP algorithm is not making decisions that result in disabled people being unfairly targeted for benefit fraud investigations" — three years of disclosure pressure built on testimony that the disclosed system did not validate in its operational details. The strategy survived this honestly because the disclosure itself became the accountability artefact; but the case shows that survivor-testimony cannot make a thesis true. When the institutional design works differently than the witness's lived experience suggested, the strategy falls back on disclosure / procedural wins rather than substantive vindication. (3) Coalition-folded forms dilute the strategy's leverage. Inside Stop Killer Robots' treaty-driven advocacy, survivor testimony is one register among diplomatic engagement at CCW/GGE/UN First Committee, technical-research output from Automated Decision Research, public-facing campaigns, and national-coalition lobbying. The November 2023 UN vote is real but attributing it to survivor-testimony specifically over-credits one register inside a coalition strategy; the strategy is most legible at its purest in litigation where the witness-as-named-plaintiff produces a discrete legal effect. (4) Duty of care is a hard cost and the scalability/credibility trade-off is structural. Motaung organised The Alliance in 2019, was fired, then carried four years of exile and a fifth year of pre-trial limbo as the public-facing whistleblower. Foxglove's own framing acknowledges the witness-as-prop failure mode is real and not theoretical. The 185-moderator collective extends the witness layer from individual to workforce-scale; 144 PTSD diagnoses by Dr Kanyanya show the duty-of-care exposure scales linearly with petitioner count. The strategy cannot eliminate the cost-per-witness — it can only distribute it across infrastructure capable of holding it. A maximal counter-read is that the witness gives the campaign a public-facing identity that NGO-fronted litigation cannot produce, and that identity is what the strategy delivers; the *evidentiary* function the strategy claims is downstream of court jurisdiction and procedural endurance, neither of which the survivor provides.

# Survivor-led testimony as the evidentiary anchor for an AI fight

People who have been directly harmed by an AI system — content moderators traumatised by reviewing extreme content, claimants wrongly denied benefits by an algorithm, the targeted of mercenary spyware, the families of those killed by autonomous-weapon strikes — are placed at the centre of the campaign as named plaintiffs, named witnesses, and named spokespeople. The organisation's role is to provide legal, communications, and care infrastructure around them; the public-facing argument is theirs.

An actor chooses this strategy because automated systems are designed to produce no human face on either end of a decision, and a serious witness restores one. A named, supported survivor is harder for an institution to attack than an NGO is; their account, once placed on the legal record or in a parliamentary committee, becomes the citation every later phase of the campaign reaches for. It also disciplines the campaign: questions of remedy come back to what would actually serve the people in front of you.

It trades off scalability for credibility and carries a duty-of-care cost that is easy to underestimate — testimony is an act of exposure, often into hostile press and adversarial process, and the support structure has to be real. A campaign that uses a survivor as a prop, rather than as a principal, will eventually be read that way.

## Where the strategy wins, where it ceilings

The strategy is at its purest in litigation, where the witness-as-named-plaintiff produces a discrete legal effect — Justice Gakeri's 6 February 2023 "proper party" holding against Meta, the 20 September 2024 Court of Appeal dismissal of Meta's jurisdictional appeals as "devoid of merit", the 12-month-deeper accumulation of public-record admissions from the DWP. The Analyst's reading is that the strategy wins when *four* conditions stack: the domestic forum has jurisdiction over the harm; a credible witness can carry the case under cross-examination over a multi-year pre-trial; the institution behind the harm is reachable through that forum (corporate or state); and the duty-of-care infrastructure (legal, communications, mental health, financial) exists to hold the witness over years of process. Where any of the four is absent the strategy degrades to a public-facing identity for an NGO-fronted campaign — useful, but not what the strategy claims to deliver.

It is also the most honest of the litigation-anchored strategies in the corpus, because its accountability artefact can be a disclosure even when the witness's substantive thesis is not borne out by the disclosed system — the GMCDP/DWP closing was that the General Matching Service does not appear to drive case outcomes the way the Coalition's testimony had suggested, and the strategy survived this because the disclosed operational record was itself the public-record artefact the campaign had been pressing for. The Analyst's reading is that this is the strategy's most underappreciated property: its accountability outcome can be the disclosure produced by the witness-as-plaintiff's standing, independent of whether the disclosed system vindicates the witness's experience.

It ceilings against procedural attrition by corporate respondents, against the structural impossibility of making a witness's lived experience true if the institutional design is in fact different, and against the dilution that occurs when survivor-testimony is folded into coalitions where treaty-advocacy, technical-research, or diplomatic-engagement registers carry the primary load. The strategy does not compose linearly past the individual-witness scale: the 185-moderator collective extends it but does not eliminate the per-witness duty-of-care cost (144 PTSD diagnoses are 144 ongoing duty-of-care obligations). The ecology around it does the work the strategy on its own cannot — paired with strategic-litigation as its legal vehicle, with organize-supply-chain-workers where the witness becomes a founding organiser (Motaung → the African Content Moderators Union), with empirical-audit-and-expose where the witness's account becomes the empirical record audit extends, with counter-narrative-framing as its public-facing register, and adjacent to humanitarian-disarmament-treaty where it is one register among coalition advocacy and to map-the-ai-supply-chain where both strategies anchor abstract harms in named exposure.
