counter_read: |
  Three structural limits on the strategy that the body's logic understates. (1) The contract-side demand is rarely met where the customer is a state actor with no peer pressure to reverse. Mijente's seven-year #NoTechForICE has not severed Palantir's federal contracts — ICE renewed the Palantir Investigative Case Management contract on 27 November 2019 (eight days after the campaign's largest day of action), and in April 2025 awarded Palantir a $30 million contract for ImmigrationOS, an AI-driven surveillance-and-deportation-logistics platform whose contract runs through September 2027. The wins are sponsorship-side, recruitment-side, and reputational (the 2020 Palantir HQ relocation, the academic and conference sponsorship drops); the core deployment hardens. The Mexican Pegasus campaign has produced one named criminal arrest in nine years (a former state prosecutor charged with illegal Pegasus resale, 2021) and zero senior-official convictions against the Peña Nieto or López Obrador administrations. Clearview is the partial exception: a regulator-led path where the vendor itself faces statutory penalty — but Clearview has substantially refused to comply with the EU orders, removed data-access-request instructions from its website, and continues to operate against non-EU customers. (2) The treadmill problem is structural to the strategy. Vendors swap, contracts re-route, supply chains reform. The same firms named in the October 2018 *Who's Behind ICE?* report — Palantir, AWS, Microsoft, Salesforce, Northrop Grumman, LexisNexis, Thomson Reuters — are still the named targets in 2025 alongside Dell and Hewlett-Packard for the Trump 2.0 detention-and-deportation machine, and ImmigrationOS is a different product than the 2018-era ICM and FALCON so even the map is moving. Citizen Lab has now identified Pegasus operators across multiple Mexican administrations, plus parallel deployments in Jordan, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, and the Gulf — the commercial-spyware industry's customer base regrows as fast as it is exposed. (3) The map's leverage is downstream of constituency strength. The strategy works where there is a constituency whose pressure on the vendor is credible (immigrant rights, civil rights, tech-worker conscience, GDPR-empowered EU regulators with statutory teeth, academic faculty whose institutions care about reputation). Where that constituency is absent or weakly mobilised, the map is journalism, not leverage. The Mexican Pegasus documentary record is among the most substantial of any commercial-spyware accountability file in the corpus, and Mexican domestic political pressure on the military-intelligence customer has remained limited. The clearest wins land where two registers stack — EU regulator plus civil-society coalition, university donors plus student conscience — and the strategy on its own does not generate those registers; it directs them.

# Map the corporate supply chain behind state AI-driven harm

Identify the specific companies, contracts, data brokers, and cloud providers that make a coercive state programme — deportation, mass surveillance, predictive policing, targeted spyware — technically possible, then publish the map. The deliverable is a sourced public document naming names: contract numbers, dollar amounts, the vendor's stated role, the technical capabilities it provides. The document is then handed to investors, employees, students, and shareholders of the named vendors.

An actor chooses this strategy because the state programme is too well-defended to attack directly and too distributed across statute and discretion to legislate against. The corporate vendors are softer targets — they answer to investors who care about reputational risk, to employees who can refuse to work on a project, to universities that can drop them from research partnerships. Naming the vendor converts a diffuse political fight into a specific institutional one with clear pressure points.

It trades off attribution risk and a treadmill problem: vendors can be replaced, contracts re-routed, and the campaign has to remap on every rotation. The strategy works best when paired with a constituency (immigrant rights, civil rights, tech workers) whose pressure on the vendor is credible; without that, the map is journalism, not leverage.
