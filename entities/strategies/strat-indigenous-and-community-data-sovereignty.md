# Indigenous and community data sovereignty

A community — Indigenous nations, kin networks, diaspora associations, language communities — asserts ownership and governance over data generated by or about its members: language corpora, genomic samples, traditional-knowledge records, demographic statistics, place-name datasets. The work institutionalises that ownership through community data trusts, sovereignty principles (the CARE principles for Indigenous data governance), community-controlled ethics review, and licensing regimes that require ongoing community consent and benefit-sharing for any AI use of the data. Where a state or company has already taken the data, the work becomes restitution: deletion, repatriation, retraining without it.

An actor chooses this strategy because AI's central feedstock is data, and the default extractive pipeline routes that data through state and corporate intermediaries who treat communal knowledge as a public good available for free. A sovereignty framing reasserts that data carries ongoing rights of its source community — not merely the rights of individuals inside it — and shifts the unit of consent from the individual to the collective. The approach also provides a mechanism communities can actually run themselves, which is rare in a movement where most leverage requires access to legislatures, courts, or capital the community does not control.

It trades interoperability with the dominant infrastructure for governance autonomy. A sovereignty regime forces a fork in the technical stack — separate AI tools trained on community-governed corpora, separate evaluation procedures, separate hosting — which is expensive to build and difficult to sustain at the scale a community of a few hundred thousand people can fund. The strategy also depends on state recognition of communal data rights, which most jurisdictions grant unevenly, and is fragile against companies that argue their AI was already trained on the extracted data before the sovereignty regime existed.
