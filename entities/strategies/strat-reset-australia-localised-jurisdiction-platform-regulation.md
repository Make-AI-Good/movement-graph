# Localised-jurisdiction policy-research as the route into Australian platform and AI regulation (Reset.Tech Australia)

This Strategy analyses how [Reset.Tech Australia](../organizations/org-reset-australia.md) pursues platform and AI regulation in Australia through localised-jurisdiction evidence-led policy research, and tracks the auditable effects of that approach.

## What the strategy is — grounded observations

*The claims in this section restate facts established in `org-reset-australia` and verified in `product/audits/org-reset-australia.md` (Claims 14, 19–25). They do not extend to any other entity.*

Reset.Tech Australia's strategy has four structurally-distinct components, each a deliberate organisational choice rather than a default.

**Localised translation of a global platform-regulation argument.** The organisation was launched on 11 November 2020 as the Australian affiliate of the global Reset network and explicitly oriented its work to the Australian regulatory file (`org-reset-australia` Claim 1, Claim 14). The substantive argument the global network carries — that the targeted-advertising business model of the major platforms and the recommender, ranking, and generative-AI systems built on top of it are the upstream cause of disinformation and algorithmic harm, and that the right object of intervention is platform business design rather than individual users — is translated by the Australian entity into the Australian regulatory stack: the *Privacy Act* review, the ACCC digital-platforms inquiry, the eSafety Commissioner's industry-codes process, the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner's Children's Online Privacy Code process, and the September 2024 mandatory-AI-guardrails consultation. The strategy is thus a *jurisdictional translation* play: the substantive argument is shared with the global network; the regulatory hooks are specifically Australian.

**Four-pillar structural-policy frame with AI running across.** Reset.Tech Australia opened with three named priority areas (foreign interference in elections, online threats to the safety of young people, and the amplification of fringe and extreme voices on platforms — Claim 14) and has since broadened into a four-pillar structural-policy frame covering competition and digital markets, consumer protection and data protection, online safety, and platform transparency and accountability. AI-specific policy work runs across all four rather than as a fifth separate file. The organisation's posture is to treat tech and AI companies like other industries — expected to deliver fair markets, safe products, and proactive risk mitigation — rather than as a sui-generis sector entitled to exemption from independent oversight.

**Evidence-led empirical testing as the method.** The strategy's method is empirical rather than purely normative. The signature consumer-protection artefact is the December 2023 report *Australians for Sale: Targeted Advertising, Data Brokering and Consumer Manipulation* (Claim 20), co-published with [CHOICE and the Consumer Policy Research Centre](https://www.purpose.com/case-study/reset-australia/) (Claim 21), which used the Xandr File — a data set briefly made public by Microsoft in 2021 — to enumerate the audience segments data brokers hold on Australian consumers and to document concrete profiling of people who gamble, who consume alcohol, who are experiencing financial stress, and children and young people. The signature platform-governance artefact is the March 2024 enforcement complaint against Meta under the Australian Code of Practice on Disinformation and Misinformation: Reset.Tech submitted 152 posts containing fact-checked falsehoods to test Meta's transparency-report claim that it applies warning labels to all such content, and found only 8 percent received labels (Claim 24). The DIGI complaints subcommittee rejected the complaint in favour of a Meta offer of future clarifying language (Claim 25). Across both artefacts the method is the same: build an empirical record against a voluntary-code claim, take it through the official channel, and use the public-record outcome as the structural argument for binding rules.

**Coalition with consumer organisations and parliamentary engagement as the channels.** The strategy's first-period output footprint reported by its co-incubator (Claim 19) — more than 100 partner organisations engaged, more than 50 meetings with politicians, nine research reports, more than 350 news articles, and 14 policy submissions — names the channels through which the empirical record is moved into the Australian regulatory environment. The two consumer-protection partners that co-published *Australians for Sale* (CHOICE and the Consumer Policy Research Centre) sit as the institutional core of that coalition; the 50+ politician meetings and 14 submissions sit as the parliamentary and consultation surface; the 350+ news articles sit as the press-cycle surface. The organisation does not take Australian Commonwealth government funding for its policy-research work, consistent with the global Reset network's positioning as independent of state revenue in jurisdictions where it operates — a deliberate choice that constrains the funder mix to philanthropy in exchange for source-of-funds independence from the Commonwealth bodies the organisation seeks to influence.

## What it accomplishes — interpretive claims

*This section is judgment, marked as judgment. The claims rest on the observations above and on the labelled-attribution effects in frontmatter, not on facts outside `grounded_in`.*

**Judgment:** the strategy substitutes a *single-jurisdiction policy-research pole* for the multi-jurisdiction litigation or multi-jurisdiction movement-building tactic the corpus's other digital-rights / platform-accountability entries lead with. The Australian regulatory file — *Privacy Act* review, ACCC digital-platforms inquiry, the disinformation-and-misinformation code, the Children's Online Privacy Code, the mandatory-AI-guardrails consultation — is treated as a coordinated regulatory stack and worked end-to-end by one local organisation, rather than as a set of files distributed across multiple specialist civil-society bodies. This is a thinner, more concentrated approach than a multi-org division of labour — and bets that the regulatory stack rewards a single organisation that can carry one argument coherently across all four pillars over multi-org specialism that would carry pillar-specific arguments separately.

**Judgment:** the strategy's most distinctive analytic move is its refusal to separate AI regulation from platform regulation. The four-pillar frame routes AI-driven recommender and ranking systems into the platform-transparency pillar, generative-AI deployment into the competition and digital-markets pillar, and algorithmic-accountability claims into the online-safety and consumer-protection pillars — rather than carving AI out as a fifth subject. The judgment-level claim: the strategy is positioned to argue that voluntary AI guardrails will fail in Australia for the same structural reason voluntary platform-content codes have failed in the disinformation file (the DIGI Code complaint is the proof-of-concept), and that a coherent Australian regulatory response requires binding rules with statutory transparency and independent-researcher data access across the whole platform-AI stack rather than carving AI off into a separate non-binding instrument. Whether the Australian regulatory environment is moved by this framing or proceeds along the technology-neutral-plus-AI-Safety-Institute path the organisation itself describes as the 2025–2026 trajectory is unproven and contested (see counter-read).

**Judgment:** the strategy's anchor in consumer-protection partners (CHOICE, the Consumer Policy Research Centre) and a registered Australian charity form — rather than purely as a research institute or purely as an advocacy front — gives the organisation a *legitimacy register* that pure-research orgs and pure-advocacy orgs do not have in the Australian regulatory file. *Australians for Sale*'s force in the *Privacy Act* review and the ACCC digital-platforms inquiry comes in significant part from the consumer-organisation co-publishers as much as from the empirical record itself: the report is not a research institute's claim about consumers, it is a claim co-signed by the consumer organisations Australians already recognise as their consumer-protection interlocutors.

**Judgment, low-confidence:** the strategy's empirical-evidence-versus-voluntary-code play (the 152-posts DIGI complaint) is *designed to be appealable in either direction* — a rejection becomes the structural argument against voluntary codes; a partial concession would become the structural argument for binding rules. This is a deliberate hedge against the documented Australian-regulatory pattern of preferring voluntary industry codes over binding statutory obligations. Whether such "lose-the-channel-win-the-argument" rhetorical hedging actually moves regulatory direction, or whether it instead allows regulators to point to the existence of the channel (the DIGI complaints subcommittee) as evidence that the voluntary architecture works, is a judgment a reader could go either way on.
