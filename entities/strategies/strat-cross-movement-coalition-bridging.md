# Cross-movement coalition bridging

Explicit organising work — most often carried by a small backbone organisation — that builds durable alliance infrastructure between the AI-good movement and adjacent movements: climate (against AI compute's grid draw, water use, siting), labor (against algorithmic management, generative displacement, tech-worker organising), racial and disability justice (against algorithmic discrimination), consumer protection (against dark patterns and adhesion contracts), housing (against tenant-screening algorithms), immigrants' rights (against biometric surveillance and detention analytics), reproductive rights (against period-tracker and location-data prosecution). The vehicle is standing coalition tables, shared messaging platforms, aligned campaign calendars, movement-to-movement staff exchanges, and joint statement-and-litigation infrastructure — not one-off endorsements.

An actor chooses this strategy because the AI movement is structurally too small to win a national or international fight on its own — the mobilised constituencies of climate, labor, and racial-justice movements are each an order of magnitude larger — and the AI-good frame layers cleanly onto each of those movements' existing grievance frames rather than displacing them. Bridging also *routes* movement infrastructure that the AI movement cannot itself build in the near term: an established labor federation's collective-bargaining apparatus, a climate coalition's mass-mobilisation calendar, a civil-rights coalition's litigation lineage. Each partnership converts the AI question into terms that constituency's members can act on without requiring them to become AI experts. The strategy is quiet compared to protest or litigation but is often the single change that turns a movement-side campaign from a five-organisation ask into a fifty-organisation coalition demand a legislator or vendor cannot dismiss.

It trades directness of AI framing for coalition breadth. The bridged coalition's public register frequently drops "AI" from the top-line message and speaks in the partner movement's native frame (climate, jobs, civil rights); the AI-specific policy demand is embedded rather than announced, and the coalition's messaging discipline can look like retreat to observers holding an AI-centric frame. Coordination costs are also high: the backbone organisation absorbs enormous convening labour to hold divergent constituencies, decision-making cycles slow to the pace of the slowest member, and message-alignment across constituencies with different theories of change is a permanent negotiation rather than a solved problem. The strategy is also structurally dependent on the political stance of the partner movement — a climate coalition whose leadership is friendly to a hyperscaler's data-centre siting will pull the AI question rightward, and a coalition-bridging strategy inherits that pull.

This strategy differs from [strat-coalition-lobbying-of-binding-regional-regulation](strat-coalition-lobbying-of-binding-regional-regulation.md) by *layer of the coalition* — coalition-lobbying is a specific-target legislative campaign a coalition runs, cross-movement bridging is the standing alliance infrastructure the specific campaigns are subsequently drawn from. It is a *pre-condition* strategy: many campaigns in this corpus (from [strat-mass-protest-for-ai-moratorium](strat-mass-protest-for-ai-moratorium.md) to [strat-organized-consumer-boycott-of-ai-products](strat-organized-consumer-boycott-of-ai-products.md)) fired because a bridged coalition already existed to carry them.
