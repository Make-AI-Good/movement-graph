# Ballot initiative and referendum on AI

Take an AI question directly to voters through the ballot-initiative, popular-referendum, or citizens'-initiative machinery a jurisdiction makes available — US state initiatives (California Propositions, Oregon Measures, Massachusetts Questions), Swiss federal Initiatives and cantonal Volksinitiativen, European Citizens' Initiative in the EU institutions, national referenda on major technology questions. The vehicle is a signature-collection campaign that puts a defined AI policy — a facial-recognition ban, a data-broker prohibition, a public-sector algorithmic-decision moratorium, a right-to-human-review, an AI-consumer bill of rights, a compute-tax — on the ballot for a binding popular vote.

An actor chooses this strategy because it bypasses the legislative venue entirely on questions the movement expects to win at the polls but lose in the committee — a legislature captured by industry interests can be routed around by taking the question to voters directly, and a state-constitutional amendment or successful initiative carries statutory or constitutional durability the same coalition could not extract from a normal legislative majority. Ballot campaigns also produce mass-public engagement of a shape no other strategy can generate — a defined vote by a defined electorate on a defined AI question, with press coverage, canvass conversation, and post-vote polling that themselves become movement infrastructure. Where the initiative wins, the coalition holds statutory ground; where the initiative loses, the coalition often still shifts the legislature by proving the question is politically contested at the popular level.

It trades win-condition clarity for extreme cost and preemptibility. Signature thresholds are high, campaigns run six-to-eight-figure budgets, industry can outspend the movement 5-to-1 in the ballot-communication phase, and the strategy is available only in the fraction of jurisdictions whose constitutions permit it (broad in California, Oregon, Colorado; narrow in most European countries and non-existent at US federal level). A successful initiative is also structurally exposed to legislative rollback in jurisdictions where the initiative does not amend the constitution — a majority in the next legislature can amend or repeal a statutory initiative — and to legal challenge under federal preemption or constitutional grounds the industry brings to court immediately after certification. The strategy is capital-intensive, jurisdiction-limited, and calendar-locked to the election cycle.

This strategy differs from [strat-municipal-affirmative-ban-on-a-class-of-ai-use](strat-municipal-affirmative-ban-on-a-class-of-ai-use.md) by *legislative actor* — a municipal ban is enacted by a city council of elected representatives, a ballot initiative is enacted by the electorate directly — and by scale, since state and national ballots reach an electorate the municipal instrument does not. It differs from [strat-coalition-lobbying-of-binding-regional-regulation](strat-coalition-lobbying-of-binding-regional-regulation.md) by *route around the legislature*, and from [strat-participatory-deliberation-as-policy-input](strat-participatory-deliberation-as-policy-input.md) by *binding force* — the initiative's vote produces enforceable law, a deliberation's report produces recommendations. As of this drafting the strategy has been used sparingly on AI-defined ballot questions specifically, and stands as an under-utilised instrument the movement's harder-won municipal wins could seed at state scale.
