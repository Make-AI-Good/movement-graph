# How EU Funding and Exports of High-Risk AI Systems Exacerbate Severe Human Rights Violations in Palestine and the Broader Region

*How EU Funding and Exports of High-Risk AI Systems Exacerbate Severe Human Rights Violations in Palestine and the Broader Region* is the [regional human rights investigation](https://7amleh.org/post/eu-funding-export-high-risk-ai-palestine-region-en) published on 16 March 2026 by [7amleh – The Arab Center for the Advancement of Social Media](../organizations/org-7amleh.md). The report maps EU funding programs, investment mechanisms, and European technology exports that supply high-risk AI systems across Palestine, Egypt, Morocco, Lebanon, and Tunisia — documenting how European regulatory standards that restrict the use of these systems inside the EU operate as a floor that does not follow EU money or EU exports beyond its borders, producing a structural double standard in which populations facing the most acute human rights risks receive the weakest AI governance protections.

## The three pathways

The report identifies three [pathways through which EU-origin resources reach high-risk AI systems](https://7amleh.org/post/eu-funding-export-high-risk-ai-palestine-region-en) deployed against populations in the MENA region. The first is EU-funded migration management: European funding programs for border management and migration control in the southern Mediterranean finance the deployment of advanced surveillance technologies in Morocco, Tunisia, Lebanon, and Egypt — countries positioned as the EU's external-border front line for stemming migration toward Europe. The second is financial and research support for Israeli weapons and technology companies developing AI-based tools, which the report documents as a cross-cutting channel connecting European investment to AI systems later deployed in the Palestinian context. The third is direct export by European companies of high-risk AI systems to MENA governments, enabled by [loopholes in existing EU legislation](https://globalvoices.org/2026/04/16/research-reveals-that-eu-ai-rules-stop-at-its-borders-with-little-accountability-for-human-rights-impacts-abroad/) governing dual-use export controls and corporate due-diligence obligations.

## Technologies catalogued

The technologies the report catalogues as flowing through these pathways include [facial recognition systems, biometric fingerprint recognition, smart city infrastructure, smart border crossing gates, traveler screening and risk analysis systems, AI-enabled drones, and digital identity systems](https://ifex.org/eu-funded-ai-systems-used-in-surveillance-and-repression-across-palestine-and-the-region/). In the Palestinian context these technologies sit within a digital-control architecture that 7amleh had documented extensively in prior work — the [July 2024 position paper on the EU AI Act and Palestinian digital rights](https://7amleh.org/post/paper-on-the-eu-s-ai-act-and-its-implications-en) had already examined the specific legal loopholes that allow AI systems prohibited or restricted inside the EU to be applied against Palestinians. The March 2026 report extends that legal analysis to a comparative regional frame, examining how the same export channels and funding mechanisms replicate the dynamic across Egypt, Morocco, Lebanon, and Tunisia.

## Legislative critique

The report finds that two pieces of EU legislation that should constrain the export and funding of human-rights-violating AI systems contain [significant loopholes that permit exactly that](https://globalvoices.org/2026/04/16/research-reveals-that-eu-ai-rules-stop-at-its-borders-with-little-accountability-for-human-rights-impacts-abroad/): the 2021 Regulation on exports of dual-use items does not reach many of the AI systems the report catalogues; the 2024 Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive's obligations are inadequate for the transparency and human rights due diligence the funding and transfer processes require. The core critique is structural: the EU AI Act's risk-classification framework identifies the categories of harm these systems pose, but that framework's force applies to deployment within the EU rather than to the export of the same systems to third-country governments with weaker rights protections. EU AI rules effectively stop at EU borders — the framing Global Voices used to characterise the report's finding — summarises the governance gap the report is aimed at closing.

## Geographic scope

The report's coverage of Palestine, Egypt, Morocco, Lebanon, and Tunisia gives the corpus its first publication-class entry with substantive treatment of Egypt and Morocco in the AI governance register. Egypt is the region's most populous state and a significant participant in EU-funded migration-management programs; Morocco similarly serves as a key EU external-border management partner under EU-funded programmes along the Atlantic and Mediterranean migration routes. The report situates both countries' AI deployments within the same EU-funding-and-export pipeline it documents for Lebanon and Tunisia, providing the corpus's first evidence-based entry on how EU-origin AI systems and funding reach North African governance contexts specifically.

## Recommendations

The report's [recommendations](https://7amleh.org/post/eu-funding-export-high-risk-ai-palestine-region-en) are addressed to EU institutions: closure of the legislative loopholes in dual-use export regulation and corporate due-diligence obligations; mandatory human rights impact assessments prior to EU funding of surveillance-technology programs in third countries; transparency requirements for EU-funded AI deployments outside EU territory; and cessation of financial and research support for AI systems used in documented human rights violations. The report's submission into UNISPAL documentation channels positions these recommendations for uptake in multilateral human rights processes alongside EU legislative reform discussions.

## Posture within the corpus

*How EU Funding and Exports of High-Risk AI Systems Exacerbate Severe Human Rights Violations in Palestine and the Broader Region* sits alongside [*Attacks on Palestinian Digital Rights*](pub-7amleh-attacks-on-palestinian-digital-rights.md) as the second [7amleh](../organizations/org-7amleh.md) publication in the corpus, extending the Palestinian digital-rights register into a comparative MENA frame. In the publications geography, it fills a gap the MENA-publications slate had not otherwise addressed directly: [SMEX](../organizations/org-smex.md)'s [*AI Investments in the Gulf*](pub-smex-ai-investments-gulf.md) covers Gulf-state commercial AI investment; 7amleh's prior *Attacks on Palestinian Digital Rights* covers Palestinian platform-accountability and content-moderation experience; neither enters the territory of EU-funded surveillance infrastructure in the southern Mediterranean and Maghreb countries specifically. This publication is the corpus's first entry centred on that intersection, and the first to provide source material anchored in the Egyptian and Moroccan AI governance contexts.
