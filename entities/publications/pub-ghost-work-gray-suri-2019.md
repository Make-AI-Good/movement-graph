# Ghost Work: How to Stop Silicon Valley from Building a New Global Underclass

*Ghost Work: How to Stop Silicon Valley from Building a New Global Underclass* is [the 2019 book](https://ghostwork.info/ghost-work/) by anthropologist [Mary L. Gray](../persons/person-mary-l-gray.md) and computer scientist [Siddharth Suri](../persons/person-siddharth-suri.md), published by Houghton Mifflin Harcourt on 7 May 2019 (ISBN 9781328566249). Winner of the [2019 McGannon Center Book Prize at Fordham University](https://www.fordham.edu/info/29820/news_for_the_mcgannon_center/11717/mcgannon_center_book_prize_for_2019_gray_and_suri_s_ghost_work) and named a [Financial Times 2019 Critic's Pick](https://ghostwork.info/ghost-work/), it names and documents "ghost work" — the task-based human labor that digital platforms and AI systems structurally require but systematically conceal, routed invisibly through APIs and algorithmic interfaces so that the worker disappears from the end user's view. It is the foundational text of the platform-labor register within the AI-good movement: the publication that coined "ghost work" as a term and placed the AI supply chain labor question in general circulation.

## Automation's last mile

The book's animating argument is what Gray and Suri call [automation's last mile](https://ghostwork.info/ghost-work/): the irreducible remainder of tasks that AI systems cannot fully automate, offloaded onto a distributed human workforce working piece-rate through web platforms and APIs. The paired claim is that work isn't disappearing in the age of AI — it is being hidden: rendered invisible by technical architecture, contractual classification, and platform design that systematically interpose an automated-looking interface between the requester and the worker. The book's core definition, as the Wikipedia ghost-work article states it, is [work performed by a human, but believed by a customer to be performed by an automated process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghost_work). The named companies structurally reliant on ghost work include Amazon, Google, Microsoft, and Uber, whose products Gray and Suri document as [functioning smoothly only through the labor of a vast, invisible human workforce](https://ghostwork.info/ghost-work/) performing content moderation, data labeling, proofreading, image tagging, and other judgment-bearing tasks that automated systems cannot yet reliably perform. Technology critic Lilly Irani is cited by the Wikipedia article as observing that tech companies [cultivate a perception of "technological magic" while obscuring the human labor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghost_work) — the discursive move the book is directed at naming and refusing.

## Research base and findings

The book's evidentiary base is a [five-year ethnographic study](https://ghostwork.info/ghost-work/) combining fieldwork and survey data across the United States and India — the two primary labor markets for English-language ghost work on platforms including Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk), CrowdFlower (later Figure Eight), and their contemporaries. The workforce documented includes [young mothers, early retirees, recent graduates, and people excluded from conventional employment markets](https://ghostwork.info/ghost-work/), working piece-rate with earnings frequently below legal minimums for traditional employment, no health benefits, no employer-relationship protections, at-will termination, and isolation as a structural feature — workers operating, as the book phrases it, "in isolation, amid great uncertainty, without feedback or benefits." [Approximately 8% of Americans have at some point participated in the ghost economy](https://ghostwork.info/ghost-work/) — the statistic the book placed in general circulation to position ghost work as a mainstream labor condition. Gray and Suri also document early self-organization: [workers on Mechanical Turk and similar platforms formed WhatsApp groups and online coordination forums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghost_work), and in 2014 workers sent a letter to Jeff Bezos calling on Amazon to stop selling them as cheap labor and to provide tools for collective representation — organizing the book identifies as "the very beginning of a labor movement among independent workers."

## The authors

[Mary L. Gray](../persons/person-mary-l-gray.md) is Senior Principal Researcher at Microsoft Research (since 2012), Faculty in the Luddy School of Informatics, Computing, and Engineering at Indiana University (with affiliations in Anthropology and Gender Studies), and Faculty Associate at Harvard's Berkman Klein Center for Internet and Society. She holds a PhD in Communication from the University of California, San Diego (2004), and her research applies cultural anthropology and ethnographic methods to the intersection of technology, labor, and human rights. In 2020 she was named a [MacArthur Fellow](https://www.macfound.org/fellows/class-of-2020/mary-l-gray), the citation focused on her investigation of how labor, identity, and human rights are transformed by the digital economy. [Siddharth Suri](../persons/person-siddharth-suri.md) is Principal Researcher at Microsoft Research, holds a PhD in Computer and Information Science from the University of Pennsylvania (2007, under Michael Kearns), and works at the intersection of computer science, behavioral economics, and crowdsourcing. Both authors were at Microsoft Research when they conducted the study; the cross-disciplinary pairing — cultural anthropology and computational social science — is the book's methodological signature.

## Reception

Named a [Financial Times 2019 Critic's Pick](https://ghostwork.info/ghost-work/) and winner of the [2019 McGannon Center Book Prize at Fordham University](https://www.fordham.edu/info/29820/news_for_the_mcgannon_center/11717/mcgannon_center_book_prize_for_2019_gray_and_suri_s_ghost_work) — the same prize that [Virginia Eubanks](../persons/person-virginia-eubanks.md)'s *Automating Inequality* won the previous year. Endorsed by [Virginia Eubanks](../persons/person-virginia-eubanks.md), who called it "Scrupulously researched and deeply humane," and Tim O'Reilly, who described the book's finding that "Humans... are computer chips" powering internet services. The book received coverage in Fast Company, NBC News, TechCrunch, and Wired, and is widely assigned in university courses on the future of work, labor economics, and technology ethics. Mary L. Gray's 2020 MacArthur Fellowship — awarded the year following publication — named *Ghost Work* as the centerpiece of her body of work.

## Posture within the corpus

*Ghost Work* is the corpus's foundational-text entry for the platform-labor and AI supply chain register — the publication that coined the term and established the conceptual vocabulary that subsequent research, reporting, and organizing in this register draws from. Within the corpus, it sits alongside [*Automating Inequality*](pub-automating-inequality.md) and [*Managed by Bots: Data Driven Exploitation in the Gig Economy*](pub-worker-info-exchange-managed-by-bots-2021.md) as the publication-side triangle of the algorithmic-labor register: *Automating Inequality* (2018) anchors the US welfare-administration layer (algorithmic welfare-eligibility scoring, child-welfare risk prediction); *Ghost Work* (2019) anchors the AI supply chain layer (platform-mediated piece-rate labor hiding behind automation); *Managed by Bots* (2021) anchors the UK gig-economy algorithmic-management layer (data rights and GDPR litigation for app-based platform workers). The corpus's direct organizational descendants of the ghost-work register are [Worker Info Exchange](../organizations/org-worker-info-exchange.md), whose GDPR-litigation and data-rights organizing operates on the platform-labor conditions the book documents; [African Content Moderators Union](../organizations/org-african-content-moderators-union.md), whose Kenyan members perform ghost work by the book's definition — content moderation for Facebook/Meta at the platform's African outsourcing operation; and [Coworker.org](../organizations/org-coworker.md), whose platform-labor workplace-rights campaigns address the same on-demand workforce conditions the book maps.
