# Before the Bullet Hits the Body: Dismantling Predictive Policing in Los Angeles

*Before the Bullet Hits the Body: Dismantling Predictive Policing in Los Angeles* is the [May 2018 community research report](https://stoplapdspying.org/before-the-bullet-hits-the-body-dismantling-predictive-policing-in-los-angeles/) produced by the [Stop LAPD Spying Coalition](../local-groups/lg-stop-lapd-spying-coalition.md), the Skid Row-based Los Angeles community coalition organising for the abolition of police surveillance since 2011. It is the foundational publication-side artefact of the Coalition's [campaign to abolish LAPD's two predictive-policing programmes](../campaigns/camp-stop-lapd-spying-predpol-ban-los-angeles-2018-2020.md) — Operation LASER and PredPol — and the document that [directly triggered the first public hearings on data-driven policing held by any US city](https://www.technologyreview.com/2020/06/05/1002709/the-activist-dismantling-racist-police-algorithms/). The corpus's entry point for the US grassroots predictive-policing abolition register, it is the first publication in the corpus to ground algorithmic-accountability analysis in an abolitionist community-organising frame targeted at the operational algorithmic-policing apparatus of a specific US municipality, and the founding research document of what the corpus records as the first US grassroots campaign to successfully dismantle a major municipal predictive-policing programme.

## Background: Operation LASER and PredPol

By the mid-2010s the LAPD operated two concurrent predictive-policing programmes. Operation LASER (Los Angeles Strategic Extraction and Restoration) was a person-based programme using [Palantir](https://automatingbanishment.org/) technology to maintain a secret database of residents designated "chronic offenders" for intensified patrol contact — a scoring system that directed police contact toward listed community members independently of any new criminal charge and from which individuals had no removal mechanism. PredPol was a place-based programme co-founded in 2012 by UCLA anthropologist Jeff Brantingham and mathematician George Mohler on an earthquake-aftershock-prediction model, generating 500-by-500-foot crime "hot spot" predictions that directed additional patrol resources to targeted city blocks. Both programmes [concentrated their effects on Black, Brown, and unhoused communities across South Central Los Angeles and Skid Row](https://stoplapdspying.org/our-fights/data-driven-policing/) and operated without meaningful public transparency or community accountability mechanism.

The Coalition had been building toward this report through its prior published output: the 2013 *People's Audit* examined LAPD's Suspicious Activity Reporting programme, establishing the Coalition's community-research methodology and its analysis of how LAPD's intelligence-gathering framework criminalised ordinary behaviour. The 2018 report extended that analytical line to the predictive-policing layer LAPD had been building since 2011, and brought the Coalition's findings into the public-hearing register for the first time.

## Argument and approach

The report's central analytical claim is that predictive policing in Los Angeles is ["inherently flawed and racist, by design"](https://stoplapdspying.org/before-the-bullet-hits-the-body-dismantling-predictive-policing-in-los-angeles/) — systems whose discriminatory effects flow from the design logic itself rather than from fixable implementation failures. The report explicitly [does not take a "top-down policy reform or recommendation approach" and does not seek "more 'transparency' and 'oversight' of policing"](https://thestalkerstate.org/before-the-bullet-hits-the-body/) — it is positioned as an instrument for dismantling the apparatus, not improving it. The analytical architecture organises the report around four named moves: the creation of the "other" through policing categories; knowledge production and the complicity of academia (naming Jeff Brantingham's UCLA research as the intellectual infrastructure for PredPol); corporate profit and complicity (naming Palantir's Operation LASER contracting); and the "deadly impact and trauma" of predictive systems on the communities they target. The production method is the Coalition's standing framework of collective study, community-based research, and direct action — grounding findings in the experience of communities under surveillance rather than in academic or policy-audience analysis.

[Hamid Khan](../persons/person-hamid-khan.md), the Coalition's founder and coordinator and a [35-year community organizer working from Skid Row](https://www.technologyreview.com/2020/06/05/1002709/the-activist-dismantling-racist-police-algorithms/), grounded the report's abolitionist framing in an analysis of predictive policing as an apparatus rooted in structural racism and capitalist enforcement logics rather than a technical system with a correctable bias problem. [The Coalition declined partnerships with reform-oriented civil-liberties organisations](https://www.technologyreview.com/2020/06/05/1002709/the-activist-dismantling-racist-police-algorithms/) over the philosophical disagreement between reform-and-oversight and abolition frames — a distinction the report enacts at the level of its demands.

## The six-point demand

The report formalised the Coalition's six-point demand, [carried consistently through the entire 2018–2020 campaign arc](https://thestalkerstate.org/before-the-bullet-hits-the-body/): (1) immediate ban on predictive policing tactics and programmes; (2) abolition of all current surveillance technology use by law enforcement; (3) prohibition on acquiring new surveillance technology; (4) full disclosure of all surveillance deployment since inception; (5) reparations for rights violations against targeted individuals and organisations; (6) redirect surveillance funding toward community investments in housing, education, health centres, youth programmes, nutrition, and employment. The demand's structure enacts the abolitionist frame: demands 1–3 end the apparatus, demand 4 names accountability for past harm, demand 5 directs repair, and demand 6 redirects the resource the apparatus consumed toward the community needs it displaced.

## Campaign outcomes

The report's release in May 2018 [directly triggered the first public hearings on data-driven policing held by any US city](https://www.technologyreview.com/2020/06/05/1002709/the-activist-dismantling-racist-police-algorithms/), convened by the City of Los Angeles under community pressure. Parallel to the hearing campaign, the Coalition pursued public records disclosure: in December 2019, following a February 2018 CPRA lawsuit, LAPD was forced to release 679 names from its secret Chronic Offender database along with LASER Zone maps, micro-hot-spot designations, and hundreds of operational documents. The [disclosed documents identified connections between LASER Zones and police killings of seven named community members](https://stoplapdspying.medium.com/stop-lapd-spying-coalition-wins-groundbreaking-public-records-lawsuit-32c3101d4575): Jesse Romero, Richard Richer, Kenny Watkins, Keith Bursey, Daniel Perez, Grechario Mack, and Robert Diaz.

In October 2019, [the Coalition organised a letter signed by over 450 academics from UCLA and universities across the United States](https://www.technologyreview.com/2020/06/05/1002709/the-activist-dismantling-racist-police-algorithms/) rejecting PredPol's intellectual foundations and challenging the ethics of Jeff Brantingham's research. The same period produced the LAPD inspector general's March 2019 audit of LASER — the Coalition's own demand — concluding the programme could not be properly audited because its operations were too complex to evaluate, removing LAPD's principal defence of the programme. [Operation LASER was dismantled in April 2019](https://stoplapdspying.org/our-fights/data-driven-policing/). [PredPol was ended in April 2020](https://stoplapdspying.org/our-fights/data-driven-policing/); [Hamid Khan](../persons/person-hamid-khan.md) [attributed the decision to "the community rising up"](https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/carolinehaskins1/los-angeles-police-department-dumping-predpol-predictive) through the sustained organising the report had anchored, directly disputing LAPD's official account that the cancellation was COVID-19-budget-driven.

## Position within the corpus

*Before the Bullet Hits the Body* fills the predictive-policing / criminal-justice algorithmic-accountability publication slot — adjacent to [*Automating Inequality*](pub-automating-inequality.md) (welfare-and-social-services algorithmic accountability) but grounded in the US police-technology and abolition register rather than the welfare-state register. Its community-based research method and explicit abolitionist frame distinguish it from the corpus's adjacent surveillance-and-tech-accountability publications: [*Who's Behind ICE?*](pub-mijente-whos-behind-ice-2018.md) documents the federal immigration-enforcement tech-supply-chain through a corporate-accountability research frame, and the [*Atlas of AI*](pub-atlas-of-ai.md) addresses the global critical-infrastructure and labour register. The report is the corpus's clearest instance of a movement-side research document credibly linked to the direct outcomes it sought: both the April 2019 LASER dismantlement and the April 2020 PredPol cancellation trace their immediate triggers to the campaign the report anchored, making it the publication-side anchor of the corpus's earliest US operational-cancellation case-study in the algorithmic-accountability movement area.
