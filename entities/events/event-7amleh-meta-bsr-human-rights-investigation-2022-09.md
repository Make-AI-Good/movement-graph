# BSR Human Rights Due Diligence of Meta's Impacts in Israel and Palestine — publication and civil society response (September 2022)

On [22 September 2022](https://about.fb.com/news/2022/09/human-rights-impact-meta-israel-palestine/), Meta published the findings of an independent Human Rights Due Diligence assessment by Business for Social Responsibility (BSR) examining its content-moderation policies and practices with respect to Arabic and Hebrew content during the May 2021 Israel-Palestine escalation — the first independent, third-party audit of Meta's content-moderation systems focused specifically on the asymmetric treatment of Arabic and Palestinian-language content, commissioned by Meta following a formal recommendation by its Oversight Board. Five days later, [7amleh — the Arab Center for the Advancement of Social Media](../organizations/org-7amleh.md) — co-led a joint statement by more than 60 civil society organisations calling on Meta to fully implement all 21 BSR recommendations. The September 2022 event is the corpus's principal documented platform-accountability audit of Arabic-language content moderation, and the pre-October 2023 documentary and coalition anchor of the broader international civil-society campaign on Meta's treatment of Palestinian voices.

## Context: the May 2021 escalation

The assessment originated with the May 2021 escalation of violence between Israel and Palestinian territories. During May 6–21, 2021, [7amleh documented a fifteen-fold increase in violent speech](https://7amleh.org/2022/09/27/statement-regarding-bsr-s-hra-for-meta-on-palestine-and-israel) targeting Palestinians compared with the same period in 2020, and across the escalation [Meta found on appeal that it had mistakenly taken down almost half of the Arabic-language content](https://meta.7amleh.org/about) its systems had removed — an error rate that, against the multi-year documentary record 7amleh had been building on Palestinian digital rights violations, made the case for an independent review from within Meta's own accountability architecture. In September 2021 the Meta Oversight Board [formally recommended](https://about.fb.com/news/2022/09/human-rights-impact-meta-israel-palestine/) that Meta commission an independent human-rights impact assessment of its content-moderation policies during the May 2021 escalation. Meta accepted and commissioned BSR, whose assessment proceeded through 2021 and 2022 with consultations across civil society, rights-holder, and stakeholder groups in Israel, Palestine, and internationally — including [7amleh](../organizations/org-7amleh.md), which had by then documented [almost 1,500 digital rights violations across 2021 and 2022, over 80% of which had occurred on Meta's platforms](https://meta.7amleh.org/about).

## BSR's findings

BSR's report, published 22 September 2022, found [over-enforcement of Arabic content compared with Hebrew](https://www.bsr.org/en/reports/meta-human-rights-israel-palestine) — Meta's automated systems had removed or restricted Arabic-language content at a disproportionate rate while under-enforcing community-standards violations in Hebrew. BSR found this asymmetric enforcement [produced adverse human-rights implications for Palestinians](https://www.bsr.org/en/reports/meta-human-rights-israel-palestine) across four rights: freedom of expression, freedom of assembly, political participation, and non-discrimination. The report's 21 recommendations spanned Meta's content-moderation policies, their enforcement, and transparency — including reevaluation of the Dangerous Organizations and Individuals (DOI) policy, investment in more precise Hebrew and Arabic language classifiers, enhanced transparency on government content-removal requests, and ongoing human-rights impact assessments.

BSR also identified areas of good practice, including Meta's establishment of a dedicated Special Operations Center with regional experts and native Arabic and Hebrew speakers.

## Meta's response

[Meta committed to fully implementing 10 of BSR's 21 recommendations](https://about.fb.com/news/2022/09/human-rights-impact-meta-israel-palestine/), partially implementing 4, and assessing the feasibility of 6 others. The one recommendation Meta declined was to fund public research exploring the relationship between counter-terrorism obligations and social media platforms. Among the accepted commitments: developing a Hebrew hostile speech machine-learning classifier, reviewing incitement and dangerous organisation policies, hiring additional content reviewers with diverse dialect capabilities, and expanding transparency in government content-removal reporting.

## Civil society response: the joint statement of 27 September 2022

On 27 September 2022, [7amleh and more than 60 civil society organisations](https://7amleh.org/2022/09/27/statement-regarding-bsr-s-hra-for-meta-on-palestine-and-israel) — including [Human Rights Watch](../organizations/org-human-rights-watch.md), [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md), [Article 19](../organizations/org-article-19.md), Amnesty International, the Electronic Frontier Foundation, Al-Haq, Jewish Voice for Peace, and a wide coalition of Palestinian and international civil-society groups — issued a joint statement responding to the BSR findings and Meta's implementation announcement. The statement commended the BSR review as a step in the right direction while pressing Meta to implement all 21 recommendations in full, with a transparent implementation timeline aligned with the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights.

The signatories [contested BSR's characterisation of the bias as "unintentional"](https://www.hrw.org/news/2022/09/27/statement-regarding-bsrs-hra-meta-palestine-israel), arguing that "even if the bias started out as unintentional, after knowing about the issues for years and not taking appropriate action, the unintentional became intentional." They also highlighted that [Meta had historically complied with approximately 90% of Israeli government takedown requests](https://7amleh.org/2022/09/27/statement-regarding-bsr-s-hra-for-meta-on-palestine-and-israel), framing the BSR audit within a wider pattern of asymmetric platform responsiveness to state pressure. [Marwa Fatafta](../persons/person-marwa-fatafta.md) of [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md), whose MENA Policy and Advocacy work had produced much of the pre-BSR documentation of Meta's Arabic-content bias, put the significance directly: ["Meta's censorship of Palestinian content is fundamentally biased. Now we have the receipts to prove it."](https://www.accessnow.org/press-release/bsr-findings-meta-palestinian-rights/)

The joint statement's five collective demands established the accountability framework that would define subsequent civil-society engagement: full implementation of all 21 recommendations with a detailed timeline; complete transparency on Israeli government content-removal requests and responses; creation of a Hebrew-language hate speech lexicon; establishment of a co-design process with civil society; and adherence to the UN Guiding Principles in all implementation work.

## Significance

The September 2022 BSR event is the corpus's principal documented instance of a third-party human-rights due diligence audit finding asymmetric treatment of Arabic and Hebrew content on a major platform — the first time an independent body commissioned by Meta itself produced a formal finding of over-enforcement of Palestinian and Arabic-language content, on the public record, with a set of recommendations to which Meta committed a structured response. For [7amleh](../organizations/org-7amleh.md), the event is the moment at which the multi-year documentary record it had been building — almost 1,500 violations, over 80% on Meta's platforms — was independently corroborated by an audit Meta had commissioned and could not dispute on procedural grounds.

The BSR event is the pre-October 2023 anchor of the broader campaign entered in the corpus as the [7amleh and the international civil-society campaign on Meta's content moderation of Palestinian content (2023–ongoing)](../campaigns/camp-7amleh-meta-palestinian-content-moderation-2023-ongoing.md). The 60+ signatory coalition of 27 September 2022 is structurally the same coalition — 7amleh, HRW, Access Now, Article 19, Amnesty International, EFF — that would anchor the post-October 2023 intensification, and the five demands of the September 2022 joint statement are the policy framework that the wartime campaign would document Meta's continued failure to deliver on. The BSR event's importance to the corpus is therefore both intrinsic — as the field's authoritative pre-war audit — and structural: the documentary and coalition foundation on which the post-October 2023 campaign built.
