# GARDA Indonesia national ojol fare-algorithm protest and strike (29 August 2024)

On 29 August 2024, [Gabungan Aksi Roda Dua Indonesia (GARDA)](../organizations/org-garda-indonesia.md) — Indonesia's principal association of app-based motorcycle taxi drivers — led a national one-day strike and protest action in Jakarta, with drivers [suspending all ride-hailing, food delivery, and courier services](https://www.thejakartapost.com/business/2024/08/28/protesting-drivers-to-suspend-online-delivery-ride-hailing-services.html) on the day. Over 1,000 drivers assembled at three sites: Merdeka Palace (the Presidential Palace), Gojek's head office in Petojo (Central Jakarta), and Grab's office in Cilandak (South Jakarta). The Indonesian Transport Workers Union (Serikat Pekerja Angkutan Indonesia, SPAI) co-organised the action alongside GARDA. The protest — one of the first mass national actions in GARDA's escalating campaign against platform commission structures — placed the opacity of algorithmically-set fares at the centre of its public demands.

## The structural grievance: algorithmic fare-setting

The central issue driving the protest was the role platform algorithms play in determining driver compensation. In Indonesia's ride-hailing sector, fare amounts and the commission share retained by platforms (Gojek, Grab, Maxim, Shopee, inDrive, Lalamove, Borzo) are set algorithmically by each company, with no regulatory floor and no driver-negotiated standard. [GARDA](../organizations/org-garda-indonesia.md) characterised this arrangement as structurally exploitative: [competing platforms algorithmically undercut fares in a race to the bottom](https://en.tempo.co/read/1909850/online-ojek-drivers-to-protest-welfare-at-merdeka-palace-and-gojek-grab-offices), reducing driver earnings while platform commission percentages held firm. A driver's protest placard condensed the relationship in terms the public could recognise: ["Driver take hikmah (wisdom), applicator takes as many pieces as possible"](https://www.thejakartapost.com/business/2024/08/30/app-based-ojek-drivers-strike-over-low-pay.html) — meaning the algorithm decides how each fare is carved up, and the platform wins every time.

The absence of government regulation compounded the algorithmic dynamic. Because no Indonesian ministry claimed binding authority over platform commission structures, drivers had no institutional avenue to contest fare-setting decisions. One protesting driver reported working ten-hour shifts but earning less than Rp 150,000 (approximately US $9.73) on most days — substantially below Jakarta's minimum wage — because fare levels and platform fees together stripped enough value from each completed order to push a full working day below the statutory floor.

## Demands

GARDA's [five-point demand package](https://en.tempo.co/read/1909850/online-ojek-drivers-to-protest-welfare-at-merdeka-palace-and-gojek-grab-offices) addressed both the algorithmic and structural dimensions of the gig-work relationship:

1. **Government-enforced fair tariff structure.** GARDA demanded that the government set a minimum fare floor for ojol trips, replacing platform-controlled algorithmic pricing with a regulated standard from which earnings could not be undercut. Igun Wicaksono, GARDA's General Chairman (Ketua Umum), framed the government's role as that of regulatory enforcer: "We want the government to determine a fair price or tariff for online motorcycle taxis, and be a regulatory enforcer and impose sanctions on any platform that harms online motorcycle taxi drivers."
2. **Commission reduction.** Drivers sought a greater share of each fare's revenue, targeting the platform commission structure and demanding a ceiling on the percentage platforms could extract from every completed order.
3. **Protection against arbitrary deactivation.** Platforms' ability to unilaterally suspend, postpone, or terminate driver partnerships — often through algorithmic performance-scoring mechanisms operating without transparent criteria or driver recourse — was identified as a core structural grievance. GARDA demanded removal of these unilateral options from platform terms.
4. **Social security and labour rights.** Demands included accident insurance, health coverage, and formal recognition of drivers' right to form labour unions and engage in collective bargaining — standard protections unavailable to gig workers classified as independent contractors rather than employees.
5. **Women workers' rights.** SPAI co-organiser Lily Pujiati raised protections specific to women drivers: paid leave for pregnancy, childbirth, and miscarriage; access to childcare; and breastfeeding support in the workplace — a dimension of gig-worker organising that rarely surfaces in coverage of Indonesian platform-labour protests.

## The action and its reception

[Drivers ceased accepting orders](https://www.thejakartapost.com/business/2024/08/28/protesting-drivers-to-suspend-online-delivery-ride-hailing-services.html) across Greater Jakarta for the duration of the day, withdrawing from all ride-hailing, food delivery, and package delivery orders. The three Jakarta protest sites drew over a thousand drivers; the action also spread to other Indonesian cities, establishing a nationally coordinated character that distinguished it from previous localised driver demonstrations. The service withdrawal generated social media complaints about disruption — reflecting both the extent of daily dependency on ojol services and the concrete leverage a coordinated work stoppage gave GARDA in putting platform practices before a non-driver public.

Platform responses were non-committal. [Gojek stated](https://www.thejakartapost.com/business/2024/08/30/app-based-ojek-drivers-strike-over-low-pay.html) that its operations continued normally and that it remained "open to drivers' input." [Grab responded](https://www.thejakartapost.com/business/2024/08/30/app-based-ojek-drivers-strike-over-low-pay.html) that its tariffs were calibrated to balance stable demand with driver earnings. Neither platform made public commitments to alter commission structures. Government response was similarly non-committal: the Transportation Ministry declined regulatory authority over platform fee structures and urged platforms to address driver concerns directly; the Manpower Ministry gave no immediate comment.

## Significance

The August 2024 protest was the first major national public action in what became GARDA's sustained escalating campaign against platform algorithmic commission structures — the action that translated GARDA's membership reach into visible government-facing street pressure. It established the demand template — government-regulated fare floor, commission cap, labour protections, anti-deactivation safeguards — that GARDA would carry forward through subsequent demonstrations at increasing scale: Aksi 205 (May 2025, coordinated multi-city strikes), Aksi 217 (July 2025, 50,000+ drivers at the Presidential Palace), and the September 2025 DPR demonstration. The eventual achievement of Perpres 27/2026 — capping platform commissions at 8%, down from up to 20% — is traceable to the mobilisation energy this protest helped build.

For the corpus's make-AI-good frame, the August 2024 action is significant as a protest in which algorithmic fare-setting — not employment status or data rights alone — was the named public grievance. Indonesian gig workers did not argue their data was misused or that they were misclassified; they argued that the algorithms themselves set prices unjustly, at the expense of drivers and in favour of platforms, in the absence of any regulatory accountability. That framing — platform algorithms as an instrument of labour extraction requiring public regulatory response — is a distinct register in the broader movement against algorithmic management, and the corpus's first entry in it from Southeast Asia.
