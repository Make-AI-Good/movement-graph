# Daniel Motaung's constitutional petition against Meta and Sama filed in Nairobi (10 May 2022)

On Tuesday 10 May 2022 the South African former Facebook content moderator [Daniel Motaung](../persons/person-daniel-motaung.md), represented by [Mercy Mutemi](../persons/person-mercy-mutemi.md) of [Nzili & Sumbi Advocates](../organizations/org-nzili-sumbi-advocates.md), [filed Petition E071 of 2022](https://new.kenyalaw.org/akn/ke/judgment/keelrc/2023/320/eng@2023-02-06) at the Milimani Law Courts in Nairobi — naming Samasource Kenya EPZ Limited t/a Sama as 1st Respondent, Meta Platforms Inc. as 2nd Respondent, and Meta Platforms Ireland Ltd as 3rd Respondent — and the UK tech-justice community-interest company [Foxglove](../organizations/org-foxglove.md) issued a same-day public [launch post](https://www.foxglove.org.uk/2022/05/10/motaung-facebook-world-first/) framing the petition as a "world-first" lawsuit to force Facebook to put the health and wellbeing of its content moderators ahead of profit. The petition is the [originating case of the Foxglove–Nzili & Sumbi Kenya docket](../campaigns/camp-foxglove-motaung-meta-sama-kenya.md) — the first major lawsuit brought against Meta in an African court — and the moment at which the labour underpinnings of frontier AI products were put on the Kenyan public record under the constitutional-petition vehicle that has since carried the [185 former moderators' parallel suit](../organizations/org-nzili-sumbi-advocates.md), the [Tigray hate-speech petition](../organizations/org-nzili-sumbi-advocates.md), and the [July 2023 ChatGPT/Sama parliamentary petition](../organizations/org-nzili-sumbi-advocates.md).

## Context

The 10 May 2022 filing was the end of a three-year arc and the start of a four-year-plus litigation. [Motaung had been recruited from South Africa to Nairobi in March 2019](https://www.opendemocracy.net/en/5050/whistleblower-daniel-motaung-facebook-kenya-court-case/) to moderate Zulu-language Facebook content for Samasource — Meta's principal sub-Saharan content-moderation vendor at the time — and had been fired in 2019 after attempting to organise the Alliance, an in-facility workers' organisation pressing for mental-health support, fair pay, and the right to unionise. From exile in South Africa he had spent the following years as the public whistleblower on Sama's Nairobi operation, including in [TIME magazine's February 2022 "Inside Facebook's African Sweatshop" exposé](https://www.business-humanrights.org/en/latest-news/meta-sama-lawsuit-re-poor-working-conditions-human-trafficking-kenya/), and had been working since 2019 with [Foxglove](https://www.foxglove.org.uk/2022/05/10/motaung-facebook-world-first/) to develop a legal vehicle by which Meta could be brought to court for the conditions under which Facebook content moderation was outsourced in Africa. The petition was [preceded by a 6 April 2022 pre-action letter](https://www.business-humanrights.org/en/latest-news/meta-sama-lawsuit-re-poor-working-conditions-human-trafficking-kenya/) setting out twelve workplace-conditions demands; Meta and Sama did not concede those demands, and the petition was filed thirty-four days later.

## What was filed on 10 May 2022

The petition was lodged in the Employment and Labour Relations Court at Nairobi and [docketed as Petition E071 of 2022](https://new.kenyalaw.org/akn/ke/judgment/keelrc/2023/320/eng@2023-02-06). Its substantive thesis was that Meta could not contract out of its responsibility for the conditions of Sama's Nairobi annotators: the platform's commercial benefit, the work's design, and the moderators' pipeline of harm sat with Meta as much as with the outsourcing contractor that nominally employed them. The pleading framed the conditions complained of as breaches of Kenyan constitutional protections of [freedom of association and expression, dignity and privacy, fair remuneration, and reasonable working conditions](https://www.business-humanrights.org/en/latest-news/meta-sama-lawsuit-re-poor-working-conditions-human-trafficking-kenya/), and as ["forced labour and modern slavery"](https://www.foxglove.org.uk/2022/05/10/motaung-facebook-world-first/) under Kenyan law. The human-trafficking claim was grounded in the contention that Sama's [recruitment ads in southern Africa had been misleading](https://www.business-humanrights.org/en/latest-news/meta-sama-lawsuit-re-poor-working-conditions-human-trafficking-kenya/) about the nature of the work — drawing applicants to Nairobi without disclosing that the role was the moderation of beheading videos and other extreme content under what the petition characterised as inadequate psychological care.

The reliefs sought were operational as well as monetary. [Foxglove's launch post](https://www.foxglove.org.uk/2022/05/10/motaung-facebook-world-first/) set out the four categories the petition asked the court to order: that Meta bring in mental-health support for outsourced moderators "equivalent to the system in place for Facebook's directly employed workers, as well as an equivalent pay scale"; that Meta and Sama "end their illegal union-busting activity, publicly acknowledge moderators' right to form a union and post physical notices in the workplace making this clear"; that they "appoint an independent human rights and psychological care consultants to conduct an internal audit of the Nairobi office to report to the court within 30 days with recommendations on how to end the toxic working environment"; and that they "pay wages that were unlawfully kept from content moderators, as well as pay each former and existing moderator damages and provide funds for them to find and pay for mental health support."

Mutemi held a [press conference at the Milimani Law Courts](https://www.context.news/big-tech/respect-african-content-moderators-says-facebook-whistleblower) on the day of filing, telling reporters that Motaung had sued Meta and Sama [on his own behalf and on behalf of all current and former content moderators](https://www.corpwatch.org/article/facebook-sued-over-mental-health-trauma-content-moderators-kenya). [Cori Crider](../persons/person-cori-crider.md), then a director of Foxglove, framed the case in its sectoral register: ["We can't have safe social media if the workers who protect us toil in a digital sweatshop"](https://www.corpwatch.org/article/facebook-sued-over-mental-health-trauma-content-moderators-kenya). Crider also signalled the geographic ambition the case was being launched with — Sama's Nairobi office was, at the time, Facebook's moderation hub for much of East and South Africa, and the petitioner side hoped a Kenyan ruling would "send ripples across the continent — and the world." Motaung gave the [accompanying press materials](https://www.corpwatch.org/article/facebook-sued-over-mental-health-trauma-content-moderators-kenya) the personal register the case would carry forward: "I went to Kenya because I wanted to change my life. I wanted to change the life of my family. Six months later, my physical and mental health had been destroyed."

## Significance

The 10 May 2022 filing is the corpus's first mapped Event of an African worker-plaintiff bringing a constitutional-employment lawsuit against a US platform over the conditions of outsourced AI-adjacent labour. Three structural features of the filing have shaped what followed. The first is the [respondent structure](https://new.kenyalaw.org/akn/ke/judgment/keelrc/2023/320/eng@2023-02-06) — naming Meta Platforms Inc. and Meta Platforms Ireland Ltd as the 2nd and 3rd Respondents alongside Sama — which set up the jurisdictional fight that consumed the case's first two and a half years and produced the corpus's most cited African appellate ruling on platform-side amenability to suit, the [20 September 2024 Nairobi Court of Appeal decision](https://www.foxglove.org.uk/2024/09/23/facebook-content-moderators-kenya-meta-appeal/) that allowed both this petition and the parallel 185-moderators suit to proceed to trial in Kenya. The second is the [pairing of actors](https://www.foxglove.org.uk/2022/05/10/motaung-facebook-world-first/) the petition put on the public record — a Nairobi fee-earning firm (Nzili & Sumbi) as counsel of record under Mutemi's tech-law practice, a London community-interest company (Foxglove) as international partner supplying communications and strategy, and an individual worker-plaintiff (Motaung) whose own organising lineage gave the case both an evidentiary base and a public-facing identity — which has since been replicated in the firm's [Tigray petition](../organizations/org-nzili-sumbi-advocates.md), the [185 former moderators' suit](../organizations/org-nzili-sumbi-advocates.md), and the [ChatGPT/Sama parliamentary petition](../organizations/org-nzili-sumbi-advocates.md). The third is the [framing of the labour conditions](https://www.foxglove.org.uk/2022/05/10/motaung-facebook-world-first/) as forced labour and modern slavery under Kenyan constitutional protection — the legal-pleading vocabulary that became the public-record anchor for Motaung's wider organising and was carried, a year later, into the founding-vote address at the [Nairobi content-moderation summit](https://www.foxglove.org.uk/2023/05/15/nairobi-content-moderation-summit/) at which more than 150 African moderators voted to establish the African Content Moderators Union.

In the immediate weeks after the filing, the petition's public-facing arm bore out the "world-first" framing in two ways. Meta's response was to apply for a gag order against Motaung, which a Nairobi judge [refused](https://www.foxglove.org.uk/2022/07/30/people-power-pays-off/) after a cross-jurisdictional coalition push organised by Foxglove and [People vs Big Tech](https://peoplevsbig.tech/stop-facebook-from-silencing-whistleblower-daniel-motaung/); and the petition itself drew international press attention as the first sustained public test of platform accountability for outsourced AI-adjacent labour in Africa. In broader corpus terms, the 10 May 2022 filing is the moment at which a labour-side worker-plaintiff case-opening Event — distinct from the artist-side [Andersen v. Stability AI class-action complaint](event-andersen-v-stability-ai-class-action-filing-2023-01-13.md) eight months later, and from the UK welfare-side [GMCDP / Foxglove DWP launch](event-foxglove-gmcdp-dwp-case-launch-2021-12-01.md) six months earlier — entered the corpus's record of how the make-AI-good movement opens its strategic litigation, and it sits as the lineage event from which the broader African content-moderation organising cluster the corpus tracks has measured its own field.
