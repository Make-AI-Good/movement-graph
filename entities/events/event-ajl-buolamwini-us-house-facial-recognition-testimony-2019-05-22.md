# Joy Buolamwini testifies before House Oversight Committee on facial recognition (22 May 2019)

On 22 May 2019, [Joy Buolamwini](../persons/person-joy-buolamwini.md), founder and president of the [Algorithmic Justice League](../organizations/org-algorithmic-justice-league.md), testified before the United States House Committee on Oversight and Reform at the first congressional hearing dedicated to the civil-rights and civil-liberties implications of facial recognition technology. The hearing — "Facial Recognition Technology (Part 1): Its Impact on Our Civil Rights and Liberties," convened under Committee Chair Rep. Elijah Cummings — marked the entry of [AJL's](../organizations/org-algorithmic-justice-league.md) Gender Shades research into the formal US legislative record and was one of the first times the US Congress heard directly from an algorithmic-auditing researcher about the disparate-impact failures of commercially deployed facial recognition.

## Context

The testimony came roughly a year after Buolamwini and [Timnit Gebru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timnit_Gebru) published the [Gender Shades](https://gs.ajl.org/) study, which had audited commercial facial-analysis products from IBM, Microsoft, and Megvii and documented dramatic accuracy disparities by skin type and gender. The study's findings had already prompted IBM to update its product and had [seeded regulatory and corporate response](https://www.ajl.org/about), but no formal US federal legislative process had yet addressed the findings. The Oversight Committee hearing in May 2019 was the first congressional panel to convene witnesses specifically on the civil-rights dimension of the technology.

Six witnesses appeared: Buolamwini (AJL / MIT Media Lab), Clare Garvie (Georgetown University Law Center's Center on Privacy and Technology), Cedric Alexander (former president of the National Organization of Black Law Enforcement Executives), Michael Punke (Amazon's Vice President of Global Public Policy), [Neema Guliani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ACLU) (ACLU, Senior Legislative Counsel), and Andrew Ferguson (law professor, University of the District of Columbia).

## Buolamwini's testimony

Buolamwini's [written testimony](https://docs.house.gov/meetings/GO/GO00/20190522/109521/HHRG-116-GO00-Wstate-BuolamwiniJ-20190522.pdf), submitted under the title "Face: The Final Frontier of Privacy," opened with the personal origin of her research: as a graduate student she had been [unable to get facial-detection software to register her face until she put on a white mask](https://medium.com/@Joy.Buolamwini/face-the-final-frontier-of-privacy-full-spoken-congressional-testimony-may-22-2019-ff8607df045b). She presented the core finding from Gender Shades: IBM's facial-analysis system was [incorrect 34.7% of the time for darker-skinned women](https://www.theregister.com/2019/05/22/congress_facial_recognition), versus an error rate of under 1% for lighter-skinned men — a gap she attributed to training datasets that were 75% male and 80% lighter-skinned, which she named "the gold standard benchmark dataset is biased and can lead to a false understanding of progress."

She illustrated downstream consequences with two cases. First, Amazon's Rekognition system had [failed to correctly identify Oprah Winfrey's face, labelling her as male](https://medium.com/@Joy.Buolamwini/face-the-final-frontier-of-privacy-full-spoken-congressional-testimony-may-22-2019-ff8607df045b) — a failure of commercial software widely used in law-enforcement contexts. Second, a Brown University student had been [misidentified as a terrorism suspect](https://medium.com/@Joy.Buolamwini/face-the-final-frontier-of-privacy-full-spoken-congressional-testimony-may-22-2019-ff8607df045b) and subsequently received death threats, illustrating the real-world harm of false-positive identification in criminal-intelligence contexts.

Buolamwini's three-part policy ask was specific: (1) a **moratorium on law-enforcement use** of facial recognition and other facial-analysis technologies, pending adoption of regulations developed in partnership with affected communities; (2) mandatory **disclosure by all federal agencies and federally funded organisations** of any current use of face-based technologies; and (3) stricter **consent requirements** for biometric data collection by commercial platforms. The ACLU's Guliani made aligned recommendations, framing the moratorium as necessary given "the capacity for abuse, lack of oversight, and technical immaturity."

## Significance

The May 2019 hearing was the first US congressional hearing dedicated to the civil-rights impact of facial recognition and the first time Gender Shades-derived findings appeared in the congressional record. It established Buolamwini and AJL as a recognised authority before the US Congress on algorithmic bias — a posture the organisation has continued to hold in subsequent US and international AI-governance processes. The hearing directly preceded a wave of municipal and federal legislative activity on facial recognition: San Francisco's city-wide ban on government use of the technology passed two weeks later, in June 2019, and the US Senate and House introduced the [Commercial Facial Recognition Privacy Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/116th-congress/senate-bill/847) and related legislation in the same congressional session.

For the corpus's algorithmic-accountability movement mapping, the event is the first US congressional-testimony event — the moment at which a research-to-policy pathway the corpus is tracking crossed into the formal legislative arena — and the primary record of AJL's early legislative-advocacy posture.
