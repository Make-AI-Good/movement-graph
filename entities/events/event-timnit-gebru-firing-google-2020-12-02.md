# Timnit Gebru forced from Google Ethical AI team (2 December 2020)

On 2 December 2020, Google cut off [Timnit Gebru](../persons/person-timnit-gebru.md)'s corporate email access — an action Google described as accepting her resignation and Gebru described as an unannounced firing — ending her two-year co-leadership of the company's Ethical AI research team. The immediate trigger was a dispute over a research paper she had co-authored on the risks of large language models. The departure, and the events it catalysed over the following year, constitute the most publicly visible research-ethics crisis in the AI industry's history and the founding moment of the independent algorithmic-accountability research infrastructure that grew outside Big Tech through 2021–2023.

## Background

Gebru had joined Google in 2018 as co-lead, alongside Margaret Mitchell, of the Ethical AI team — a research unit tasked with investigating the social harms of AI systems from inside one of the field's dominant players. She brought credentials from the 2018 [*Gender Shades*](../publications/pub-gender-shades.md) audit with [Joy Buolamwini](../persons/person-joy-buolamwini.md) (the first rigorous benchmarking of commercial facial-recognition systems' disparate accuracy across race and gender), and a reputation for methodology-forward work that engaged communities affected by AI systems rather than only technical peers.

In late 2020, Gebru was co-authoring [*On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big?*](../publications/pub-stochastic-parrots.md) — a paper arguing that the AI industry's race to train ever-larger language models on uncurated internet text carried under-acknowledged environmental costs, embedded structural bias from opaque training corpora, crowded out more grounded research approaches, and produced systems whose fluency masked a fundamental absence of meaning. The paper's co-authors included Emily M. Bender (University of Washington), Angelina McMillan-Major, and Margaret Mitchell; all four names were on the Google-internal draft at the time Google management requested it be retracted.

## The departure

In early December 2020, Megan Kacholia, a Google engineering vice-president, [sent Gebru an email](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timnit_Gebru) demanding that she either withdraw the Stochastic Parrots paper from its pending conference submission or remove the names of all Google-affiliated authors. Gebru responded listing conditions under which she would consider either step — conditions Google declined to engage — and separately sent an email to the Google Brain Women and Allies internal listserv [describing the institutional pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timnit_Gebru) she had encountered. On 2 December 2020, Google terminated her corporate email access. Google AI head Jeff Dean publicly characterised the decision as finding the paper had [not met Google's publication bar](https://www.technologyreview.com/2020/12/04/1013294/google-ai-ethics-research-paper-forced-out-timnit-gebru/) and framed Google's action as accepting a resignation; Gebru maintained she had offered no resignation and was fired without warning.

Within days of the departure becoming public, [over a thousand Google employees](https://www.technologyreview.com/2020/12/04/1013294/google-ai-ethics-research-paper-forced-out-timnit-gebru/) signed a letter demanding an explanation and institutional changes, joined by thousands of researchers across academia and industry. Samy Bengio, Google Brain co-founder and Gebru's manager, publicly stated he stood by her; he resigned from Google approximately eighteen months later. The Stochastic Parrots paper was eventually [published at the 2021 ACM FAccT conference](../publications/pub-stochastic-parrots.md) — with Mitchell publishing under a pseudonym — after Google did not succeed in preventing its submission.

## Margaret Mitchell's termination

On 19 February 2021, [Google terminated Margaret Mitchell](https://techcrunch.com/2021/02/19/google-fires-top-ai-ethics-researcher-margaret-mitchell/) — Gebru's co-lead and Stochastic Parrots co-author — citing code-of-conduct and security-policy violations. Mitchell had used automated scripts to search Google's internal servers for evidence of the institutional treatment that had preceded Gebru's departure. Google's public statement named the policy violations as the grounds; Mitchell stated publicly that her effort to document mistreatment and support Gebru's account was central to what she had been doing and that she had been fired for it. She joined Hugging Face in August 2021.

## DAIR and the researcher exodus

On 2 December 2021 — exactly one year after her Google exit — [Timnit Gebru](../persons/person-timnit-gebru.md) [announced the founding of the Distributed AI Research Institute](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2021/12/02/timnit-gebru-dair/) ([DAIR](../organizations/org-dair-institute.md)) with $3.7 million in initial funding from the Ford Foundation, MacArthur Foundation, Kapor Center, Open Society Foundation, and Rockefeller Foundation. The anniversary date was deliberate: DAIR's founding was framed explicitly as the institutional form that the work Google had suppressed required — an independent, community-rooted research institute structurally outside the incentive architecture of Big Tech. In February 2023, Alex Hanna and Dylan Baker, both from Google's Ethical AI team, joined [DAIR](../organizations/org-dair-institute.md) as Director of Research and Lead Research Engineer respectively, extending the continuity between the departure and the independent institution it produced.

## Significance

The Gebru departure is the corpus's clearest single instance of a Big Tech research-autonomy crisis producing an independent movement institution as its direct output. The Maven employee petition (April 2018) established the model of collective employee action targeting AI development decisions; the Gebru departure extended that terrain into the research domain, making visible the structural tension between in-house AI ethics research — whose credibility depends on an independence the employment relation forecloses — and the companies that sponsor it.

For the make-AI-good movement's subsequent trajectory, the event accomplished several things simultaneously: it demonstrated that Big Tech ethics research units were structurally inadequate as the primary venue for algorithmic-accountability work; it gave [DAIR](../organizations/org-dair-institute.md) a founding narrative with substantial public reach; and it shifted [*Stochastic Parrots*](../publications/pub-stochastic-parrots.md) — whose four-risk framing of LLM development became the core academic vocabulary for LLM-critique organising — from a conference paper into a movement document whose suppression attempt had been publicly documented. The subsequent wave of AI ethics researcher departures from Big Tech labs, the founding of independent algorithmic-accountability organisations, and the civil-society coalition's LLM-critique framing in the EU AI Act campaign each trace a line through the events of December 2020.
