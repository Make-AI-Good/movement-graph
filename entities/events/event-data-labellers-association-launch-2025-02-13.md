# Launch of the Data Labellers Association, Nairobi (13 February 2025)

On **Thursday 13 February 2025**, an [in-person launch programme in Nairobi with a parallel online livestream](https://data-workers.org/DLA/) marked the founding of the [Data Labellers Association](../organizations/org-data-labellers-association.md) (DLA) — the first worker-led representative body in Africa, and one of the first anywhere, organising the data labellers and annotators whose hand-classified examples underwrite the training of commercial AI systems. The launch was held under the theme "Empowering the People Powering AI" and installed [Joan Kinyua as president, Ephantus Kanyugi as vice-president, and Michael Geoffrey Abuyabo Asia as secretary](https://www.computerweekly.com/news/366619321/Kenyan-AI-workers-form-Data-Labelers-Association) of an association that [drew 339 members in its first week](https://www.computerweekly.com/news/366619321/Kenyan-AI-workers-form-Data-Labelers-Association) and would [grow to roughly eight hundred members within months](https://www.martijnarets.com/en/blog-en/data-labelers-association-speaks-up-for-invisible-workers-ultimately-its-about-respect-and-human-decency/) of the launch day. From its first public moment the DLA positioned itself as ["a continental and transnational space for all data workers"](https://datasyn.substack.com/p/invisible-hands-visible-futures-the) rather than a Kenya-only association.

## Context

The launch day was the public surfacing of an organising effort that had begun [informally in late 2023](https://www.martijnarets.com/en/blog-en/data-labelers-association-speaks-up-for-invisible-workers-ultimately-its-about-respect-and-human-decency/) inside the Kenyan data-annotation workforce, around an initial nucleus of ten workers experienced in the platforms supplying training data to the major commercial AI systems. The labour conditions the association was formed to challenge were on the public record in three converging registers by the time of the launch: in long-form journalism documenting [the wage-decline arc from roughly ten US dollars an hour to as little as two US dollars for three hours of labour, webcam-based monitoring, and repeated exposure to nude, child, and deceased-person imagery](https://www.martijnarets.com/en/blog-en/data-labelers-association-speaks-up-for-invisible-workers-ultimately-its-about-respect-and-human-decency/); in [Joan Kinyua's own first-person testimony](https://www.bmz-digital.global/en/5-questions-joan-kinyua/) drawn from over five years of data-labelling work for projects ranging across self-driving cars, medical diagnostics, and content moderation; and in the [twenty-one-month-old constitutional petitions against Meta and its outsourcers in Kenya](../events/event-african-content-moderators-union-founding-vote-2023-05-01.md) that had moved the conditions of the wider AI supply chain in Nairobi onto the country's High Court docket.

The DLA's launch also sat inside the [Africa Tech Workers Movement](../organizations/org-africa-tech-workers-movement.md) framing that the previous twenty-two months had built around the [African Content Moderators Union's founding vote of 1 May 2023](../events/event-african-content-moderators-union-founding-vote-2023-05-01.md). The ACMU's founding had organised the content-moderation layer of the AI supply chain in Nairobi from below; the DLA's launch in February 2025 extended that worker-controlled architecture upstream into the data-labelling and annotation layer, completing the sectoral coverage of the Africa Tech Workers Movement's four-body constituency (ACMU for content moderators, the DLA for data labellers and annotators, [Techworker Community Africa](../organizations/org-techworker-community-africa.md) for the broader Kenyan tech workforce, and African Tech Workers Rising for cross-platform organising). Kinyua, in the months running up to the launch, had [represented the still-forming DLA at an International Labour Conference preparatory meeting in Naivasha in December 2024](https://www.bmz-digital.global/en/5-questions-joan-kinyua/) and had begun the association's submissions on Kenya's [Business Laws (Amendment) Bill 2024](https://www.bmz-digital.global/en/5-questions-joan-kinyua/) — the bill that thirty-five Kenyan tech workers would shortly take to the High Court — so the 13 February launch day was a formal surfacing of a body that was already operating inside Kenyan policy fora.

## The 13 February launch day

The [launch was held in Nairobi with a parallel online livestream](https://data-workers.org/DLA/) so that data labellers and supporters outside Kenya could participate. The day's stated theme — ["Empowering the People Powering AI"](https://data-workers.org/DLA/) — set the framing on which the launch programme's substantive content turned: that AI systems, on the public-facing register the launch was installing, are built and maintained by a workforce whose labour has been treated as invisible and whose conditions have until then been left out of the international AI-policy conversation. The framing has carried forward as the association's standing mission language on its [own organisational site](https://datalabelers.org/) — "Empowering the Human Workforce Behind AI" — and as the version Kinyua used at the launch itself: ["AI does not exist on its own. Behind every algorithm, every dataset, and every technological advancement, there is invisible labor."](https://data-workers.org/DLA/)

Two formal Memoranda of Understanding were signed at the launch, both with Kenyan civic-tech partners: [one with Pollicy](https://data-workers.org/DLA/) — the Kampala- and Nairobi-anchored feminist data and policy organisation already present in the corpus — and [one with Siasa Place](https://data-workers.org/DLA/) — the youth-engagement civic organisation that has also supplied advocacy-research support to the [African Content Moderators Union](../organizations/org-african-content-moderators-union.md). The international supporters [named at launch](https://data-workers.org/DLA/) were the [Distributed AI Research Institute](../organizations/org-dair-institute.md), AI Now, Turkopticon (the worker-side project organising Amazon Mechanical Turk annotators), the Data Workers' Inquiry, Foxglove (the [UK tech-justice CIC](../organizations/org-foxglove.md) already supporting the broader Nairobi accountability stack), and content moderators in Essen, Germany, who sent a [pre-recorded solidarity message](https://data-workers.org/DLA/) into the launch programme — a small but unusual feature for a Kenyan worker-association launch and an early signal of the transnational orientation the DLA was claiming.

## Founding officers and demand set

The vote and acclamation on launch day installed a slate of three named officers around whom the association's first year of work would run: [Joan Kinyua as president](https://www.computerweekly.com/news/366619321/Kenyan-AI-workers-form-Data-Labelers-Association), a former Sama data labeller who had worked across self-driving-car, medical-diagnostic, and content-moderation projects since 2017; [Ephantus Kanyugi as vice-president](https://www.computerweekly.com/news/366619321/Kenyan-AI-workers-form-Data-Labelers-Association), a thirty-year-old former data trainer repeatedly credited as a co-founder in the founding-cohort coverage; and [Michael Geoffrey Abuyabo Asia as secretary](https://www.computerweekly.com/news/366619321/Kenyan-AI-workers-form-Data-Labelers-Association). The demand set [pressed at launch and carried into the press cycle around it](https://www.computerweekly.com/news/366619321/Kenyan-AI-workers-form-Data-Labelers-Association) was the now-canonical short list of the African worker-organising response to the AI supply chain — fair compensation in place of cents-per-task piece rates, formal employment contracts with clear terms, access to healthcare and pensions, mental-health support specific to content-moderation and annotation-imagery trauma, legal assistance with pay disputes and sudden account deactivations, and the right to collective bargaining — but the launch-day framing put the legal void in which Kenyan data labour operates at the centre. [Abuyabo Asia named that void directly](https://www.computerweekly.com/news/366619321/Kenyan-AI-workers-form-Data-Labelers-Association) in his launch-week press appearances: "A contract is supposed to be agreed within the confines of the law, but they know the law is not there, so it becomes a loophole they're utilising."

The DLA's [own published programme](https://data-workers.org/DLA/) at launch grouped the work into five strands — policy advocacy, mental-health support, civic education and mentorship, skills development, and litigation assistance — and the [longer-form profile published five months after launch](https://www.martijnarets.com/en/blog-en/data-labelers-association-speaks-up-for-invisible-workers-ultimately-its-about-respect-and-human-decency/) gathered the same activity into four working goals (awareness and community-building, policy change, mental health and training, and research mapping the workforce). The two framings are consistent, and both were visible in the launch programme.

## Significance

The 13 February 2025 launch is the corpus's first mapped Event of an African worker-controlled association coming into existence specifically around the data-labelling and annotation layer of the AI supply chain — the layer that sits upstream of content moderation and at the substantive heart of the training pipeline for the major commercial AI systems. Three features of the launch shape what follows from it in the corpus's record.

The first is the [transnational-by-design character](https://datasyn.substack.com/p/invisible-hands-visible-futures-the) of the association as constituted on launch day. Where the [African Content Moderators Union](../organizations/org-african-content-moderators-union.md) had been founded as the first continental moderators' union and built outward from a Kenyan starting point, the DLA's launch positioned the body from its first public moment as "a continental and transnational space for all data workers" — a framing reinforced by the Essen pre-recorded solidarity message and by the international-supporter list named at launch. The architectural commitment is to a body that organises the supply-chain layer wherever it operates, not only in Kenya.

The second is the [Africa Tech Workers Movement sectoral completion](../organizations/org-africa-tech-workers-movement.md) the launch accomplished. The ACMU's [1 May 2023 founding vote](../events/event-african-content-moderators-union-founding-vote-2023-05-01.md) had organised content moderators; the DLA's launch organised the data-labelling and annotation workforce; together with [Techworker Community Africa](../organizations/org-techworker-community-africa.md)'s broader-tech remit and African Tech Workers Rising's cross-platform organising, the four constituent bodies of the Movement now cover the principal sectoral layers of the Kenyan and pan-African AI supply chain. The DLA's launch is the moment at which that four-body architecture became operationally complete.

The third is the [launch-day mission frame itself](https://data-workers.org/DLA/) — that AI does not exist on its own and that the workforce powering it is the legitimate site of policy intervention. The frame has carried directly into the DLA's [first-year submissions to Kenyan labour legislation](https://www.bmz-digital.global/en/5-questions-joan-kinyua/) and into Kinyua's representations at international labour fora, and it provides the corpus with a clean register for the non-AI public being engaged by this work: not engineers or AI-policy professionals, but the data labellers themselves and the international labour-organising and civic-tech communities that have aligned with them. The launch is the moment at which that engagement received an organisational vehicle.
