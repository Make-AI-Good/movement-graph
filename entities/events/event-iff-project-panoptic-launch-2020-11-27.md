# Internet Freedom Foundation Project Panoptic launch (27 November 2020)

On [27 November 2020](https://www.lawctopus.com/internet-freedom-foundation-iff-launches-project-panoptic/) the [Internet Freedom Foundation](../organizations/org-internet-freedom-foundation.md) (IFF) publicly launched [Project Panoptic](https://panoptic.in/about), a public tracker of facial-recognition technology (FRT) procurement and deployment by central, state, and municipal authorities across India, built jointly with volunteers from DataKind Bengaluru and the Indian open-source software company Frappe Technologies. The launch was framed as the first comprehensive public database of Indian government facial-recognition procurement and deployment, paired with a public petition demanding an immediate government moratorium on FRT development and use and a crowdsourced citizen-reporting feature extending the dataset beyond what desk-research from public-procurement archives could surface. The launch event itself was [paired with a presentation](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202011/project-panoptic-launches-as-facial-recognition-use-by-delhi-police-expands) by Mutale Nkonde, founder and chief executive of the New York-based civil-rights organisation AI for the People, and was accompanied by an IFF call for volunteers to support the tracker's continuing work. The launch made public a dataset IFF and its volunteer collaborators had built over the preceding four months and gave the organisation the substantive backbone for the [multi-year campaign](../campaigns/camp-iff-project-panoptic.md) that the tracker has anchored since.

## The build that preceded the launch

The Project Panoptic dashboard was [built by a nine-volunteer DataKind Bengaluru team](https://medium.com/datakind-bangalore/tracing-the-trackers-c8419edfbe4) — a project manager, two Data Ambassadors, four Data Experts (software engineers and a data scientist architect), and one designer — working virtually over four months in 2020 alongside IFF's permanent staff to translate the patchwork of FRT-tender records scattered across Indian central and state procurement portals, vendor press releases, and budget line-items into a single, structured, state-by-state public dashboard. The build used a Python / Flask / Folium stack with a SQL backend before the dashboard was migrated onto Frappe Technologies' open-source platform ahead of launch, so IFF's permanent staff could maintain the dataset and the public site without ongoing volunteer engineering capacity. The build is recorded on DataKind Bengaluru's own case-study writeup as the chapter's first project to bring design expertise onto the team — a structural choice reflecting the public-facing register the launch needed to land, with the dashboard serving simultaneously as a civil-society reference dataset and as the campaign's principal public-communications artefact.

## What launched on 27 November 2020

The launch made public three connected artefacts. The first was [the Project Panoptic dashboard itself](https://panoptic.in/about) — a public-facing structured database of named Indian government FRT projects, with state-by-state breakdowns, named deploying authorities (police forces, transport authorities, municipal corporations, welfare-delivery agencies, and state and central government departments), named vendors, named budgets, and named stated purposes — accessible at panoptic.in. The second was the [public petition](https://panoptic.in/about) carried on the same site, calling on the Indian government to immediately put a moratorium on the development and use of FRT by government authorities until a primary-legislation framework with proportionality safeguards, judicial oversight, and independent regulation could be put in place. The third was the ["Report an FRT System" crowdsourced citizen-reporting feature](https://panoptic.in/about) — a public submission flow asking members of the public who had witnessed or learned of an FRT deployment in their city or state to submit it to the tracker for verification and inclusion. The three together constituted the campaign's launched repertoire: an authoritative reference dataset, a sustained public-facing demand, and a crowdsourced data-collection feedback loop that converted the wider Indian public from passive audience into the campaign's running evidence base.

## The launch-day presentation and the call for volunteers

The launch event was [paired with a presentation](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202011/project-panoptic-launches-as-facial-recognition-use-by-delhi-police-expands) by Mutale Nkonde, founder and chief executive of AI for the People — the New York-based civil-rights organisation Nkonde founded after her US federal-policy career and her 2019 work on the US federal Algorithmic Accountability Act — addressing the launch event on 27 November 2020. Nkonde's involvement on the launch programme is the launch's clearest public signal of the cross-jurisdictional civil-society register the campaign was being positioned to occupy: the launch staged the Indian tracker not as a domestic-only Indian civil-liberties artefact but as the South-Asian counterpart in an emerging international civil-society response to police and state facial-recognition deployment — a field [Algorithmic Justice League](../organizations/org-algorithmic-justice-league.md), [Big Brother Watch](../organizations/org-big-brother-watch.md), AI for the People, Amnesty International, and Article 19 were already working across at the time of the launch. Alongside the substantive launch IFF issued [a call for volunteers](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202011/project-panoptic-launches-as-facial-recognition-use-by-delhi-police-expands), inviting Indian researchers, lawyers, designers, and technologists to support the tracker's continuing work — a recruitment thread that has fed the project's volunteer base across its years of operation since.

## Contemporary deployment context

The launch landed against [a contemporary expansion](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202011/project-panoptic-launches-as-facial-recognition-use-by-delhi-police-expands) of the Delhi Police's facial-recognition deployment — at the time the most-publicised single police-FRT deployment in India and a named case on the launch dashboard. The Delhi Police's reported FRT systems were being deployed for crowd-surveillance during contentious protest moments in the national capital, including in the policing context around the protests against the Citizenship Amendment Act and the 2020 Delhi communal violence, with the deployment's reported expansion through late 2020 supplying the substantive backdrop the launch was responding to. The launch dashboard's wider deployment record stretched well beyond Delhi — to police forces in other Indian states, transport-authority deployments in metropolitan rail and airport systems, welfare-delivery and identity-verification deployments interfacing with Aadhaar, and municipal-corporation CCTV-and-FRT integration projects — and the campaign's state-by-state framing was the substantive shape that the launch made legible to the Indian public for the first time.

## Significance for the corpus

The 27 November 2020 launch is the corpus's first event anchored in India and its first event anchored anywhere in South Asia — closing a corpus event-anchor gap that the broader [Internet Freedom Foundation](../organizations/org-internet-freedom-foundation.md) and its strategic-litigation, surveillance-reform, and shutdown-litigation portfolios had previously sat in without a single Indian convening on the record. The launch is also the corpus's first event whose form is a civil-society-built public-tracker-and-petition launch — structurally distinct from the coalition-launch events the corpus already carries (the [Campaign to Stop Killer Robots London launch](event-skr-launch-london-2013-04.md) of 22–23 April 2013, the [Access Now #KeepItOn coalition public launch](event-access-now-keepiton-launch-2016-06-08.md) of 8 June 2016) and from the regional-thematic-hearing and unconference events anchoring the corpus's other event slices ([IACHR 192nd-session regional hearing](event-derechos-digitales-iachr-192-ai-hearing-2025-03-07.md) of 7 March 2025; [SMEX Bread & Net 6th edition](event-smex-bread-and-net-6th-edition-2024-05.md) of 13–15 May 2024). It is the corpus's first event in which a civic-technology partnership (DataKind Bengaluru as volunteer build team, Frappe Technologies as platform host) is the launch's operational backbone alongside the lead civil-society organisation, and the corpus's first event whose principal public artefact is itself a continuously-updated dataset rather than a one-off statement, report, or press conference.

Within the [campaign's own arc](../campaigns/camp-iff-project-panoptic.md) the 27 November 2020 launch is the founding event the rest of the campaign's running outputs sit on. The dataset that went public on launch day grew to [78 documented FRT projects across India with a combined budgeted spend of approximately 9.6 billion Indian rupees by November 2021](https://internetfreedom.in/tag/project-panoptic/), supplied the substantive evidence base for the Hyderabad #BanTheScan sub-campaign jointly launched with Amnesty International and Article 19 on 10 November 2021, anchored the January 2022 public-interest-litigation petition brought by S.Q. Masood in the Telangana High Court — India's first major judicial challenge to police facial recognition, with IFF legal counsel — and has [expanded past 120 named government FRT tenders by 2024](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Freedom_Foundation), with running state-by-state breakdowns and a continuously-active crowdsourced citizen-reporting stream. The launch day's public-petition demand for a moratorium on government FRT remains the campaign's substantive position six years on; the deployment estate the launch made legible has continued to grow, the moratorium has not been imposed, and the launch-day dashboard remains the field's principal reference dataset on Indian state facial-recognition surveillance.
