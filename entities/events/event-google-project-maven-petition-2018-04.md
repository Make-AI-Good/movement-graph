# Google employees petition against Project Maven drone-AI contract (April 2018)

In April 2018, more than 3,100 Google employees [signed an internal petition](https://gizmodo.com/thousands-of-google-employees-protest-companys-involvem-1824988565) addressed to CEO Sundar Pichai demanding the company cancel its Pentagon contract for drone-surveillance AI and commit to never building weapons technology. Reported first by Gizmodo on 4 April 2018, the action is the earliest large-scale collective mobilisation of tech workers specifically targeting their employer's AI-ethics decisions — and the first successful employee campaign to cancel a military AI contract.

## Project Maven

Project Maven — formally the Pentagon's Algorithmic Warfare Cross-Functional Team — was launched by the US Department of Defense in April 2017 to [accelerate military integration of machine learning](https://www.armscontrol.org/act/2018-07/news/google-renounces-ai-work-weapons). Google was contracted to develop computer-vision algorithms to analyse video footage captured by US military drones for object detection and tracking. The initial contract value was $15 million over 18 months, with internal Google projections [estimating potential growth to $250 million annually](https://theintercept.com/2018/06/01/google-drone-ai-project-maven-contract-renew/). Knowledge of the contract spread unevenly through Google from late 2017; by early 2018 at least a dozen engineering teams had learned of it through informal internal channels.

## The petition

The petition, addressed to Pichai and [first published by Gizmodo](https://gizmodo.com/thousands-of-google-employees-protest-companys-involvem-1824988565), carried approximately 3,100 signatories — including dozens of senior engineers — and made two demands: immediate cancellation of the Maven contract, and a public company commitment never to develop weapons technology. Its core argument was that "Google should not be in the business of war" and that the contract contradicted the company's historical "Don't Be Evil" standard. A smaller number of employees resigned outright in protest rather than sign.

Organising ran across multiple internal tracks: one group tracked colleagues who had resigned; another monitored media coverage; a third systematically submitted Maven questions at company all-hands meetings and recruited co-workers to upvote them, forcing leadership responses. The campaign was [distributed across Google's global offices](https://logicmag.io/the-making-of-the-tech-worker-movement/full-text/) — Mountain View, Seattle, New York, Montreal, Dublin, and Zurich — and operated without any formal union structure; the [Alphabet Workers Union–CWA](https://alphabetworkersunion.org/) did not exist yet and would not launch until January 2021.

## Outcome

On 1 June 2018, Google Cloud CEO Diane Greene [announced](https://theintercept.com/2018/06/01/google-drone-ai-project-maven-contract-renew/) that Google would not renew the Maven contract when it expired in March 2019. The following month Google published its Artificial Intelligence Principles, [explicitly prohibiting](https://www.armscontrol.org/act/2018-07/news/google-renounces-ai-work-weapons) AI applications for weapons or surveillance violating international norms. Microsoft and Amazon Web Services subsequently assumed the drone-AI contract work Google had declined.

## Significance

The Maven petition established the principal model for tech-worker AI-ethics activism. [Logic Magazine's retrospective](https://logicmag.io/the-making-of-the-tech-worker-movement/full-text/) on the tech-worker movement documents how it shifted workers' self-understanding — from professionals with individual leverage to a collective with power over their employer's core product decisions — and catalysed a wave of analogous campaigns within weeks: Microsoft employees mobilised against ICE facial-recognition contracts; Amazon employees raised objections to Rekognition sales to law enforcement; Salesforce workers organised similarly. The November 2018 global Google walkout, in which approximately 20,000 workers across 50 offices protested the company's handling of sexual harassment, drew directly on the Maven campaign's networks and tactics.

For the corpus's tech-worker-organising thread, the petition is the founding event: the moment at which collective employee action targeting AI development decisions first succeeded in changing a major platform company's course — and the origin of the #TechWontBuildIt pattern the movement would iterate on across subsequent years.
