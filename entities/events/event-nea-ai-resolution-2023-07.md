# NEA 102nd Representative Assembly — AI in Education Mandate (July 2023)

The [National Education Association](../organizations/org-national-education-association.md)'s 102nd Representative Assembly, convened in July 2023 at the Orange County Convention Center in Orlando, Florida, produced the first formal AI governance action by the largest labor union in the United States. While the assembly's dominant theme was ["Freedom to Learn"](https://www.nea.org/nea-today/all-news-articles/2023-nea-representative-assembly-freedom-learn-spotlight) — the NEA's legislative and organizing response to accelerating book bans and attacks on inclusive education — the topic of artificial intelligence [repeatedly surfaced in delegate discussions](https://www.edweek.org/teaching-learning/nea-approves-ai-guidance-but-its-vital-for-educators-to-tread-carefully/2024/07) across the multi-day gathering. NEA President Becky Pringle responded from the assembly floor by [announcing that the NEA would convene a Task Force on Artificial Intelligence in Education](https://www.edweek.org/teaching-learning/nea-president-sympathizes-with-teachers-who-want-to-quit-heres-her-advice/2023/07), framing the initiative as exploratory rather than adversarial: "The reality is it's here, and it's not going anywhere." Approximately 6,000 delegates representing 2.8 million educators across every US state and territory were present.

## Context

The 2023 RA took place at the moment when generative AI — launched into mainstream awareness by ChatGPT's November 2022 release — had entered daily classroom practice without any institutional reckoning from the sector. Teachers were encountering AI-generated student submissions, experimenting with AI lesson-planning and administrative tools, and receiving contradictory school and district directives ranging from total prohibition to uncritical adoption. At the federal level, [fewer than 20 US states](https://www.nea.org/about-nea/media-center/press-releases/nea-delegates-approve-measure-recommend-policy-actions-use-ai) had issued any AI guidance to schools at all. The NEA's member base — the educators most directly exposed to AI's deployment in classrooms — had no collective institutional position from which to engage that deployment. Delegates raised AI in the context of several of the assembly's standing thematic areas: equity, professional autonomy, student data privacy, and fair labor conditions.

## AI Mandate

Pringle's announcement established that the task force would bring together [external technology-sector representatives, international experts, and labor union partners](https://www.edweek.org/teaching-learning/nea-president-sympathizes-with-teachers-who-want-to-quit-heres-her-advice/2023/07) alongside educators — a deliberately broad envelope designed to give the NEA the external technical and policy expertise its members had not themselves accumulated, while keeping educator perspectives central. The framing — "What do we want to say about that? What do we know about it?" — positioned the task force as a democratic knowledge-building exercise ahead of any policy commitment, consistent with the NEA's governance model of membership-driven policy formation through the representative assembly.

Following the July 2023 assembly, President Pringle [formally appointed the Task Force on AI in Education in fall 2023](https://www.nea.org/resource-library/artificial-intelligence-education/ii-task-force-and-its-work), chaired by Secretary-Treasurer Noel Candelaria. The task force ran [virtual sessions from November 2023 through April 2024](https://www.nea.org/resource-library/artificial-intelligence-education/ii-task-force-and-its-work) and convened in person in Chicago on March 1, 2024, with two virtual subgroups addressing AI uses in education and professional support, and ethics, equity, bias, and intellectual property respectively. The group consulted subject-matter experts and engaged educators at national conferences across its seven-month run. The Task Force Report was published on June 26, 2024; on July 4, 2024, approximately 7,000 delegates to the 103rd NEA Representative Assembly in Philadelphia voted by voice to approve the NEA's first-ever policy statement on artificial intelligence in education.

## Significance

The 2023 NEA RA is the corpus's anchor point for US education-sector labor organizing on AI governance — the first time the largest US labor union's highest deliberative body formally engaged with AI as a governance question rather than a pedagogical curiosity. The significance is structural as much as substantive: the NEA's representative assembly is a democratic ratification surface that no NGO statement or think-tank report can replicate. The 2023 RA's AI action, while informal by comparison with the 2024 policy statement it produced, was the mechanism by which AI governance arrived on the union's institutional agenda — the moment after which the question was no longer whether the NEA would have a position but what that position would be. The 2024 policy statement, and the professional-development and federal-advocacy infrastructure the NEA built around it, are downstream of the 2023 mandate.

Within the broader movement corpus, the 2023 NEA RA stands alongside the [African Content Moderators Union founding vote (May 2023)](event-african-content-moderators-union-founding-vote-2023-05-01.md) as evidence that 2023 was the year AI labor governance moved from creative and gig-economy workers — [WGA](../organizations/org-wga.md), [SAG-AFTRA](../organizations/org-sag-aftra.md), [Concept Art Association](../organizations/org-concept-art-association.md) — into the broader worker-organizing ecosystem, including the educational workforce.
