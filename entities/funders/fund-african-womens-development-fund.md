# African Women's Development Fund

The African Women's Development Fund (AWDF) is a pan-African feminist fund [established in 2000 by Bisi Adeleye-Fayemi (Nigeria), Joana Foster (Ghana), and Dr. Hilda Tadria (Uganda)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Women's_Development_Fund) and [headquartered at P.M.B CT 89 Cantonments, Accra, Ghana](https://awdf.org/who-we-are/our-herstory/). The Fund describes itself in its own words as ["a feminist fund"](https://awdf.org/) working in service of ["an Africa, and a world, where gender justice, freedom and dignity are achieved for all"](https://awdf.org/), and as a vehicle to "resource, support and nurture African women's rights organisations and feminist movements to sustain themselves." Its origin story is precisely traceable: [Dr. Hilda Tadria and Joana Foster met at the 1994 Dakar preparatory conference for the Fourth World Conference on Women](https://www.journey.awdf.org/) and conceived the fund during a beach walk, framing the question as how to build ["a resource pool, by African women, for African women"](https://www.journey.awdf.org/). Bisi Adeleye-Fayemi, then at Akina Mama wa Afrika, developed the concept further; the [Global Fund for Women contributed a USD 5,000 grant to develop the founding proposal](https://www.journey.awdf.org/), and [AWDF was introduced at the June 2000 Beijing+5 review in New York, where impromptu pledges from African women in the room generated an additional USD 13,000](https://www.journey.awdf.org/). By the [December 2001 Africa launch in Accra](https://www.journey.awdf.org/), AWDF had already distributed USD 344,000 in first grants to 38 women's organisations across 28 African countries. Current Chief Executive Officer [Françoise Moudouthe](https://awdf.org/who-we-are/meet-the-team/), a pan-African feminist from Cameroon who previously founded the African feminist platform Eyala, leads the Fund alongside Director of Operations Gertrude Bibi Annoh-Quarshie and Director of Programmes Nana Zulu; the Executive Board is chaired by Jean-Ann Ndow (concurrently leading the Malala Fund's Girl Programme) and continues to include co-founders Dr. Hilda M. Tadria and Bisi Adeleye-Fayemi. Co-founder [Joana Foster died in November 2016](https://awdf.org/who-we-are/our-herstory/); the Joana Foster Memorial Grant was established in her honour with an initial USD 55,000 distribution. AWDF's status in this corpus is active.

## Grantmaking — scale, scope, and instruments

AWDF reports [cumulative grantmaking of approximately US$111 million since inception to 1,773 women's rights and feminist organisations across 47 African countries and 5 Middle East countries (Palestine, Iraq, Yemen, Jordan, Lebanon), with US$12 million awarded in 2024](https://awdf.org/). The historical trajectory is documented in the Fund's [own 22-year impact summary through 2022](https://www.journey.awdf.org/): 2,884 grants, USD 68.8 million distributed, 1,555 women's rights and feminist organisations funded, and a USD 4.7 million endowment built up from a starting capital that was effectively zero. Earlier inflection points include the [15th-anniversary figure of over US$25 million granted to at least 1,200 women's organisations in 42 African countries](https://awdf.org/and-then-the-women-came-a-tribute-to-the-african-womens-development-fund/) and the [March 2022 cumulative figure of over US$50 million](https://www.influencewatch.org/organization/african-womens-development-fund-awdf/), placing the Fund on a roughly-doubling trajectory across the last decade. Premises followed funds: AWDF [initially occupied the third floor of Aviation House in Accra, moved to its own building by 2004, and by 2008 had purchased two buildings to operate from a permanent rent-free headquarters](https://www.journey.awdf.org/).

The Fund's [current grantmaking is organised around five thematic areas — Ending Violence Against Women and Girls, Economic Empowerment, Health and Well-Being, Leadership and Governance, and Climate Justice](https://www2.fundsforngos.org/articles/advancing-gender-equality-the-work-of-african-womens-development-fund/) — and five programmatic pillars — [Resourcing, Agency and Resilience, Knowledge and Voice, Solidarity and Care, and Impact and Learning](https://awdf.org/). Typical grants run [US$1,000–US$50,000, with multi-year strategic grants up to US$120,000 and capacity-building grants for organisational growth and development](https://www2.fundsforngos.org/articles/advancing-gender-equality-the-work-of-african-womens-development-fund/). The Fund's eligibility criteria are stated plainly: grantee organisations must be ["women-led, with a majority women staff and board"](https://awdf.org/grant-types-and-criteria/), committed to advancing women's rights or gender equality, registered in an African country (excluding Sudan, South Sudan, and Somalia), and operating at local, national, sub-regional, or regional levels. Grants are capped at less than 50% of an applicant's annual income; applications above US$20,000 require an audit report; assessments run biannually.

The current grant-line architecture combines several named instruments. The [2025 Open / Kasa! / Komboa call](https://awdf.org/call-for-applications-open-kasa-komboa-grants/) bundled three categories: Open Grants for general thematic-area work, [Kasa! Initiative Grants for women's rights organisations registered in Ghana, Nigeria, and Senegal addressing sexual violence](https://awdf.org/call-for-applications-open-kasa-komboa-grants/) — a line capitalised in [December 2021 by a US$3.75 million collaboration with the Ford Foundation and Open Society Foundations](https://www.influencewatch.org/organization/african-womens-development-fund-awdf/) — and [Komboa Grants for women and gender-diverse-persons-led organisations and networks working on voice, agency, and leadership](https://awdf.org/call-for-applications-open-kasa-komboa-grants/). Beyond grantmaking, AWDF runs the [African Women's Leadership Institute, which has supported over 6,000 women leaders over twenty years](https://awdf.org/who-we-are/our-herstory/), and has used non-traditional fundraising vehicles including the [Sheroes album, featuring 17 African women artists including Angelique Kidjo and Yvonne Chaka Chaka, with royalties supporting the Fund's work](https://www.journey.awdf.org/).

## Toward participatory grantmaking — the GAP

In May 2026, AWDF opened applications for a [Participatory Grant Advisory Panel (GAP)](https://www.globalsouthopportunities.com/2026/05/06/development-30/) to inform its grantmaking on child sexual abuse prevention, response, and survivor support across Africa. The panel is composed of survivors of child sexual abuse (disclosure optional), gender-justice and child-protection advocates, community leaders and grassroots organisers, social-development practitioners, and survivor-support-system professionals, and is framed by AWDF as a shift toward ["participatory philanthropy, where those affected by social issues contribute directly to shaping solutions"](https://www.globalsouthopportunities.com/2026/05/06/development-30/) — decisions reflecting "both technical expertise and lived experience" rather than relying solely on institutional decision-makers. The GAP is AWDF's first publicly documented adoption of an external lived-experience advisory architecture inside a specific grant line; whether it generalises beyond the child-sexual-abuse vertical to other thematic areas is the open question the design choice raises.

## Resource base and funder-network position

AWDF is the corpus's first feminist fund whose own donor base is publicly traceable in detail across multiple in-corpus funders. The Fund's major donors include the [Ford Foundation](fund-ford-foundation.md), the [Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md), the Carnegie Corporation, the Global Fund for Women, the Hewlett Foundation, the Novo Foundation, the Women's Foundation of Minnesota, [MacKenzie Scott](https://www.influencewatch.org/organization/african-womens-development-fund-awdf/) (a March 2022 grant of undisclosed amount), and the governments of Canada and the Netherlands. The [December 2021 US$3.75 million Kasa! Fund collaboration](https://www.influencewatch.org/organization/african-womens-development-fund-awdf/) — anchored jointly with the Ford Foundation and Open Society Foundations to support work addressing sexual violence — is the most concrete documented case of two in-corpus funders pooling resources through AWDF as a re-granting partner. AWDF also operates with a US sister organisation, [African Women's Development Fund USA](https://www.influencewatch.org/organization/african-womens-development-fund-awdf/), as a US fundraising vehicle. Read together, the donor list and the Kasa! Fund pattern position AWDF as a piece of pan-African feminist re-granting infrastructure that several of the corpus's largest US foundations rely on to reach African women's organisations they could not reach directly.

## Position in the corpus funder slice

AWDF is the corpus's first **Ghana-headquartered funder of any kind** and the second **pan-African re-granting vehicle** after the [African Digital Rights Fund](fund-african-digital-rights-fund.md) (hosted in Kampala by CIPESA) — but the two occupy structurally different registers. ADRF is a small-grant, rapid-response, round-by-round pooled programme administered by an ICT-policy think tank, with cumulative disbursement around US$1.3 million across roughly seven years and grants of US$1,000–US$10,000 capped at six months. AWDF is an endowed, multi-year, large-grant feminist foundation with cumulative disbursement of approximately US$111 million across a quarter-century, typical grants of US$1,000–US$50,000, and multi-year strategic grants to US$120,000. Together the two give the corpus a contrasting pair of pan-African philanthropic models — one Pollicy-and-digital-rights-shaped and donor-pool-funded, one feminist-movement-shaped and endowed — rather than a single "Africa-based re-granting" register.

AWDF is also the corpus's second **feminist fund** after [Mama Cash](fund-mama-cash.md), but in a register Mama Cash does not occupy. Mama Cash is an Amsterdam-headquartered, internationally-scoped, activist-led-participatory-grantmaking feminist fund founded in 1983; AWDF is Accra-headquartered, pan-African-scoped, founder-led-and-Board-led, and was launched in 2000 with a deliberate ["by African women, for African women"](https://www.journey.awdf.org/) anchoring that positions African-women-led philanthropy as the alternative to North-to-South aid. Mama Cash's flagship Resilience Fund decisions are made by an eleven-person Community Committee of external feminist activists; AWDF's grantmaking decisions are made internally, with the new [GAP](https://www.globalsouthopportunities.com/2026/05/06/development-30/) representing a first move toward external lived-experience advisory inside a single vertical rather than across the whole portfolio. Together the two feminist funds in the corpus map onto two recognisable feminist-philanthropy traditions — the Northern-headquartered fund that resources Global-South feminist movements through participatory governance, and the Global-South-headquartered fund that resources its own continent through founder-and-Board-led African-feminist governance.

Within the broader funder slice — which runs across US-fortune-anchored ([Ford Foundation](fund-ford-foundation.md), [MacArthur Foundation](fund-macarthur-foundation.md), [Omidyar Network](fund-omidyar-network.md), [Democracy Fund](fund-democracy-fund.md), [Mozilla Foundation](fund-mozilla-foundation.md), [Kapor Foundation](fund-kapor-foundation.md), [Patrick J. McGovern Foundation](fund-patrick-j-mcgovern-foundation.md)), UK ([Nuffield Foundation](fund-nuffield-foundation.md), [Sigrid Rausing Trust](fund-sigrid-rausing-trust.md)) and international-headquartered-in-London ([Luminate](fund-luminate.md)) entries, Continental European ([Stiftung Mercator](fund-stiftung-mercator.md), [Adessium Foundation](fund-adessium-foundation.md)) and [pooled European](fund-european-ai-society-fund.md) vehicles, internationally-scoped ([Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md)) foundations, regional and family foundations on [other continents](fund-mcconnell-foundation.md), [Australia](fund-minderoo-foundation.md), and [Latin America](fund-fundacion-avina.md), the [South African individual-fellowship](fund-shuttleworth-foundation.md) model, the [pan-African digital-rights pooled vehicle](fund-african-digital-rights-fund.md), and the [Dutch feminist fund](fund-mama-cash.md) — AWDF is the corpus's principal window onto an African-headquartered, African-women-led, endowment-anchored, pan-African feminist funder operating at the US$10M-per-year-flow scale and twenty-five-year horizon. It is the funder slice's most direct example of African-feminist movement infrastructure built and led from inside the continent rather than resourced into it.
