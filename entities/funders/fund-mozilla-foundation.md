# Mozilla Foundation

The Mozilla Foundation is a US 501(c)(3) nonprofit headquartered in Mountain View, California, founded on [15 July 2003](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mozilla_Foundation) to ensure the long-term independence of the open-source Mozilla project after AOL scaled back its involvement with Netscape (the Foundation received an initial $2 million from AOL alongside transferred hardware and intellectual property). It is the parent organisation of the Mozilla Corporation — established 3 August 2005 to produce Firefox — and of MZLA Technologies Corporation, which develops Thunderbird. The Foundation describes itself on its [home page](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/) as "a global non-profit and parent of the Mozilla Corporation," and frames its mission around three pillars: "Make good tech the norm. Make good things together. Make good ideas matter." Mark Surman has been at the centre of the Foundation since August 2008, when he became Executive Director; in 2022 he took on the additional role of [Mozilla Foundation President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Surman), working on Mozilla-wide strategy alongside Mitchell Baker, with Nabiha Syed [appointed Executive Director on 15 May 2024](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mozilla_Foundation). The Foundation's [current board](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/who-we-are/) is chaired by Nicole Wong, with Helen Turvey, Alondra Nelson, Edwin Macharia, and Zain Habboo as members.

Mozilla's structural position is unusual within the funder slice of this corpus: it is not an endowed grantmaking foundation in the Ford or MacArthur mould, but a tech-product-anchored nonprofit whose grantmaking, fellowships, advocacy, research, and open-source infrastructure work sit alongside (and are partly cross-subsidised by) the commercial activity of the Mozilla Corporation. The home page reports a [cumulative $35 million in grants awarded](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/) and a movement of more than six million people; in November 2024 the Foundation announced [a roughly 30% reduction of its workforce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mozilla_Foundation), citing a "relentless onslaught of change," which has reshaped but not displaced its AI-good programming.

## Trustworthy AI as theory of change

Mozilla's organising frame for its AI-good work is set out in [*Creating Trustworthy AI*](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/insights/trustworthy-ai-whitepaper/), a white paper [released in May 2020](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/research/library/trustworthy-ai-white-paper/) after a year-long consultation with AI domain experts across industry, civil society, academia, and the public sphere. The paper focuses specifically on consumer AI — "general purpose internet products and services aimed at a wide audience" — and [names five guiding principles](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/blog/trustworthy-ai-abridged-version/) (agency, accountability, privacy, fairness, and safety) alongside three core challenges the Foundation argues the field must address: monopoly and centralisation, data privacy and governance, and bias and discrimination. The white paper has functioned as the through-line of Mozilla's subsequent grantmaking, fellowship, and advocacy decisions, with the [Trustworthy AI Funding Principles](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/what-we-fund/trustworthy-ai-funding-principles/) carrying its criteria directly into the Foundation's grant decisions.

## Grantmaking and the Public Interest AI coalition

Mozilla's most consequential entry into the corpus's funder field is its co-launch role in the ten-foundation Public Interest AI initiative. On [1 November 2023, the Foundation announced](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/blog/mozilla-joins-philanthropic-coalition-vice-president-harris-to-support-public-interest-ai/) that it had joined a coalition with the [Ford Foundation](fund-ford-foundation.md), the [MacArthur Foundation](fund-macarthur-foundation.md), the David and Lucile Packard Foundation, [Democracy Fund](fund-democracy-fund.md), Heising-Simons Foundation, [Kapor Foundation](fund-kapor-foundation.md), [Omidyar Network](fund-omidyar-network.md), [Open Society Foundations](../funders/fund-open-society-foundations.md), and the Wallace Global Fund to commit more than $200 million toward "a more trustworthy AI ecosystem." [Mozilla's announcement](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/blog/mozilla-joins-philanthropic-coalition-vice-president-harris-to-support-public-interest-ai/) carries a direct framing from J. Bob Alotta, Mozilla's Senior Vice President of Global Programs, that "there is currently a harmful status quo in AI — one defined by profit before people, centralized power, and little transparency or accountability." The initiative organised its grantmaking around five focus areas identified by Vice President Kamala Harris — democracy and rights, public-interest innovation, workers' rights and labour standards, transparency and accountability of AI models and companies, and the development of international AI rules and norms — and the [Ford Foundation's parallel release](https://www.fordfoundation.org/news-and-stories/news-and-press/news/philanthropies-launch-new-initiative-to-ensure-ai-advances-the-public-interest/) is the same announcement viewed from the coalition's other co-leading partner.

Beyond the coalition, the Foundation's standing grantmaking vehicle is the [Mozilla Foundation Incubator](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/what-we-do/grantmaking/incubator/), which runs cohort programmes for responsible-AI builders — including the Democracy x AI cohort and the Data Futures Lab — and hands out fellowships and small grants to civil-society projects working at the intersection of community organising and AI governance. The [Responsible Computing Challenge](https://www.mozillafoundation.org/en/responsible-computing-challenge/grant-opportunities/) is the Foundation's higher-education curriculum-grant programme, supporting faculty embedding social, political, and ethical context into computer-science teaching; the programme expanded into South Africa in 2024 as part of Mozilla's wider push to seed responsible-computing capacity in the African higher-education system. The Foundation also names the [Algorithmic Justice League](../organizations/org-algorithmic-justice-league.md) among its long-standing grantees: AJL's [own publicly listed funders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithmic_Justice_League) place Mozilla alongside the Ford, MacArthur, Rockefeller, and Alfred P. Sloan foundations as core institutional supporters of the AJL's research and public-awareness work on algorithmic bias.

## Common Voice and the open-data infrastructure flavour

A distinctive feature of Mozilla's funder shape is that a substantial part of its AI-good footprint takes the form of operated open-source infrastructure rather than third-party grantmaking. [Common Voice](https://commonvoice.mozilla.org/), [launched in 2017](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Voice), is the most legible example: a crowdsourced multilingual speech corpus released into the public domain under CC0, with the explicit framing that opening up training data is one concrete way to "level the playing field while mitigating AI bias." Common Voice has since grown into the world's largest multilingual public-domain voice dataset, covering 60-plus languages including widely spoken and regional or minoritised languages such as Welsh and Kabyle. The same logic — that field-shaping in AI is partly accomplished by producing public-interest infrastructure, not only by funding others to do so — runs through the Foundation's Data Futures Lab and its broader research and advocacy output, and is one of the things that visibly distinguishes Mozilla from the otherwise comparable US foundations in its co-launch coalition.

## Relationship to the broader AI-good movement

Mozilla's footprint in the corpus comes through two distinct paths. As a funder it backs civil-society organisations working on algorithmic accountability and bias — AJL is the most legible current case — and underwrites field-building coalitions through the Public Interest AI initiative and the Foundation's own Incubator and Responsible Computing programmes. As a research and advocacy actor it sits inside specific corpus campaigns: the [Legal Resources Centre](../organizations/org-legal-resources-centre.md)'s Democratising Big Tech programme, for instance, ran its 2024 South-African-election experimental ad investigation as a [consortium with Mozilla, Global Witness, and CIPIT](https://cipesa.org/2025/07/democratising-big-tech-lessons-from-south-africas-2024-election/), with Mozilla acting as a research partner rather than a grantmaker. Within the funder slice of this corpus the Foundation diversifies the shape decisively: it is the only global tech-product-anchored nonprofit alongside the [Ford](fund-ford-foundation.md), [MacArthur](fund-macarthur-foundation.md), [Nuffield](fund-nuffield-foundation.md), and [Sigrid Rausing Trust](fund-sigrid-rausing-trust.md) funders, and the only entry whose AI-good work runs on parallel rails of grantmaking, fellowship cohorts, operated open-data infrastructure, and direct co-investigation with grassroots organisations.
