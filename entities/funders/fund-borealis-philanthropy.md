# Borealis Philanthropy

Borealis Philanthropy is a Minneapolis-headquartered US racial-equity philanthropic intermediary [that obtained tax-exempt status in July 2014](https://projects.propublica.org/nonprofits/organizations/464598642), founded by Margarita "Magui" Rubalcava, who previously directed immigration grantmaking at [NEO Philanthropy](https://www.influencewatch.org/non-profit/borealis-philanthropy/)'s Four Freedoms Fund. Rubalcava built Borealis on a structural model analogous to NEO Philanthropy's own pooled-fund architecture — assembling a portfolio of thematically distinct donor collaboratives rather than running a single endowment-backed programme — and grew the organisation from a $127,541 first-year filing into a $32-million-annual institution within roughly a decade. Borealis describes itself as a "philanthropic intermediary" matching donors with grassroots movements, channelling capital into Black-led movement building, disability justice, LGBTQ community power, community-safety reform, and racial equity in journalism. In [2024 Borealis disbursed approximately $32.7 million to 486 organisations](https://borealisphilanthropy.org/about-us/2024-annual-report/) across nine funds; cumulative grantmaking since 2020 exceeds $165 million.

## Nine-fund architecture

Borealis operates as a family of nine donor collaboratives, each with its own steering committee and grantmaking strategy:

- **Black-Led Movement Fund** (2016–2025) — invested over $39 million across its nine-year run supporting the Movement for Black Lives and values-aligned organisations shaping policy and building local Black community power.
- **Communities Transforming Policing Fund** — resources communities to increase police accountability, transparency, and alternatives to incarceration; $5.16 million to 61 grantees in 2024.
- **Disability Inclusion Fund** — seeds and expands the capacity of the disability-justice movement through grassroots leadership, policy advocacy, and narrative change; $5.25 million to 75 grantees in 2024.
- **Emerging LGBTQ Leaders of Color Fund** — supports young BIPOC queer and trans leaders organising across intersecting issues; $2.73 million to 49 grantees in 2024.
- **Fund for Trans Generations** — the broadest single-fund grantee base in the portfolio at 109 recipients in 2024, totalling $2.94 million.
- **Racial Equity to Accelerate Change (REACH) Fund** — invests in "movement weavers" and racial-equity practitioners across issue areas; $1.915 million to 20 grantees in 2024. See the tech-accountability sub-section below.
- **Racial Equity in Journalism Fund** — supports news organisations led by and for People of Colour; $3.99 million to 41 grantees in 2024, backed by a [$1 million 2021 MacArthur grant](https://www.macfound.org/grantee/borealis-philanthropy-10114834/).
- **Racial Equity in Philanthropy Fund** — invested in philanthropy-serving organisations to equip funders with racial-equity practices since 2018; $6.1 million to 25 grantees in 2024; [sunset in 2025](https://borealisphilanthropy.org/our-funds/).
- **Spark Justice Fund** — resources grassroots groups working toward decarceration and community-led safety; $1.68 million to 55 grantees in 2024.

The architecture is structurally different from the endowed private-foundation model: Borealis holds pooled donor capital rather than its own permanent endowment, and grantmaking decisions within each fund are made by committees composed of leaders representative of and invested in the fund's issue area. As of FY2024 the organisation carries $78.2 million in assets against $42.5 million in revenue, with the excess-of-expenses-over-revenue funded from prior-period accumulated capital.

## Tech-accountability programs

Two Borealis sub-programs place it at the grassroots tech-accountability intersection.

The **Disability x Tech Fund (DIF x Tech)**, [launched February 2023](https://www.fordfoundation.org/news-and-stories/news-and-press/news/borealis-philanthropy-and-ford-foundation-launch-1-million-disability-x-tech-fund-to-advance-leadership-of-people-with-disabilities-in-tech-innovation/) in partnership with the [Ford Foundation](fund-ford-foundation.md), is self-described as "the only national fund supporting disability-led groups working to bring about transformational change at the intersections of disability rights, justice and technology." Its grantmaking centres on algorithmic bias in benefits access, biometric surveillance, and digital-economy participation barriers — the technology harms that most acutely affect people with disabilities. The [MacArthur Foundation's $400,000 2024 grant to DIFxTech](https://www.macfound.org/grantee/borealis-philanthropy-10114834/) is framed around elevating "disability justice as a public interest technology priority" and democratising "technology development, provide equitable access to digital infrastructure." The inaugural cohort of seven grantees and fellows includes the Surveillance Technology Oversight Project (S.T.O.P.), Autistic Women & Nonbinary Network, CommunicationFIRST, CymaSpace, Deaf Spotlight (fellow Topher Ávila), Suma, and Community Legal Services fellow Rieko Shepherd — organisations spanning litigation, accessible tooling, community-based platforms, and policy advocacy at the disability-tech intersection.

The **REACH Fund** is the second tech-relevant program. Its [2025 request for proposals](https://borealisphilanthropy.org/news-and-views/the-racial-equity-to-accelerate-change-fund-launches-request-for-proposals-through-july-24-2025/) explicitly identifies "liberatory tech and data strategists" among the practitioner roles the fund resources, describing this category as practitioners building "tools that return control of data to communities, and training organizers on counter-surveillance to ensure that policies and practices make movements and communities safer." Two-year grants range from $30,000 to $150,000. The REACH Fund also inaugurated a participatory grantmaking committee in 2025 composed of "leaders deeply embedded in movements" — including Black, disabled, migrant, and LGBTQ communities — as the deciding body for awards, a structural commitment to community-controlled grantmaking.

## Upstream donors

[Per InfluenceWatch's compilation of Borealis's Form 990 filings](https://www.influencewatch.org/non-profit/borealis-philanthropy/), the principal named institutional funders include the [Ford Foundation](fund-ford-foundation.md), the [Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md), the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, the JPB Foundation, the NoVo Foundation, and MacKenzie Scott's Yield Giving. [MacArthur's nine grants totalling $7.49 million (2018–2025)](https://www.macfound.org/grantee/borealis-philanthropy-10114834/) are documented in the MacArthur grantee record. The [Rockefeller Brothers Fund](fund-rockefeller-brothers-fund.md), the Nathan Cummings Foundation, and the Compton Foundation appear as additional named institutional donors. The upstream picture shows Borealis drawing from a roster that substantially overlaps with the corpus's existing US private-foundation cluster, but channelling that capital through community-controlled steering committees rather than programme-officer-directed grantmaking — a structural design choice that positions Borealis as a re-granting infrastructure layer between mainstream US philanthropy and the grassroots organisations those foundations want to reach.

## Position in the corpus funder slice

The synth rationale for this entry — "closes racial-justice + AI-accountability infrastructure funder slot not covered by Kapor or Tides" — identifies a gap the corpus's existing US funder entries did not fill. The [Kapor Foundation](fund-kapor-foundation.md) addresses racial equity in tech primarily through STEM workforce pipeline and social-impact startup investment rather than movement-infrastructure grantmaking; the [Tides Foundation](fund-tides-foundation.md) runs a broader US progressive intermediary with AI-adjacent grantmaking primarily embedded in its donor-advised-fund layer (invisible in public 990 records) and New Media Ventures rather than in named tech-accountability programs. Borealis's DIF x Tech and REACH programs make the tech-accountability throughline explicit and named, and the intermediary model (community-controlled steering committees, pooled collaborative funds, two-year grantee relationships) is distinct from both.

Within the corpus's broader intermediary funder type — pooled re-granting vehicles occupying the layer between mainstream institutional philanthropy and grassroots grantees — the closest structural analogues are [Civitates](fund-civitates.md) (European democratic-renewal pooled fund), the [African Digital Rights Fund](fund-african-digital-rights-fund.md), and [Hivos](fund-hivos.md). But the comparison clarifies the distinction: those vehicles are narrower in thematic scope, primarily geography-constrained, and focused on a specific tech-rights remit; Borealis operates a multi-fund portfolio across a wider racial-equity terrain, with tech accountability as one concretely named program rather than the fund's sole purpose. The closest US analogue in the corpus is Tides, but the structural and thematic contrasts noted above put Borealis in a genuinely distinct slot: the US racial-equity–movement-infrastructure funder with an explicit tech-surveillance-accountability wing.
