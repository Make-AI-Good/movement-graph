# Renewable Freedom Foundation

The Renewable Freedom Foundation is a German private foundation (Stiftung Erneuerbare Freiheit) [established on 26 July 2012 by Georg Schäff](https://stiftungen.bayern.de/stiftung/11718), legally registered in Munich, Bavaria, as an _allgemeine Stiftung des bürgerlichen Rechts_ — a general German civil-law private foundation. The Foundation operates with an English-language surface at renewablefreedom.org and a German-language counterpart at erneuerbare-freiheit.de; its primary operational infrastructure is a [Technology and Human Rights Hub in Berlin](https://renewablefreedom.org/projects/technology-and-human-rights-hub/). The Foundation's stated aim is to protect and preserve civil liberties, particularly in the digital landscape, working through civic education, networking, civil-society support, and grantmaking.

## Programs and grantmaking

The Foundation's documented work runs across three programmatic tracks.

**Direct grantmaking (2014–2016 documented portfolio).** The [Foundation's past grants page](https://renewablefreedom.org/grants/history/) records a portfolio concentrated in privacy technology and European digital-rights advocacy. Large grants went to [GNUnet](https://gnunet.org/) for distributed privacy networking software, the Activist Hub Berlin for office support for tech activists, and the [Tor Anonymization Project](https://www.torproject.org/) for Tor operations and exit nodes. [La Quadrature du Net](../organizations/org-la-quadrature-du-net.md) received a small grant for internet rights defence. [Privacy International](../organizations/org-privacy-international.md), jointly with the Open Rights Group, received a large grant for a joint advocacy project. Other grantees from this period included the Library of Freedom, the Open Knowledge Foundation, iRights.info, and Stiftung Neue Verantwortung for a privacy research project. Amounts are classified on the published page as Small (under €10k), Medium (under €50k), and Large (over €50k).

**European Digital Rights Fund.** In 2017 the Foundation [designed and launched the European Digital Rights Fund](https://renewablefreedom.org/projects/european-digital-rights-small-grants-program/) in partnership with [European Digital Rights (EDRi)](../organizations/org-edri.md), [inviting over 100 digital-rights experts from the EDRi network](https://renewablefreedom.org/projects/european-digital-rights-small-grants-program/) to participate in a peer-driven grantmaking platform. The programme targets small and volunteer-run NGOs that struggle to access traditional funding processes, offering grants up to €5,000 for workshops, cross-sector meetings, project planning, and small project execution, and micro-grants below €2,000 for immediate operational needs such as travel, campaign materials, and essential equipment. A [key selection criterion is fostering collaboration across a wide set of civil-society actors](https://renewablefreedom.org/projects/european-digital-rights-small-grants-program/) — digital-rights groups, activists, researchers, consumer-protection advocates, and litigants. The Fund operates on a rolling basis through the EDRi membership network.

**Digital Whistleblowing Fund.** A [programme supporting organisations standing up digital whistleblowing platforms](https://renewablefreedom.org/projects/digital-whistleblowing-fund/), providing financial, operational, and strategic support across strategy, law, IT, and security to investigative journalists, human-rights and environmental activists, and anti-corruption organisations. Thematic calls, with an initial focus on anti-corruption activism, are evaluated by a selection committee drawn from whistleblowing, journalism, activism, anti-corruption, and hacking fields.

**Technology and Human Rights Hub.** A [Berlin-based coworking and coordination space](https://renewablefreedom.org/projects/technology-and-human-rights-hub/) for developers building privacy-enhancing and anti-censorship tools, investigative journalists, and political activists, positioned by the Foundation as the early infrastructure for what it describes as a full-fledged digital freedom institute. The Foundation cites Berlin's historical experience as a surveilled city as the rationale for the choice of location.

## Seed funding of the Digital Freedom Fund

The most structurally consequential documented act in the Foundation's history is its role as one of four seed funders of the [Digital Freedom Fund](fund-digital-freedom-fund.md) at its [launch on 25 January 2018](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/digital-freedom-fund-launches-to-support-digital-rights-litigation-in-europe/). The DFF — Europe's principal pooled strategic-litigation vehicle for digital rights — was capitalised at founding by [Adessium Foundation](fund-adessium-foundation.md), [Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md), [Omidyar Network](fund-omidyar-network.md), and the Renewable Freedom Foundation. The DFF's [funding-transparency page](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/funding/) lists RFF among its previous funders, confirming that the relationship extended from the seed phase through the DFF's early operational period. Since 2018 the DFF has made nearly 150 grants totalling over €5 million across 30 countries, supporting some 90 organisations and producing over 200 positive legal, policy, and social outcomes in European digital-rights litigation — ranging from the SyRI welfare-risk-scoring ruling to the [Foxglove–JCWI visa-streaming algorithm challenge](../campaigns/camp-foxglove-jcwi-visa-streaming-2020.md).

## Position in the corpus funder slice

The Renewable Freedom Foundation fills a specific gap in the corpus's German funder coverage. [Stiftung Mercator](fund-stiftung-mercator.md) and [Robert Bosch Stiftung](fund-robert-bosch-stiftung.md) represent large German foundations operating across broad multi-sector mandates; RFF is a smaller, more narrowly-focused German foundation whose entire mandate sits at the intersection of digital rights, civil liberties, privacy technology, and human-rights-respecting software — closer in programmatic scope to the [Digital Freedom Fund](fund-digital-freedom-fund.md) or the [European AI & Society Fund](fund-european-ai-society-fund.md) than to a general-purpose family foundation. It is also the corpus's first German foundation whose direct grantmaking was concentrated in privacy technology (Tor, GNUnet) alongside policy-advocacy and litigation-infrastructure roles.

The Foundation is notable in the corpus as an early-stage infrastructure builder: its peer-led small-grants vehicle for the EDRi community predated the current wave of pooled civil-society grantmaking vehicles in European digital rights, and its seed role in the Digital Freedom Fund placed it at the capitalisation moment of the European digital-rights litigation field's central re-granting structure. Where the existing funder slice gives the corpus windows onto large-endowment German foundations and European pooled vehicles, the Renewable Freedom Foundation is the corpus's window onto a smaller German civil-society foundation that seeded field infrastructure, ran a community-peer-led small-grants vehicle, and directly resourced privacy-technology development as a complement to legal and policy advocacy.
