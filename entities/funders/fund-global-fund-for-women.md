# Global Fund for Women

The Global Fund for Women is a US feminist grantmaking foundation [founded in 1987 in Palo Alto, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Fund_for_Women) by Anne Firth Murray, Frances Kissling, Laura Lederer, and Nita Barrow, now headquartered in San Francisco. The Fund defines its mission as funding ["bold, ambitious, and expansive gender justice movements to create meaningful change that will last beyond our lifetimes"](https://www.globalfundforwomen.org/who-we-are/vision-mission/) — a movements-first framing that positions it as a resource vehicle for feminist activists globally rather than a programme-deliverer in a specific issue lane. It [awarded its first grants in 1988 to eight grantees totalling $31,000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Fund_for_Women) and has since grown to distribute [over $100 million to more than 4,000 organisations in more than 170 countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Fund_for_Women), with approximately [$15.5 million in annual grants](https://www.learningtogive.org/resources/global-fund-women). The current President and CEO is PeiYao Chen, [who has a ten-year tenure with the organisation](https://www.globalfundforwomen.org/latest/article/peiyao-chen-new-president-ceo-of-global-fund-for-women/) and previously served as Senior Vice President of Global Operations; [Board Co-Chairs are Caroline Barlerin and Dayna Ash](https://www.globalfundforwomen.org/who-we-are/team/). Prior leaders include [founding president Anne Firth Murray (1986–1996), Kavita Ramdas (1996–2010, who grew assets from $6 million to $21 million), and Latanya Mapp Frett (2019–2023)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Fund_for_Women). The Fund [provided the USD 5,000 proposal-development grant that allowed the African Women's Development Fund founders to develop their founding proposal in 1994](https://www.journey.awdf.org/), establishing a funder-network role as incubator of Global South women's funds that the corpus now carries across three feminist fund entries.

## Founding and grantmaking model

The Fund was conceived by Anne Firth Murray [while she worked at the Hewlett Foundation](https://www.globalfundforwomen.org/who-we-are/vision-mission/), with the other three founders joining the effort. The grant selection model runs through an advisory council: the Fund [receives approximately 2,700 proposals annually and selects roughly 400 for funding](https://www.learningtogive.org/resources/global-fund-women), with [typical grants of $5,000 to $50,000 and first-time grants typically in the $5,000–$13,000 range](https://www.learningtogive.org/resources/global-fund-women). Unlike foundations that operate primarily from an endowment, Global Fund for Women funds its annual grantmaking through individual, foundation, and corporate contributions — making it a donation-dependent vehicle at a global scale. The corpus has no visibility into a comprehensive current grantee list; the Learning to Give figure of ~400 funded proposals per year at typical grant sizes implies a portfolio spanning well over a hundred organisations active at any given time, across all regions of the globe.

## Technology Initiative and digital rights work

The Fund's technology work is consolidated under the [Technology Initiative](https://www.globalfundforwomen.org/initiatives/technologyinitiative/), one of its nine named strategic initiatives. The initiative addresses four inter-related aims: helping women and girls end the gender technology gap; empowering them to create innovative technology solutions; improving their access to and control of technology; and developing technology-based responses to gender equality challenges including violence, health, and political participation. The technology-facilitated gender-based violence (TFGBV) dimension — which increasingly encompasses AI-generated harmful content, algorithmic amplification of abuse, and platform-enabled harassment — is a named component of the mandate, placing the Fund adjacent to the emerging field of feminist AI-harm advocacy.

The [IGNITE: Women Fueling Science and Technology](https://ignite.globalfundforwomen.org/technologyfund) programme pairs a public-facing campaign — stories of women and girls in STEM, organised around five categories (Visionaries, Creatives, Leaders, Geeks, Changemakers) — with a dedicated Technology Fund donation mechanism. The IGNITE Technology Fund frames technology access as a human rights issue and documents the structural gap: [200 million more men than women globally have internet access](https://ignite.globalfundforwomen.org/technologyfund), and women are [21% less likely to own a mobile phone than men](https://ignite.globalfundforwomen.org/technologyfund). Named grantees under the Technology Initiative include Feminist Approach to Technology (India, training young women aged 12–18 in technology confidence and computer skills) and Inwelle Study and Resource Centre (Nigeria, ICT skills for teenage girls for economic empowerment).

## Position in the corpus funder slice

Global Fund for Women occupies a structural position in this corpus no other entry fills. It is the corpus's oldest feminist grantmaking foundation — founded in 1987, three years before the Convention on the Rights of the Child was adopted — and operates at a cumulative grantmaking scale ($100M+ to 4,000+ organisations in 170+ countries) substantially larger than the other feminist funders in the corpus. [Mama Cash](fund-mama-cash.md) (founded 1983, Amsterdam, participatory-grantmaking structure through an external Community Committee), [Numun Fund](fund-numun-fund.md) (launched grantmaking 2022, Global South–based, feminist-tech-specific, $1.6M first cohort), and [African Women's Development Fund](fund-african-womens-development-fund.md) (founded 2000, Accra, pan-African scope, $111M cumulative over 25 years) each operate in narrower geographic or thematic scope, or at a more recent scale. Global Fund for Women's 170-country footprint is the corpus's widest among feminist funders.

The Fund's funder-network incubator role is structurally distinct from all other funder entries. [The USD 5,000 seed grant to AWDF for proposal development in 1994](https://www.journey.awdf.org/) — before AWDF formally launched — places GFW upstream in a chain now traceable through three in-corpus feminist fund entries: GFW seeded AWDF's founding, and AWDF subsequently became the corpus's pan-African feminist re-granting vehicle. No other in-corpus funder has a documented incubator relationship with another in-corpus fund.
