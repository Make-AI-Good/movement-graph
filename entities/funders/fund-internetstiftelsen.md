# Internetstiftelsen

Internetstiftelsen — [The Swedish Internet Foundation](https://internetstiftelsen.se/en/about-us/we-are-the-swedish-internet-foundation/) — is an independent private Swedish public-benefit foundation [founded on 26 August 1997](https://internetstiftelsen.se/en/about-us/we-are-the-swedish-internet-foundation/records-and-regulations/) and headquartered in Stockholm, established with an initial capital of SEK 200,000 and charged with two structural responsibilities: managing and developing Sweden's .se top-level domain, and promoting the positive development of the internet in Sweden more broadly. Formerly known as IIS (Internet Infrastructure Foundation / Internet Foundation in Sweden), the organisation rebranded as Internetstiftelsen in 2017. The foundation's charter mandates reinvesting at least 25 percent of annual turnover in research, innovation, and education focused on internet development; Wikipedia's 2020 snapshot puts this investment at SEK 88.1 million for that year. The foundation is a self-governing entity with no owner or members — its seven-member [board is elected by specified organisations](https://internetstiftelsen.se/en/about-us/we-are-the-swedish-internet-foundation/records-and-regulations/) (drawn from the technical community, academia, and associated organisations), with one additional member elected by the board itself, and surplus revenue from domain registrations (both .se and the Niuean .nu domain it operates) is the sole funding source for its public-benefit activities. CEO Carl Piva and communication chief Jannike Tillå have led the organisation through its most recent expansion into AI-public-education programming (2023–).

## Charter and operating model

The [charter's mission](https://internetstiftelsen.se/en/about-us/we-are-the-swedish-internet-foundation/) is framed around three commitments: "security issues, personal privacy and an open internet" — a formulation that anticipates the AI-governance terrain the foundation has moved into without having been written for it. The foundation is "business-driven and public-benefit": domain-registration revenue (from Sweden's .se registrar market and from licensing to registrars of .nu) funds a portfolio of public-benefit programs with no grant-seeking from other foundations and no government subsidy. The 25% reinvestment floor in the charter is a binding rule rather than an aspirational target — it is the mechanism by which the foundation's commercial activity (domain sales) is systematically converted into public infrastructure. In addition to the InternetFonden grant program and the AI programmes described below, the foundation's public-benefit portfolio includes the Bredbandskollen broadband speed-measurement service (a public-access diagnostic tool for Swedish internet infrastructure quality), the Internetkunskap platform (internet safety and awareness resources for the general public), the Internetmuseum digital museum (the first entirely digital museum inducted by the Association of Swedish Museums, launched 2014), the Digitala lektioner school curriculum resources, and the Svenska federationer identity federation infrastructure (account and authentication management for the Swedish healthcare and education sectors). The public portfolio is financed from the same domain-revenue base as the InternetFonden grants; there is no separate endowment.

## InternetFonden — the grant programme

InternetFonden is Internetstiftelsen's open grant programme for internet-promoting projects, running since 2004. The fund [distributes grants twice yearly to open applicants](https://news.cision.com/se/internetstiftelsen/r/14-nya-internetframjande-projekt-delar-pa-2-9-miljoner-kronor,c2001086) — companies, organisations, or individuals holding a Swedish organisation number or personal identification number — for projects that contribute to "positive internet development." The grant ceiling is SEK 399,000 per project (including VAT), with projects lasting up to one year. The programme is non-commercial and non-exclusive in scope: it has funded digital accessibility tools, coding education for teenagers, digital language platforms for newly arrived immigrants, web standards development, IoT research, internet-history documentary projects, and privacy and security educational initiatives. A single illustrative 2016 round disbursed SEK 2.9 million across 14 projects spanning this breadth. Cumulative totals reported from the programme's inception: 323 projects funded for a total of 65 million SEK (approximately €5.8 million at mid-2020s exchange rates) through the point in time when sources were last compiled — a figure that continues to grow with each biannual round. Unlike the large EU pooled vehicles in the corpus (Civitates, European AI & Society Fund), InternetFonden does not operate by invitation or cohort: applications are open, and there is no preset thematic focus that would exclude grassroots digital-rights or AI-accountability work from eligible territory.

## AI public-education and research programmes

Internetstiftelsen has expanded its public-benefit portfolio in two directly AI-relevant directions since 2023. The **AI knowledge initiative**, [launched on 15 June 2023](https://internetstiftelsen.se/om-oss/press/pressmeddelanden/internetstiftelsen-vill-lara-fler-om-ai-slapper-ny-kunskapssatsning/), positions AI literacy as a precondition for democratic participation — CEO Carl Piva's stated framing at launch was that "understanding AI technology is essential to democratic participation, not merely conversational knowledge." The initiative provides the Swedish public with accessible explanations of what AI is and how it operates, examples of AI deployment in Swedish public-sector contexts (including the Tax Agency's customer service chatbot and social-services robot handlers), and coverage of the risks and failure modes of AI systems. It was developed with plans for further initiatives in collaboration with other organisations. The companion **Swedes and AI (Svenskarna och AI)** annual survey series, which [published its first full report in 2024](https://svenskarnaochinternet.se/utvalt/svenskarna-och-ai-2024/), tracks AI adoption and public attitudes across the Swedish population with nationally representative sampling (1,090 interviews, August 2024). The 2024 findings report one-third of Swedish adults (ages 18–84) had used AI tools, with a stark 60% versus 10% adoption disparity between the 18–34 and 65–84 cohorts; nearly 4 in 10 Swedes expressed uncertainty or reluctance about using AI in the future; and the dominant pattern was deepening use among existing adopters rather than broadening to new demographics. The survey series is positioned alongside the long-running "Swedes and the Internet" (Svenskarna och internet) annual research programme — the foundation's established method for producing publicly accessible, democratically relevant empirical research on Swedish digital life.

## Algorithmic-accountability publishing

Beyond formal programmes, Internetstiftelsen has published substantive editorial content on algorithmic accountability and AI governance that places it squarely in the AI-good discourse. A [September 2024 feature on algorithmic decision-making risks](https://internetstiftelsen.se/nyheter/stora-risker-nar-algoritmerna-tar-besluten/) led with two high-profile cases of AI-driven public-sector failure: the Gothenburg school-placement algorithm that calculated distances "as the crow flies" rather than actual travel routes (producing hour-long commutes and split classes, with officials initially denying errors and refusing to disclose the algorithm until sued); and the Dutch childcare-subsidy AI scandal, in which a system flagged approximately 35,000 families for repayment — 94% erroneously — with applicants with foreign backgrounds systematically marked as higher risk. The article collected expert assessments including Charlotta Kronblad (legal researcher who led the Gothenburg litigation) on the accountability gap when institutions deny algorithmic error, and Jacob Dexe of RISE Research Institutes on the need to treat AI failures as learning opportunities rather than one-off incidents. A companion [article on trusting artificial intelligence](https://internetstiftelsen.se/nyheter/hur-ska-vi-kunna-lita-pa-artificiell-intelligens/) frames AI governance as a fundamentally societal question, outlines the EU's seven principles for trustworthy AI, and cites legal sociologist Stefan Larsson on the non-binding character of the EU guidelines and the difficulty of reconciling competing ethical and legal requirements in practice. This editorial posture — empirical, accountability-focused, accessible to a general Swedish readership — is consistent with the foundation's broader public-education mandate, applied to the AI domain. The annual Internet Days (Internetdagarna) conference, [running since 1999](https://internetdagarna.se/en/) as Sweden's most significant annual internet conference, is a further vector for this work: the conference programme routinely covers AI, platform accountability, and digital rights themes and brings together the technical community with civil-society groups including [DFRI](https://www.dfri.se/) (Föreningen för digitala fri- och rättigheter — Sweden's primary digital rights civil-society organisation), contributing to the same public-discourse formation the published research and editorial work supports.

## Position in the corpus funder slice

Within the corpus's funder slice, Internetstiftelsen fills the first Sweden-anchored and first Scandinavian-domain-infrastructure slot — a distinct shape from every other funder entry in the corpus. Where the European funder cluster divides principally between Brussels-pooled re-granting vehicles ([Civitates](fund-civitates.md), [European AI & Society Fund](fund-european-ai-society-fund.md)), multi-programme continental foundations ([Robert Bosch Stiftung](fund-robert-bosch-stiftung.md), [Stiftung Mercator](fund-stiftung-mercator.md), [Bertelsmann Stiftung](fund-bertelsmann-stiftung.md), [Fondation de France](fund-fondation-de-france.md)), and Nordic freedom-of-expression foundations ([Fritt Ord Foundation](fund-fritt-ord.md)), Internetstiftelsen is the first entry whose structural origin is domain-registry revenue rather than philanthropic endowment or pooled philanthropy. The public-benefit commitment is architecturally embedded in the business model — the 25% charter floor converts domain-registration commercial activity into internet-public-good investment by design — rather than being a separate philanthropic decision layered on top. This makes it closer in structure to the Internet Society Foundation (separate entity, separate corpus entry) than to the endowed foundations in the Nordic cluster.

Its AI-and-digital-rights relevance is primarily through the **education-and-public-knowledge vector** — AI literacy programming, the Swedes and AI research series, algorithmic-accountability editorial coverage — rather than through direct grantmaking to AI-accountability civil-society organisations, which is how the Civitates Tech & Democracy sub-fund or the European AI & Society Fund operate. The InternetFonden programme could fund such work under its broad "positive internet development" mandate, and its open, non-themed application structure does not exclude digital-rights or AI work, but the corpus does not yet document a specific AI-accountability grantee relationship. The foundation's civil-society connectivity is most visibly demonstrated through the Internetdagarna conference (DFRI co-presence) and the open InternetFonden grant programme's track record of funding grassroots internet-civil-society projects. In the corpus's Nordic coverage: [Fritt Ord](fund-fritt-ord.md) (Norway) now closes the Norway slot; Internetstiftelsen closes the Sweden slot; the Scandinavian funder footprint in the corpus maps to the two most direct national connectors to the European digital-rights ecosystem currently documented.
