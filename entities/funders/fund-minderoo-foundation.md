# Minderoo Foundation

Minderoo Foundation is an Australian private philanthropic foundation [established in 2001 by mining entrepreneur Andrew Forrest and his then-wife Nicola Forrest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minderoo_Foundation) and headquartered in Perth, Western Australia. Its endowment is anchored on the Forrest family's stake in Fortescue, the iron-ore mining company Andrew Forrest founded; major donations include USD 400 million in 2017, USD 520 million in 2020, and a 2023 transfer of Fortescue shares worth approximately USD 5 billion that was [described as the largest single charitable donation in Australian history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minderoo_Foundation). John Hartman, previously the foundation's chief investment officer, has served as CEO since October 2022, and Allan Myers became Board Chair in October 2024 after Andrew and Nicola Forrest stepped down from their co-chair roles while retaining board seats. The foundation [now organises its work around four current strategic priorities](https://www.minderoo.org/) — Communities, Gender Equality, Natural Ecosystems, and flexible Impact Missions — having moved through a substantial 2024 restructure that materially reshaped its programmatic surface area.

## A 2001 Forrest-fortune endowment with mining-derived endowment

The defining structural fact of Minderoo is its anchoring on the Forrest family's mining fortune. Andrew Forrest founded Fortescue (formerly Fortescue Metals Group) in 2003 and built one of the world's larger iron-ore producers; the foundation his and Nicola Forrest's family established in 2001 has been progressively endowed through transfers of Fortescue shares and Forrest-related capital. The foundation's [own about page](https://www.minderoo.org/) frames the organisation as "a proudly Australian philanthropy, working to forge a fair future by courageously challenging inequalities and campaigning for meaningful change," and the foundation has emerged as one of the largest Australian private philanthropic vehicles by both endowment and annual outlay — USD 210 million in active commitments for impact in FY24 per the [foundation's own 2024 wrap-up](https://www.minderoo.org/wrap-up-2024/).

The structural distinctiveness Minderoo brings to this corpus is geographic and fortune-anchor. It is the corpus's first Australia-headquartered funder of any kind, the corpus's first Oceania entry, and the corpus's first mining-fortune-anchored philanthropic vehicle — a structurally distinct posture from the corpus's existing tech-fortune-anchored funders ([Mozilla Foundation](fund-mozilla-foundation.md), [Omidyar Network](fund-omidyar-network.md)) and from its existing endowed-foundation funders whose endowments derive from manufacturing, oil, or other twentieth-century industrial fortunes ([Ford Foundation](fund-ford-foundation.md), [MacArthur Foundation](fund-macarthur-foundation.md), [Nuffield Foundation](fund-nuffield-foundation.md)).

## AI-good footprint — the Frontier Technology Initiative era

The substantive AI-good footprint Minderoo built was concentrated in a 2020–2024 strand of work named the Frontier Technology Initiative, which at its peak constituted one of the foundation's named grantmaking programmes. The signature artefact of the FTI was the [Tech Impact Network of five academic research centres at universities in Australia, the UK, and the US, anchored by initial grants of AU$20 million / USD 14 million](https://philanthropynewsdigest.org/news/minderoo-foundation-launches-14-million-global-tech-impact-network):

- The **Minderoo Centre for Technology and Democracy** at the University of Cambridge, hosted at the Centre for Research in the Arts, Social Sciences and Humanities (CRASSH), [continues to operate under Director Gina Neff and Co-Founder John Naughton](https://www.mctd.ac.uk/), with [primary funding from Minderoo Foundation and decisions concerning the Centre resting exclusively at Cambridge](https://www.crassh.cam.ac.uk/research/projects-centres/minderoo-centre-for-technology-and-democracy/);
- The **Minderoo Tech & Policy Lab** at the University of Western Australia, founded in October 2020 with independent gift funding from Minderoo Foundation in 2020 and 2021 and led by Associate Professor Julia Powles, was [formally recognised as a UWA Strategic Research Centre in November 2023, renamed the UWA Tech & Policy Lab, and now operates with no ongoing affiliation with Minderoo Foundation](https://www.uwa.edu.au/schools/research/uwa-tech-and-policy-lab) on a base of government, industry, and university funding;
- The **Minderoo Initiative on Technology and Power** at UCLA, [launched in August 2020 with USD 2.9 million in initial Minderoo funding to the UCLA Center for Critical Internet Inquiry under co-directors Safiya Umoja Noble and Sarah T. Roberts](https://newsroom.ucla.edu/releases/researchers-receive-2-9m-to-study-intersection-of-technology-power-and-society), anchored on the racial-justice-and-internet-studies register Noble's *Algorithms of Oppression* established;
- The **Minderoo Initiative on Technology and Society** at New York University; and
- The **Minderoo-Oxford Challenge Fund in AI Governance** at the University of Oxford, run as [a stimulus programme to Minderoo Foundation's global Tech Impact Network through the new Institute for Ethics in AI at the Stephen A. Schwarzman Centre for the Humanities](https://www.torch.ox.ac.uk/minderoo-oxford-challenge-fund-ai-governance), seeded as a series of experimental grants designed to expand the notion of AI ethics across history, art, performance, linguistics, governance, and community-led project work.

The network's organising frame, [as published at launch](https://philanthropynewsdigest.org/news/minderoo-foundation-launches-14-million-global-tech-impact-network), ran across three themes — tackling lawlessness in the technology ecosystem, empowering workers, and reimagining technology — and the foundation's posture toward the centres was uncompromising on monitoring the negative and harmful impacts of frontier technology on the general public, driving accountability of the tech ecosystem to international human rights, consulting with governments on law reform, and pursuing high-profile advocacy on behalf of the general public.

## The 2024 restructure and the FTI's recession from strategic centre

Under John Hartman's CEO tenure, Minderoo undertook a [substantial restructure in April 2024 that cut or transferred approximately 100 employees and consolidated several international offices](https://philanthropynewsdigest.org/news/minderoo-foundation-reorganizes-cuts-or-transfers-100-employees). Hartman's framing of the restructure was that the foundation has "a responsibility to meet the ambition and generosity of our founders" and that "under my leadership, Minderoo Foundation is undergoing a transformation to ensure we create maximum and lasting impact for our beneficiaries, with a higher portion of our corpus directed towards impact." The restructure was paired with a 2030 Strategy refresh whose [own foundation-side framing in the 2024 wrap-up](https://www.minderoo.org/wrap-up-2024/) names three core priorities — advancing gender equality, empowering communities, and protecting ecosystems — plus flexible Impact Missions for urgent crises, and emphasises a "partner-led approach" of empowering communities closest to challenges to drive solutions.

The Frontier Technology Initiative is not named as a standalone strategic priority in the foundation's current public-facing framing or in the 2024 wrap-up's programmatic highlights, and the foundation's home page lists only the four current strategic priorities (Communities, Gender Equality, Natural Ecosystems, Impact Missions). The five Tech Impact Network centres' subsequent trajectories have run differently: the Cambridge MCTD continues with Minderoo funding and active research output; the UWA centre has transitioned out of Minderoo affiliation and operates as the renamed UWA Tech & Policy Lab; the UCLA, NYU, and Oxford strands continue as named centres or fund-vehicles at their respective institutional hosts. The corpus entry treats the foundation as `status: active` (Minderoo Foundation itself remains an operating philanthropy with substantial annual outlay) while recording the AI-good footprint as the 2020–2024-anchored FTI strand whose institutional residue persists across the academic-centre network even as the foundation's own strategic centre has shifted away from technology as a named priority.

## Position in the corpus funder slice

Minderoo closes several structural slots none of the corpus's existing funder entries occupies. It is the corpus's first Oceania / Australia-headquartered foundation of any kind — the existing funder cluster runs heavily US-headquartered ([Ford Foundation](fund-ford-foundation.md), [MacArthur Foundation](fund-macarthur-foundation.md), [Mozilla Foundation](fund-mozilla-foundation.md), [Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md), [Omidyar Network](fund-omidyar-network.md), [Democracy Fund](fund-democracy-fund.md)), with one Continental European entry ([Stiftung Mercator](fund-stiftung-mercator.md)), UK entries ([Nuffield Foundation](fund-nuffield-foundation.md), [Sigrid Rausing Trust](fund-sigrid-rausing-trust.md)), one international entry headquartered in London ([Luminate](fund-luminate.md)), one Africa-administered re-granting fund ([African Digital Rights Fund](fund-african-digital-rights-fund.md)), one Latin America entry ([Fundación Avina](fund-fundacion-avina.md)), and one European pooled vehicle ([European AI & Society Fund](fund-european-ai-society-fund.md)) — with no entry from any Pacific, Australasian, or Oceanic jurisdiction. It is also the corpus's first mining-fortune-anchored philanthropic vehicle, distinguished from the tech-fortune-anchored Mozilla and Omidyar entries by the very different relationship between the founder's commercial activity and the foundation's tech-and-society work.

Structurally Minderoo is closest to the [Nuffield Foundation](fund-nuffield-foundation.md) in shape — an endowed private foundation whose AI-and-society work is concentrated in a hosted-or-funded academic-centre infrastructure rather than in direct grants to civil-society organisations. The corpus's structurally parallel pair is Nuffield's hosting of the [Ada Lovelace Institute](../organizations/org-ada-lovelace-institute.md), where Minderoo's structural shape is funding a transcontinental network of named centres at named universities rather than incubating a single hosted institute. The differences are equally instructive: Nuffield's commitment to Ada Lovelace is sustained as one of three named research centres the Foundation hosts; Minderoo's commitment to the Tech Impact Network was a 2020–2024 strand whose institutional priority has receded through the 2024 restructure even as several of the centres themselves persist.

Where the existing funder slice gives the corpus a window onto how US-fortune-anchored, UK-fortune-anchored, European-foundation, and Latin-American-headquartered philanthropy have resourced civil-society and academic work on AI, Minderoo is the corpus's principal window onto how an Australian mining-fortune-anchored foundation built — and has now substantially de-emphasised — a globally distributed academic-centre infrastructure on technology, democracy, and the public-interest critique of frontier technology.
