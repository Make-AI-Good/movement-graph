# Internet Society Foundation

The Internet Society Foundation is a US-headquartered supporting foundation [incorporated in the District of Columbia in 2017 and publicly launched in February 2019](https://www.isocfoundation.org/2019/11/internet-for-good-introducing-the-internet-society-foundation/) as the dedicated grantmaking arm of the [Internet Society](https://www.internetsociety.org/transparency/structure-and-related-organizations/), the technical-community membership organisation founded in 1992 by internet-protocol pioneers including Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn. The Internet Society is itself a District of Columbia nonprofit corporation and 501(c)(3) public charity, and serves as the [sole member of the Internet Society Foundation, the Public Interest Registry (PIR, the Pennsylvania 501(c)(3) public charity that operates the .ORG top-level domain), the IETF Administration LLC, and the Connected Giving Foundation](https://www.internetsociety.org/transparency/structure-and-related-organizations/). The Foundation operates out of [Fredericksburg, Virginia with twenty-six staff (FY2024)](https://www.causeiq.com/organizations/internet-society-foundation,823285688/) and is led by [President and CEO Sally Wentworth](https://www.isocfoundation.org/leadership-team/), who simultaneously serves as President and CEO of the Internet Society; the senior team also includes Chris Locke as Executive Vice President and Managing Director and Christopher Burns as Vice President for Philanthropy. The inaugural Executive Director from the 2019 launch was [Sarah Armstrong](https://www.isocfoundation.org/2019/11/internet-for-good-introducing-the-internet-society-foundation/), whose prior nonprofit experience focused on community-based grantmaking.

## Structure and financial pipeline

The Foundation's structural distinctiveness in the corpus's funder slice runs through its relationship to PIR. The Internet Society's transparency page describes PIR's purpose explicitly: [PIR "is a registry operator that manages the .ORG top-level domain (TLD) along with other TLDs for nonprofit organizations. PIR's activities also provide funding for the Internet Society and the Internet Society Foundation"](https://www.internetsociety.org/transparency/structure-and-related-organizations/). The Foundation is therefore the central programmatic surface through which revenue from the registration and renewal of .ORG domain names — the namespace under which most nonprofit, civil-society, and public-interest organisations on the Internet are registered — is converted into grants to civil-society organisations working on connectivity, digital inclusion, and Internet research. The pipeline is one of the more unusual financial architectures in the corpus's funder slice: the Foundation has no endowment-and-investment-return revenue model in the conventional sense, no fortune-anchored founder gift, and no government-channelling bilateral-aid pipeline; its operating capital is upstream-registry-routed from a sibling subsidiary that supplies its civil-society-customer base. The [Connected Giving Foundation, a separate Pennsylvania nonprofit, manages investment assets on behalf of both the Internet Society and the Internet Society Foundation](https://www.internetsociety.org/transparency/structure-and-related-organizations/).

The Foundation's FY2024 financials reflect the pipeline at operating scale. [Total revenues for FY2024 were $32.7 million, total expenses $33.9 million, total assets $90.3 million, and total grants paid $14.2 million on the partial public listing](https://www.causeiq.com/organizations/internet-society-foundation,823285688/). The Foundation's own 2024 impact summary [puts the granted total at $16.2 million to 203 grantees across 111 countries](https://www.isocfoundation.org/2025/03/2024-impact-report-how-16-2m-in-grants-powered-change-in-111-countries/) — the discrepancy with the Cause IQ figure reflects the standard Form-990 partial-listing convention rather than a substantive disagreement. The Board of Trustees of the Foundation is composed of the current trustees of the Internet Society itself: for the 2024–2025 term, Ted Hardie serves as Chair, with Olufunke Baruwa, Ramanou Biaou, Brian Haberman, Maureen Hilyard, Russ Housley, and Victor Kuarsingh as Treasurer; this overlapping-trustee structure reflects the Foundation's supporting-organisation status under the Internet Society.

## Grant portfolio

The Foundation's [eleven-programme grant portfolio](https://www.isocfoundation.org/funding-areas/) is the most thematically segmented in the corpus's funder slice. The eleven programmes can be grouped into five clusters that together describe the Foundation's connectivity-and-access programmatic identity.

The first and largest cluster is **building and expanding internet access**. [Community-Centered Connectivity](https://www.isocfoundation.org/grant-programme/community-centered-connectivity/) is the flagship programme: a three-track structure under which Catalyst grants of up to $50,000 fund 12-month projects developing new or expanding existing solutions in one digitally-excluded community; Scaling grants of up to $200,000 fund 18-month projects deploying proven solutions across multiple communities; and Systems grants of over $200,000 fund 24-month invitation-only large-scale replication. The programme explicitly addresses the three barriers to meaningful connectivity (availability, affordability, adoption) and states a stated preference for organisations serving refugees and displaced communities, Indigenous communities, and women and girls. The BOLT (Building Opportunities, Leveraging Technologies) programme funds technical and social innovations promoting Internet connectivity at smaller operational scale. The Connecting-the-Unconnected programme — the public-facing successor naming line for several of the above — has issued small grants in the $13,000–$40,000 range to named community-network operators across Africa, Asia, Latin America, and the United States: the [August 2025 announcement of nine 2025 grantees](https://www.isocfoundation.org/2025/08/announcing-a-new-group-of-9-grantee-partners-to-connect-the-unconnected-worldwide/) named recipients including Hahatay in Senegal ($20,000), Zimbabwe Community Network Initiatives ($13,783), Centro de Investigación en Comunicación Comunitaria in Mexico ($40,000), Kakuma Ventures across Kenya and Uganda ($20,000), MAGUFINA in Malawi ($40,000), Oasis Mathare Community Network in Kenya ($39,973), and Project Hello World in Uganda ($37,964); the [June 2024 announcement of ten 2024 grantees](https://www.isocfoundation.org/2024/06/new-round-of-funding-awarded-for-building-and-expanding-internet-connectivity/) named Portal sem Porteiras in Brazil ($16,500), the Welfare Association for New Generation Balochistan in Pakistan ($23,000), Athi Community Network in Kenya ($37,457), BOSCO-U in Uganda ($22,391), Equitable Origin in the United States ($19,500), Kijiji Yeetu in Kenya ($17,517), and Media Awareness and Justice Initiative in Nigeria ($19,207).

The second cluster is **infrastructure for the technical layer of the Internet**. Sustainable Peering Infrastructure funds local Internet Exchange Points (IXPs) and the organisations that operate them; Resiliency Grants fund preparedness for and recovery from climate-related disruption of Internet infrastructure. These programmes are the most technically-targeted in the corpus's funder slice, with little parallel in any other entry.

The third cluster is **research**. The [Research Grant Program](https://www.isocfoundation.org/grant-programme/research-grant-programme/) is structurally significant: up to $200,000 for independent researchers and up to $500,000 for institutions, with grants pegged to four named themes — Inclusive Internet on barriers to digital access; Greening the Internet on energy use and resource extraction in Internet infrastructure; Measuring Meaningful Connectivity on measurement frameworks beyond basic access and on the emerging-technology landscape including AI and cloud services; and A Trustworthy Internet on emerging risks and threats to the Internet as an open and reliable platform. The Measuring Meaningful Connectivity theme is the most explicit AI-adjacent programmatic surface in the Foundation's portfolio — it names AI and cloud services in the description — although the framing is firmly access-side rather than rights-side.

The fourth cluster is **digital skills and chapter participation**. SCILLS (Skills, Capabilities, and Internet Leadership for Local Sustainability) funds internet-skills development; Beyond the Net, the Foundation's oldest named programme inherited from the parent Internet Society in 2019, funds [Internet Society chapter-led local projects](https://www.isocfoundation.org/grant-programme/beyond-the-net-grant-program/); Chapter Admin Funding underwrites the operational running of ISOC chapters worldwide; Internet Governance Forum Events funds participation in local, national, regional, and global IGF processes and Internet Governance schools.

The fifth and newest cluster is **public-interest cybersecurity and encryption**. The [Common Good Cyber Fund launched in 2025](https://www.isocfoundation.org/funding-areas/) sustains nonprofit organisations whose work strengthens the public-interest cybersecurity ecosystem; Global Encryption Day Events funds community-led activities celebrating strong encryption.

## Named in-corpus funding relationship

The Foundation's named institutional connection to this corpus runs through one entry. The [Association for Progressive Communications](../organizations/org-apc.md) lists [the Internet Society among its current foundation-and-bilateral-aid funders on its public funders page](https://www.apc.org/en/about/people/funders), alongside the Dutch Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the European Union's EIDHR, the [Ford Foundation](fund-ford-foundation.md), the Global Fund for Women, IDRC, [Luminate](fund-luminate.md), the Oak Foundation, Sida, the UK FCDO, and the UK Digital Access Programme. APC's page attributes the funding to "Internet Society" rather than to the Foundation specifically; post-2019 ISOC grantmaking runs through the Foundation as the sole grantmaking surface for ISOC's civil-society programmes, and the APC line should be read accordingly. APC's role as the global network-of-networks coordinating Global-South digital-rights civil-society, including on AI governance, is precisely the kind of pan-regional infrastructure that the Foundation's chapter-and-network grantmaking is structured to back, and the relationship is the clearest in-corpus expression of the Foundation's connectivity-civil-society grantmaking translating into the rights-and-accountability side of the field.

Beyond APC, the Foundation's named 2024 and 2025 community-network grantees (Portal sem Porteiras, Welfare Association for New Generation Balochistan, Athi Community Network, BOSCO-U, MAGUFINA, Oasis Mathare Community Network, and the others) sit one step below the corpus's current entity threshold — they are mostly small, single-region community-network operators rather than the national or regional advocacy organisations the corpus currently records, and the bridge from those grantees to the corpus's advocacy slice runs through the regional sub-granting infrastructures (APC's community-networks line, [African Digital Rights Fund](fund-african-digital-rights-fund.md), [Indela](fund-indela.md)) rather than through direct ISOC Foundation grantmaking. The Foundation's AI-good footprint in this corpus is therefore best read as the underlying connectivity layer onto which the corpus's rights-and-accountability work projects, rather than as a direct AI-grantmaking line — closer in shape to [Shuttleworth Foundation](fund-shuttleworth-foundation.md)'s second-order AI-good footprint through alumni fellows than to the direct AI-and-society grantmaking lines of [Ford](fund-ford-foundation.md), [MacArthur](fund-macarthur-foundation.md), or the [European AI & Society Fund](fund-european-ai-society-fund.md).

## Position in the corpus funder slice

Within the funder slice of this corpus the Internet Society Foundation is the first connectivity-and-digital-inclusion-focused funder. The existing funder cluster runs heavily toward rights-and-accountability grantmaking — the four large US-headquartered foundations [Ford](fund-ford-foundation.md), [MacArthur](fund-macarthur-foundation.md), [Mozilla](fund-mozilla-foundation.md), and the [Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md) all run programmes on AI accountability, surveillance, content moderation, and platform governance; [Hivos](fund-hivos.md) runs the Civic Rights in a Digital Age impact area and the Digital Defenders Partnership; [Luminate](fund-luminate.md) runs digital-rights and tech-accountability programmes from the Omidyar lineage; [Adessium](fund-adessium-foundation.md), [Sigrid Rausing Trust](fund-sigrid-rausing-trust.md), and [Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md) anchor the European civil-society rights field. The Internet Society Foundation does not principally fund any of those rights-and-accountability lines. Its programmatic identity is set one layer below them — at the connectivity, community-network, peering-infrastructure, internet-skills, internet-governance-participation, and applied-internet-research layer that all of the above rights-and-accountability work sits on top of and ultimately depends on. It is the corpus's first window onto that infrastructure-and-access layer of the digital-rights philanthropic field, and the synth-flagged rationale for adding it — "complements the rights / accountability funder cluster" — is exactly that complementarity.

The closest functional analogue inside the corpus is the [African Digital Rights Fund](fund-african-digital-rights-fund.md): both are pooled-funding vehicles disbursing many small grants ($1,000–$10,000 range for ADRF; $13,000–$40,000 range for the Foundation's Connecting-the-Unconnected programme) to a wide cohort of small grantees. But ADRF is a regional re-granting fund hosted by an African convening organisation (CIPESA) and explicitly oriented at digital-rights advocacy and litigation; the Foundation is a global supporting-foundation arm of a technical-community membership organisation oriented at connectivity infrastructure and digital inclusion. The two are at adjacent layers of the same field but with different programmatic centres of gravity.

The structural feature that is most distinctive of the Foundation in this corpus is its [PIR / .ORG-registry-funded pipeline](https://www.internetsociety.org/transparency/structure-and-related-organizations/). The other Northern foundations in the corpus run on private endowments ([Ford](fund-ford-foundation.md), [MacArthur](fund-macarthur-foundation.md), [Sigrid Rausing Trust](fund-sigrid-rausing-trust.md), [Nuffield](fund-nuffield-foundation.md), [Stiftung Mercator](fund-stiftung-mercator.md), [Robert Bosch Stiftung](fund-robert-bosch-stiftung.md)), founder gifts ([Omidyar Network](fund-omidyar-network.md), [Minderoo](fund-minderoo-foundation.md), [Kapor Foundation](fund-kapor-foundation.md), [Shuttleworth](fund-shuttleworth-foundation.md)), government-and-lottery channels ([Hivos](fund-hivos.md)), or pooled-philanthropy contributions ([European AI & Society Fund](fund-european-ai-society-fund.md)). The Internet Society Foundation is the corpus's first funder whose grantmaking is principally underwritten by a sibling registry's domain-name revenue — a structurally novel pipeline in which the civil-society sector's own digital infrastructure (the .ORG namespace) is the philanthropic engine. This is also the most direct illustration in the corpus's funder slice of how the technical-community wing of the Internet's institutional history connects financially to the grassroots and civil-society wing the corpus principally tracks.
