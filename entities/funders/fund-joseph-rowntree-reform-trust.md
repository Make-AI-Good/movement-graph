# Joseph Rowntree Reform Trust

The Joseph Rowntree Reform Trust (JRRT) is a UK non-charitable limited company [established in 1904](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/) by Quaker industrialist Joseph Rowntree — the same founder whose legacy anchors the Joseph Rowntree Foundation and the Joseph Rowntree Charitable Trust — with a mandate to fund political and democratic reform in Britain. Registered in England [as Company No. 357963](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/about-us) and headquartered at The Garden House, Water End, York, JRRT is staffed by [five employees (three full-time, two part-time)](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/about-us) and governed by a Board of Directors with a Finance Committee [chaired by Duncan Hames](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/about-us). The Trust's current frame describes [both "a positive agenda to revitalise and strengthen democratic resilience" and "a protective agenda"](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/) against anti-democratic and authoritarian movements in the UK.

## A non-charitable company by design

The defining structural fact about JRRT is that it is not a charity. Unlike the [Joseph Rowntree Foundation](fund-joseph-rowntree-foundation.md) (registered charity number 1184957) and the Joseph Rowntree Charitable Trust (a separately-registered Quaker philanthropic trust), JRRT was [deliberately constituted as a limited company](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/about-us) so that it can fund direct political campaigning, advocacy, and electoral-system reform work that UK charity law prohibits registered charities from supporting. This is not a structural anomaly but the reason for JRRT's existence: its endowment was established precisely to fill the funding gap that charity law leaves — to resource campaigning that complements research and community development funded through the charitable Rowntree arms. JRRT previously operated as the [Joseph Rowntree Social Service Trust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Rowntree_Reform_Trust) until 1990, when it adopted its current name to reflect the explicitly political character of its mandate. The Trust [endowed the JRSST Charitable Trust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Rowntree_Reform_Trust) as a separate vehicle for work requiring charitable status, preserving the full range of Rowntree philanthropic intent across the two entities.

## Funding priorities

JRRT organises its grantmaking around [five core democracy priorities](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/what-we-fund). **Money in Politics** funds campaigns for tighter campaign-finance regulation, transparency on political giving, scrutiny of cash-for-honours practices, and tackling improper lobbying. **Voter Participation and Elections System Reform** supports automatic voter registration, votes at sixteen, and counter-voter-suppression work. **Political Rights — Freedom of Expression and Protest Rights** backs campaigns to repeal restrictive protest legislation, build protest-rights knowledge and training, and hold protest policing to account. **Disinformation** funds monitoring and regulation of [bots, deepfakes, and fake commentators](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/what-we-fund), strengthening penalties for officials spreading false information, platform regulation, and citizen media literacy. **Checks and Balances** covers electoral integrity, parliamentary accountability, independent appointment processes, and functional freedom-of-information regimes.

Across its current portfolio, confirmed grantees include [the Democracy Network](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/grants) (£449,826, 2026, democracy), [Fair Vote UK](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/grants) (£47,100, 2026), the [Bureau of Investigative Journalism](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/grants) (£50,000, 2026, freedom of expression and protest rights), and [DeSmog UK](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/grants) (£50,000, 2026, money in politics). The Trust also offers [fast-track grants of up to £10,000 with a two-week response time](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/grants) and moves toward multiyear and core funding arrangements for strategic partners. A [360Giving-compatible dataset](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/grants) of JRRT's grants since January 2015 is available for download.

## Digital civil liberties and AI oversight footprint

JRRT's AI-good relevance runs through its digital civil liberties grantmaking and its Disinformation priority. The Trust's Disinformation work explicitly targets regulation of [deepfakes and AI-generated content](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/what-we-fund) alongside bots and fake commentators — a direct AI throughline inside JRRT's democratic-integrity agenda. Its Political Rights priority encompasses the surveillance accountability and censorship-resistance work that digital civil liberties organisations carry.

Among its confirmed grantees, [Big Brother Watch](../organizations/org-big-brother-watch.md) is a current recipient: Big Brother Watch's own [funding transparency page](https://bigbrotherwatch.org.uk/funding/) names JRRT as a current grant-maker under the description "protecting rights and democracy against digital threats." Big Brother Watch is the UK's primary campaign organisation against live facial recognition technology, having forced suspension of police deployments and brought strategic legal challenges against AI-powered biometric surveillance — its work sits at the core of the AI-oversight strand in UK civil society. The [Open Rights Group](https://www.jrrt.org.uk/grants), also a confirmed JRRT grantee, campaigns on data protection, privacy, and algorithmic profiling, working the full stack of UK and EU digital rights regulation from the grassroots up.

## Position in the corpus funder slice

Within the funder slice of this corpus JRRT occupies a structural slot no other current entry holds: the **non-charitable political-campaign funder**. Every other UK funder in the corpus is a registered charity or charitable foundation; JRRT is the only entry whose legal structure is designed to fund what charities cannot. That structural choice is load-bearing for the movement: civil liberties campaigning on AI surveillance, platform regulation, and protest rights sits in the gap between research (funded by foundations) and formal litigation (funded by strategic-litigation funders like the Sigrid Rausing Trust), and it is precisely this campaigning tier that JRRT's non-charitable structure enables it to resource.

The three other Rowntree organisations neighbouring JRRT in the corpus are distinct along the same structural axis. The [Joseph Rowntree Foundation](fund-joseph-rowntree-foundation.md) is a registered charity running AI-specific research and convening through its AI for Public Good programme, with a poverty-first lens and an explicitly neutral political posture; the Joseph Rowntree Charitable Trust is a separately governed Quaker charitable trust funding strategic-litigation and civil-liberties organisations; and JRRT is the only one of the three with a mandate to fund direct political advocacy, electoral reform, and political-campaigning organisations. The non-charitable design that distinguishes JRRT from its Rowntree siblings is the same property that makes it the natural funder of Big Brother Watch and Open Rights Group — organisations whose most effective work is political campaigning that UK charity law would constrain a registered charity from funding on the same terms.
