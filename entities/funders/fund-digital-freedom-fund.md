# Digital Freedom Fund

The Digital Freedom Fund is a Netherlands-registered, Amsterdam-based pooled vehicle [launched on 25 January 2018](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/digital-freedom-fund-launches-to-support-digital-rights-litigation-in-europe/) to fund and support strategic litigation advancing and protecting digital rights across Europe and, in some cases, beyond. At launch its founding director was Nani Jansen Reventlow, described as ["an experienced human rights lawyer and strategic litigator"](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/digital-freedom-fund-launches-to-support-digital-rights-litigation-in-europe/), and its operations were anchored in Berlin and Brussels, with a [registered address at Korte Lijnbaanssteeg 1-4513, 1012 SL Amsterdam](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/digital-freedom-fund-launches-to-support-digital-rights-litigation-in-europe/). Since launch the Fund has made [nearly 150 grants totalling over €5 million](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/about/) across [30 countries](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/about/), supporting some [90 organisations](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/about/) and producing [over 200 positive legal, policy, and social outcomes](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/about/). Its [400+ organisation network](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/about/) is the broadest single-vehicle community of digital-rights litigators and advocates in Europe.

## Founding and structure

The DFF's [four seed funders](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/digital-freedom-fund-launches-to-support-digital-rights-litigation-in-europe/) were [Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md), [Adessium Foundation](fund-adessium-foundation.md), [Omidyar Network](fund-omidyar-network.md), and the Renewable Freedom Foundation. Its [first open grant call was scheduled for April 2018](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/digital-freedom-fund-launches-to-support-digital-rights-litigation-in-europe/), with David Kaye, then UN Special Rapporteur on the right to freedom of opinion and expression, named as a Friend of the Fund at launch. By 2024–26 the Fund had moved to a distributed [co-director leadership model](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/about/), and grant decision-making had shifted to a community-led [Peer Group model](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/about/) that places affected communities at the centre of funding choices. The Fund operates alongside its grantmaking programme with a [400+ organisation network](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/about/), convening events, developing litigation skills-building resources, and running the [digiRISE programme](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/about/) to build civil-society capacity to enforce EU Charter of Fundamental Rights rights in digital contexts. Thematically its current infrastructure runs through an [AI and Digital Infrastructure Hub](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/) and a [Digital Democracy Hub](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/).

Its [current funders](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/funding/) include [Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md), [Luminate](fund-luminate.md), [Oak Foundation](fund-oak-foundation.md), [Ford Foundation](fund-ford-foundation.md), and the Limelight Foundation. Prior funders listed on its funding-transparency page include [Robert Bosch Stiftung](fund-robert-bosch-stiftung.md), [Stiftung Mercator](fund-stiftung-mercator.md), the Renewable Freedom Foundation, [Sigrid Rausing Trust](fund-sigrid-rausing-trust.md), the EU CERV programme, and the Democracy and Media Foundation. The Fund operates under an [Ethical Funding Policy](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/funding/) that governs which sources it accepts.

## Grantmaking model

Grants run on two tracks. [Litigation Track Support](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/grants/) carries cases through multiple court instances and now includes post-litigation activities — advocacy and enforcement following judgment — with an average award of [€45,000](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/grants/). [Pre-Litigation Research Support](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/grants/) funds preparation work before litigation commences — legal research, evidence gathering, identifying claimants — at an average of [€25,000](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/grants/). The [annual grantmaking budget is approximately €800,000](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/grants/), with [approximately 20 grants approved per year](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/grants/) through [regular open calls](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/grants/) (the most recent ran December 2025 to February 2026). Individual grants [range from €3,000 to over €100,000 in rare cases](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/grants/).

The [geographic scope centres on Council of Europe member states](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/grants/), with some extension to Latin America and Africa when the litigation itself falls within those jurisdictions. Eligible applicants include NGOs, pro bono lawyers, and advocacy groups across racial justice, feminist, environmental, and other movement contexts. The [strategic litigation eligibility criterion](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/grants/) requires that cases have "potential for impact extending beyond the parties directly involved" and capacity to "bring about legislative, policy or social change" — a threshold that distinguishes DFF grants from individual legal-aid and positions the Fund explicitly as a movement infrastructure vehicle rather than a case-by-case legal charity.

The DFF's [five current focus areas](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/) are AI and digital infrastructure, platform accountability, digital democracy, digital environmental justice, and spyware. The [case studies portfolio](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/case-studies/) spans AI and algorithmic discrimination (welfare risk-scoring, predictive policing, algorithmic benefit-denial), platform accountability (DSA-grounded gender-based censorship of sexual and reproductive health content, Facebook data breach mass actions), surveillance and spyware (Pegasus deployment in Spain, Europol surveillance), gig work (platform location-tracking accountability), LGBTQI+ digital identity rights, and asylum and migration (Frontex geolocation surveillance, device-extraction challenges).

## Portfolio — connections to the corpus

Three in-corpus entities carry documented DFF grant relationships that predate or are contemporaneous with the drafting of this entry.

The most detailed on record is the [JCWI / Foxglove challenge to the Home Office visa-streaming algorithm](../campaigns/camp-foxglove-jcwi-visa-streaming-2020.md). DFF's [own case page](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/uk-home-office-visa-application-streaming-algorithm/) records [€35,702 in emergency-litigation and single-instance grants](../campaigns/camp-foxglove-jcwi-visa-streaming-2020.md) that underwrote the Foxglove–JCWI judicial review claim, which in August 2020 secured the first UK government concession ending a live judicial-review challenge to a government algorithmic decision-making system.

[La Quadrature du Net](../organizations/org-la-quadrature-du-net.md) is named in the DFF [case studies portfolio](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/case-studies/) as the lead litigant (with 14 co-organisations) in the French CNAF algorithmic discrimination challenge — litigation against the French Family Benefits Agency's welfare risk-scoring algorithm, begun in October 2024, asserting that the system produces discriminatory targeting of marginalized benefit claimants.

[Panoptykon Foundation](../organizations/org-panoptykon-foundation.md) [lists the Digital Freedom Fund](https://en.panoptykon.org/about-us/funding) as one of its current funders on its own funding-transparency page, establishing the Warsaw-based digital-rights foundation as a DFF grantee alongside its Open Society Foundations, Sigrid Rausing Trust, European AI & Society Fund, and Civitates relationships.

The SyRI case — the 2020 Hague District Court ruling that the Netherlands' welfare fraud risk-scoring algorithm was unlawful under the European Convention on Human Rights — is documented in the corpus at [`event-syri-hague-district-court-ruling-2020-02-05`](../events/event-syri-hague-district-court-ruling-2020-02-05.md). The DFF's [own retrospective on the case](https://digitalfreedomfund.org/case-studies/) names it as one of the Fund's portfolio precedents for algorithmic-accountability litigation, and the SyRI event entity cites a DFF retrospective piece among its sources.

## Position in the corpus funder slice

The Digital Freedom Fund fills a structural slot in the corpus that no existing funder entry occupies: a pooled re-granting vehicle whose express mandate is strategic litigation in digital rights, as distinct from either direct-grantmaking foundations (Adessium, Ford, Open Society Foundations) or cause-area pooled vehicles whose scope is broader than litigation (the [European AI & Society Fund](fund-european-ai-society-fund.md), which funds civil-society advocacy, research, and capacity-building across the AI policy field). Within the broader funder slice, the Fund sits closest to the [European AI & Society Fund](fund-european-ai-society-fund.md) in organisational form — both are Netherlands-registered pooled vehicles re-granting to European civil-society actors — but differs in its instrument (litigation specifically) and in the communities it explicitly centres (affected communities, including Roma, people on the move, LGBTQI+ communities, women, and gig workers, as constituent voice in grant decision-making rather than as beneficiary only).

The Fund is also the corpus's clearest specimen of litigation-infrastructure funding: a vehicle whose value to the field lies not primarily in any single grant or case, but in maintaining a permanent, Europe-wide capacity to bring digital rights into courtrooms that would otherwise find few institutional pathways for strategic-litigation resourcing. The [€35,702 JCWI case grant](../campaigns/camp-foxglove-jcwi-visa-streaming-2020.md) — one of the Fund's earliest UK-facing grants — illustrates the model: a small emergency allocation to a campaign that produced the UK's first judicial concession ending a live algorithmic-accountability claim.

Where the existing funder slice gives the corpus windows onto US-fortune-anchored philanthropy ([Ford Foundation](fund-ford-foundation.md), [Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md), [Luminate](fund-luminate.md)), EA-aligned AI-safety grantmaking ([Coefficient Giving](fund-coefficient-giving.md)), Dutch family-foundation bilateral grantmaking ([Adessium Foundation](fund-adessium-foundation.md)), and pan-European pooled civil-society resourcing ([European AI & Society Fund](fund-european-ai-society-fund.md)), the Digital Freedom Fund is the corpus's principal window onto pooled strategic-litigation infrastructure for the European digital-rights and AI-accountability movement.
