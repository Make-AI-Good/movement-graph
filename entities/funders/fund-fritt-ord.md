# Fritt Ord Foundation

The Fritt Ord Foundation — *Stiftelsen Fritt Ord*, "the free word" — is a private Norwegian public-utility foundation [established on 7 June 1974 and operational from 1 January 1975](https://frittord.no/en/about/the-history-of-fritt-ord), headquartered in Oslo and constituted as the sole major Norwegian foundation dedicated to protecting and promoting freedom of expression. Its [Articles of Association define its paramount object](https://frittord.no/en/about/what-is-the-fritt-ord-foundation) as protecting and promoting freedom of expression and the environment for freedom of expression in Norway, especially by encouraging lively debate and through the courageous use of free speech. The foundation carries [an endowment of NOK 1 billion](https://frittord.no/en/about/what-is-the-fritt-ord-foundation), which is locked under its constituting rules and cannot be paid out as grants; all grantmaking is funded from investment returns and income generated by the capital base. Knut Olav Åmås has served as Executive Director since September 2014; [Bård Vegar Solhjell — a former Norwegian MP, government minister (Education, Environment), and director general of NORAD — assumed the Board Chair role on 1 July 2025](https://frittord.no/en/news/bard-vegar-solhjell-new-chair-of-the-fritt-ord-board), succeeding Grete Brochmann who chaired from 2016 to 2025.

## Origins in the Narvesen succession

The foundation's origins lie in a post-World-War-II decision about who should own the infrastructure of the Norwegian press. The Narvesen Kiosk Company was Norway's sole newspaper and periodical distribution channel — a monopoly node in the information supply chain whose ownership represented a structural question for the long-term independence of the press. When the Narvesen owners chose to place the company's assets under a public-interest foundation rather than allowing them to pass to private or commercial ownership, [the Fritt Ord Foundation was established in 1974 as that vehicle](https://frittord.no/en/about/the-history-of-fritt-ord), with the express purpose of holding and deploying the capital in service of freedom of expression. The occupation-era experience of a single party controlling print distribution — and the suppression that entailed — sits in the background of that founding choice; Fritt Ord was constituted precisely to prevent a recurrence through structural means. The foundation has since divested from direct press-infrastructure ownership and now operates as a pure grant-making vehicle, but the Narvesen succession remains the constituting origin story, and the NOK 1 billion endowment is its capital legacy.

## Domestic grantmaking programs

Fritt Ord's domestic Norwegian grantmaking runs across several distinct program areas. **Journalism** is the highest-volume line: the foundation [announced a NOK 100 million earmark over four years in 2017 — NOK 25 million per year — for the Quality Journalism in a New Era programme](https://frittord.no/en/news/fritt-ord-to-grant-mnok-100-to-promote-norwegian-journalism), described as nearly doubling its prior annual journalism-grant level of NOK 12–15 million; the programme targets structural support for independent journalism capable of sustaining investigative and public-interest reporting in the digital era. **Documentary film and photography** accounts for approximately 15 percent of the foundation's annual budget; notable funded documentaries include Tonje Hessen Schei's *iHuman* (2019) — an examination of AI surveillance, algorithmic power, and civil liberties — alongside *Do Not Split* (Hong Kong protests, 2019) and Terje Tofiord's *Gunda*. **Freedom-of-expression monitoring** has been the subject of a continuous independent research project running from 2013 to 2026, tracking the status of freedom of expression in Norway across media, law, and public life. **Culture and the arts** funds include the Store Norske Leksikon (Norway's national encyclopaedia), literary prizes, and a critics and satirists grant scheme. **Student grants** of NOK 40,000 are offered annually for master's thesis work explicitly naming "the role of technology in freedom of expression" as an eligible topic. The foundation also co-runs the **Free Media Awards** for Eastern European independent press — in partnership with ZEIT STIFTUNG BUCERIUS since 2004 — as a standing international program outside the domestic focus.

## Civitates — founding partner and principal digital-rights channel

Fritt Ord's most consequential channel for AI-good and digital-rights impact is its position as one of the sixteen founding member foundations of [Civitates](fund-civitates.md) — the Brussels-headquartered pooled European democracy fund whose Tech & Democracy sub-fund directly resources civil-society organisations working to enforce the EU Digital Services Act, AI Act, and GDPR. [Civitates was publicly announced on 28 February 2019](https://civitates-eu.org/sixteen-european-philanthropy-organisations-join-forces-to-create-civitates-a-consortium-to-protect-and-expand-civil-society-in-europe/), naming Fritt Ord alongside Adessium Foundation, the Stefan Batory Foundation, Bertelsmann Stiftung, the European Cultural Foundation, ERSTE Stiftung, Fondation de France, the King Baudouin Foundation, Körber Stiftung, [Luminate](fund-luminate.md), the [Charles Stewart Mott Foundation](fund-charles-stewart-mott-foundation.md), Nicolas Puech Foundation, the Oak Foundation, the [Open Society Foundations](fund-open-society-foundations.md), the [Robert Bosch Stiftung](fund-robert-bosch-stiftung.md), and [Stiftung Mercator](fund-stiftung-mercator.md) as the inaugural co-founding members. [Fritt Ord's own Civitates project page](https://frittord.no/en/projects/international-projects/civitates) frames the partnership as a response to democratic backsliding across Europe and positions it as the foundation's primary structured vehicle for grantmaking beyond Norway's borders.

Through the Civitates Tech & Democracy sub-fund, Fritt Ord co-funds the corpus's two most prominent European algorithmic-accountability organisations — [AlgorithmWatch](../organizations/org-algorithmwatch.md) and [Access Now](../organizations/org-access-now.md) — alongside [European Digital Rights (EDRi)](../organizations/org-edri.md) and a current cohort of eight anchor grantees working on DSA and AI Act civil-society enforcement. A [2025 Civitates Tech & Democracy open call](https://frittord.no/en/news/civitates-tech-democracy-open-call) announced by Fritt Ord offered a €1.6 million pool (up to €160,000 per grantee) to organisations in Hungary, Italy, Poland, Slovakia, Spain, Romania, and the Netherlands engaged in DSA implementation oversight — a direct continuation of the sub-fund's strategy of seeding national-level civil-society capacity to hold digital platforms accountable under EU law. Because Civitates is a pooled re-granting vehicle, Fritt Ord's individual contribution is not separately disclosed; the foundation is one of the funding co-signatories on every Tech & Democracy grant rather than the named funder of any individual organisation.

## Position in the corpus funder slice

Within the corpus's funder slice Fritt Ord fills the first Norway-anchored slot and the first Nordic / Scandinavian standalone freedom-of-expression foundation — distinct from the Civitates partner roster itself (which lists Fritt Ord as one member among sixteen, and which is already in the corpus as [fund-civitates](fund-civitates.md)). Where the corpus's previous European funder entries divide principally between Brussels-pooled vehicles ([Civitates](fund-civitates.md), [European AI & Society Fund](fund-european-ai-society-fund.md)), large multi-programme European foundations ([Robert Bosch Stiftung](fund-robert-bosch-stiftung.md), [Stiftung Mercator](fund-stiftung-mercator.md), [Bertelsmann Stiftung](fund-bertelsmann-stiftung.md), [Fondation de France](fund-fondation-de-france.md)), and UK / Ireland trusts ([Nuffield Foundation](fund-nuffield-foundation.md), [Joseph Rowntree Foundation](fund-joseph-rowntree-foundation.md), [Sigrid Rausing Trust](fund-sigrid-rausing-trust.md)), Fritt Ord is the first single-country Nordic foundation with a freedom-of-expression mandate that connects to the European digital-rights funding ecosystem via Civitates co-founding. Its domestic remit — journalism, documentary, cultural expression, and freedom-of-expression monitoring in Norway — is a structurally different shape from the Brussels-pooled vehicles and the multi-programme continental foundations; it is a national-mandate foundation whose *international* impact flows almost entirely through its co-founding role in Civitates and the Tech & Democracy grantmaking that role enables, rather than through its own direct cross-border grant programs.

The documentary co-funding thread — particularly *iHuman*, Tonje Hessen Schei's 2019 examination of AI surveillance and algorithmic civil-liberties — illustrates how Fritt Ord's domestic grantmaking surface periodically intersects the AI-good terrain through cultural artifact support rather than civil-society advocacy grants, a distinct vector from every other funder entry in the corpus.
