# Maiam nayri Wingara campaign for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander data sovereignty in AI governance (2021–ongoing)

[Maiam nayri Wingara](../organizations/org-maiam-nayri-wingara.md)'s campaign to establish Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander data sovereignty as a foundational principle for Australia's AI governance frameworks represents one of the most consistently articulated Indigenous AI rights positions in the Oceania region. Running as a sustained multi-front effort since approximately 2021, the campaign connects the collective's Indigenous Data Sovereignty (IDSov) frameworks to the evolving AI policy landscape — pressing for governance structures that ensure Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities retain authority over their data across the full AI lifecycle rather than accepting retrospective consultation mechanisms. Its fronts span formal government consultation submissions, national summit convening, public advocacy, and international coalition-building through the [Global Indigenous Data Alliance](../organizations/org-global-indigenous-data-alliance.md).

## Intellectual foundation — Walter and Kukutai (2019)

The campaign's intellectual foundation was laid in July 2019 when Maggie Walter, Maiam nayri Wingara co-founder, and Tahu Kukutai of [Te Mana Raraunga](../organizations/org-te-mana-raraunga.md) submitted ["Artificial Intelligence and Indigenous Data Sovereignty"](https://acola.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/07/acola-ai-input-paper_indigenous-data-sovereignty_walter-kukutai.pdf) as an input paper to the Australian Council of Learned Academies (ACOLA) Horizon Scanning Project on "The Effective and Ethical Development of Artificial Intelligence." The paper argued three propositions that have since anchored the campaign: that growing evidence of racial bias in algorithms means AI has real negative impacts on Indigenous peoples; that Indigenous voices are systematically absent from AI algorithm development from conception through to maintenance despite these systems being applied to Indigenous communities; and that AI development must comply with Indigenous data sovereignty principles, specifically the CARE Principles and the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples. The ACOLA project fed directly into Australia's AI ethics consultation; the paper placed Indigenous data sovereignty within the formal terms of Australia's first national AI governance exercise.

## Closing the Gap and the Australian AI policy context (2020 onwards)

The National Agreement on Closing the Gap, signed in July 2020 by the Commonwealth and all state and territory governments alongside the Coalition of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peak Organisations, embedded Indigenous data sovereignty as Priority Reform 4 — making IDSov a binding cross-government policy commitment rather than an aspiration. This created structural leverage for the campaign: any AI governance framework developed by Australian governments that did not incorporate Indigenous data sovereignty would be in tension with commitments already made under the National Agreement. From 2021 onwards, as Australia's AI policy agenda accelerated with the release of its first AI Action Plan in June 2021, the campaign increasingly pressed for those Priority Reform 4 commitments to extend explicitly into AI development, deployment, and oversight.

## Universities Accord engagement (April 2023)

Maiam nayri Wingara [made a formal submission](https://www.education.gov.au/australian-universities-accord/consultations/australian-universities-accord-panel-discussion-paper-consultation/submission/16274) to the Australian Universities Accord consultation in April 2023, titled "Universities Accord — The Erasure of Indigenous Leadership and Governance." The submission argued that universities are not merely creators of data about Indigenous peoples but are themselves part of Indigenous data ecosystems, and that Indigenous sovereignty over those ecosystems must be structurally enforced. Key demands included requiring every university to commit to Article 14 of UNDRIP (the right of Indigenous peoples to establish and control educational systems); requiring universities to recognize Indigenous peoples' right to exercise control over data ecosystems including creation, development, stewardship, analysis, dissemination, and infrastructure; and mandating Indigenous identified PVC/DVC and Co-Chancellor positions within every institution. The AI governance implication is direct: universities are central sites of AI research and development; IDSov applied to universities means Indigenous sovereignty at the AI development stage, not downstream of it.

## 2023 National Indigenous Data Sovereignty Summit (June 2023)

In June 2023, Maiam nayri Wingara co-convened the second National Indigenous Data Sovereignty Summit in partnership with the Australian Indigenous Governance Institute and the Lowitja Institute — bringing together more than 130 Indigenous delegates from every Australian State and Territory alongside international participants from Aotearoa New Zealand, Canada, and the United States. Where the 2018 summit had focused on definitional and principled work, the 2023 summit was explicitly oriented toward operationalization: how do Indigenous data sovereignty principles get embedded in institutional practice, including in digital and AI contexts? The summit produced the Indigenous Data Governance Communique (published August 2023), addressed to all entities involved in the creation, collection, access, analysis, interpretation, management, dissemination, and reuse of data and data infrastructure in Australia. The communique placed data governance in AI contexts within the broader demand for operational IDSov implementation — calling on data-handling institutions, including those developing AI systems, to govern Indigenous data under Indigenous authority.

## Named AI harms and public advocacy positioning (October 2024)

By October 2024, Maiam nayri Wingara's AI governance position had crystallized into specific, publicly articulated demands. In an article in *The Conversation*, [Tamika Worrell documented the collective's position](https://theconversation.com/ai-affects-everyone-including-indigenous-people-its-time-we-have-a-say-in-how-its-built-239605): Indigenous communities must retain sovereignty over their own data "from creation to infrastructure"; Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples must have active participation in creating, maintaining, and evaluating AI systems from the beginning, not retrospective consultation after deployment; and the collective named specific AI harms requiring redress — AI image generators trained on Indigenous cultural materials without consent, AI-generated misinformation about Indigenous peoples, and AI-generated imagery deployed in political campaigns that harm Indigenous interests. These were framed as data sovereignty failures: AI systems that reproduce Indigenous identity and knowledge without Indigenous authority violate the same rights that administrative data exploitation has long violated. [Mirage News amplified these demands](https://www.miragenews.com/indigenous-voices-demand-say-in-ai-development-1334851/) the following day, describing the collective's call for governance authority over AI "from creation to infrastructure" as part of a broader international Indigenous AI rights advocacy moment. The collective's framing drew on "Indigenous Futurisms" — a vision of AI design reflecting Indigenous understandings of a living, interconnected world rather than reproducing extractive data logics.

## Global Indigenous Data Sovereignty Conference 2025 — binding standards demand (March–April 2025)

The [Global Indigenous Data Sovereignty Conference 2025](https://www.reconciliation.org.au/global-indigenous-data-sovereignty-conference-2025/) (GIDSov 2025), convened by Maiam nayri Wingara and Yardhura Walani — the National Centre for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Wellbeing Research at the Australian National University — on Ngunnawal and Ngambri Country from March 31 to April 3, 2025, was the largest international Indigenous data sovereignty gathering to that point. More than 250 Indigenous peoples from eleven or more countries attended, including Australia, Aotearoa New Zealand, Canada, Kenya, Mexico, Norway, Peru, the Philippines, Sweden, the United States, and Vanuatu. The GIDSov 2025 Communique produced the campaign's most specific AI governance demands to date: calling for binding standards, regulations, and laws to ensure AI does not misappropriate and misuse Indigenous knowledge; calling for Traditional Owners, young Indigenous leaders, government agencies, lawyers, academics, and technology companies to work together to develop AI systems that support Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples' goals and aspirations; and explicitly naming voluntary frameworks as insufficient given the pace of commercial AI deployment against Indigenous communities.

The conference represented an escalation in the campaign's ambition: from principled advocacy and government submissions to an international coalition demand for binding legal instruments. [Philanthropy Australia's October 2025 framing](https://www.philanthropy.org.au/news-and-stories/the-three-ai-governance-essentials-sector-leaders-need-to-know/) — naming Maiam nayri Wingara's CARE Principles as one of three essentials for responsible AI governance — showed that the collective's framework had penetrated mainstream civil-society conversations about AI governance, not merely Indigenous advocacy circles.

## International coalition dimension — GIDA AI protocols (February 2026)

At the [GIDA-convened IDSov and AI Workshop](https://www.gida-global.org/gidaidsovaiworkshop) held February 25–27, 2026 at COLPOS in Texcoco, Mexico, Maiam nayri Wingara joined [Te Mana Raraunga](../organizations/org-te-mana-raraunga.md), the [U.S. Indigenous Data Sovereignty Network](../organizations/org-us-indigenous-data-sovereignty-network.md), the [First Nations Information Governance Centre](../organizations/org-first-nations-information-governance-centre.md), the [Pacific Data Sovereignty Network](../organizations/org-pacific-data-sovereignty-network.md), GIDA-Inuit, and GIDA-Sápmi to develop Indigenous protocols for AI governance at the international level. The workshop extended the campaign's Australian domestic work into a coordinated international framework — developing specific operational protocols that can anchor Indigenous AI governance demands in multiple national contexts simultaneously.

## Corpus role

This campaign is the corpus's primary entry point for Indigenous AI data sovereignty advocacy in the Oceania region. It connects the broader global Indigenous data sovereignty architecture — [GIDA](../organizations/org-global-indigenous-data-alliance.md), [Te Mana Raraunga](../organizations/org-te-mana-raraunga.md), CARE Principles, UNDRIP — to Australia's specific AI governance processes: the AI Action Plan, the Universities Accord, the mandatory guardrails consultation, and the national legal landscape. Its arc — from foundational academic input (2019) through national summit advocacy (2023) to international binding-standards demands (2025) — reflects the escalating ambition of the Indigenous AI governance movement as voluntary frameworks have repeatedly proven insufficient to constrain commercial AI's use of Indigenous data without consent.
